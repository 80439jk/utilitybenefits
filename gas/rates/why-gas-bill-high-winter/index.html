Why is my gas bill so high in winter?

Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The answer is almost always one of three things: (1) dramatically higher heating use during cold-weather months (a typical cold-state household uses 3–5× more gas in January than August), (2) wholesale gas supply price increases that flow through to your bill (winter 2024 and 2025 saw 15–30% spikes), or (3) a billing or meter issue (less common but always check). The diagnostic test is to compare therms used — not just dollars — to the same month last year.

Diagnose first: compare therms, not dollars

Before you assume your utility is overcharging or that supply prices spiked, do this five-minute check:

  1. Pull this month's bill and the same month from last year (most utilities have a 12-month usage chart on the bill itself, or in your online account).
  2. Compare therms used (or CCF/MCF in some markets) — not the dollar amount.
  3. If therms are similar to last year but dollars are 20%+ higher → it's a supply price issue (Cause #2 below).
  4. If therms are dramatically higher than last year → it's a usage issue (Cause #1 below).
  5. If neither matches your normal pattern → check for a billing or meter issue (Cause #3 below).

Cause #1: Cold weather using more gas

Most common cause

You burned more gas than usual

Heating accounts for 50–80% of total annual gas usage in cold-weather households. The colder it gets outside, the more gas your furnace burns to maintain the same indoor temperature. Industry rule of thumb: every degree colder than normal increases heating use by 3–4%.

How to confirm: Therms used are 30%+ higher than the same month last year.

What's happening this winter (2025–2026): Most of the country saw a colder-than-normal December and January. Many households are seeing gas bills 25–40% higher than last winter purely from weather.

What to do:

  • Lower your thermostat 2–3°F. Each degree saves roughly 3% on heating costs over the season. Setting your thermostat to 68°F instead of 71°F saves 9–12%.
  • Programmable / smart thermostat. Setback during sleep hours and away periods saves 10–15% with no comfort sacrifice. Federal IRA rebates may apply.
  • Air sealing & insulation. The biggest single fix for older homes. Free through the Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) for income-qualified households. Otherwise $1,500–$5,000 with multi-year payback.
  • Furnace tune-up. A poorly maintained furnace runs at 70–80% of rated efficiency. $150 service often pays back within one season.
  • Block drafts on doors and windows. Cheap weather stripping and door sweeps can reduce heat loss 5–15% in older homes.

Cause #2: Supply prices went up

Common cause in 2024–2025

Wholesale gas got more expensive

Most household gas bills have two parts: a delivery charge (set by your local utility, regulated, relatively stable) and a supply charge (the actual cost of gas, which varies with wholesale market prices). When wholesale prices rise, the supply portion rises proportionally.

How to confirm: Therms used are similar to last year, but per-therm price is 15%+ higher.

What's happening this winter: U.S. natural gas spot prices (Henry Hub) ran 20–30% higher in winter 2024–2025 vs the previous year, driven by export demand, storage levels, and a colder-than-average winter. Most utilities pass these through directly to residential customers.

What to do:

  • If you're in a deregulated state (GA, OH, PA, NY, MD, DC): Switch suppliers. Fixed-rate plans through suppliers like Constellation, Direct Energy, Just Energy, NRG can lock in 12–24 months of price stability and typically save 5–15%.
  • In regulated states: You can't switch suppliers, but you can request a budget billing or "level pay" plan that averages your annual gas costs across 12 monthly payments — eliminating the winter shock without changing total cost.
  • For seasonal usage: Conservation tactics (Cause #1 fixes) are still your highest-leverage move regardless of supply price changes.

Cause #3: Billing or meter issue

Less common but always check

Something is wrong with the bill itself

Less frequent than the first two, but specific scenarios where the bill itself is wrong:

  • Estimated reads. If your utility couldn't get an actual meter reading (technician access, weather), they estimate based on history. The next actual read can produce a "true-up" bill that looks shockingly large but is just catching up to actual usage.
  • Meter malfunction. Rare but possible. Sustained dramatic increases (50%+) with no usage changes warrants a meter test request — utilities test for free or low cost.
  • Account billing change. Promotional rates ending, rate class reclassification, or new fees added by regulator approval.
  • Wrong meter on bill. Rare but happens — your bill reads someone else's meter. Compare meter number on bill to physical meter.
How to confirm: Usage and price both look similar to history, but bill amount is anomalous. Or you see "estimated" instead of "actual" on past bills.

What to do:

  • Call utility billing. Ask for a detailed line-item explanation of the bill change. Most utilities will adjust if there's an estimating error.
  • Request a meter test. Utilities are required to test on customer request. Cost is typically waived if the meter is found defective.
  • File a PUC complaint if the utility refuses to investigate. State Public Utility Commissions have formal complaint processes that move utilities quickly.

What to do if you genuinely can't pay

Facing a winter shutoff or empty fuel tank?

Most cold-weather states have winter shutoff moratoriums that protect you — but you have to actively engage. A specialist can engage LIHEAP Crisis, your utility's hardship program, AND invoke shutoff protections in parallel.

If your gas bill is genuinely beyond what you can pay this month, several layers of help exist:

  • LIHEAP — federal program covering $400–$1,500/yr per qualifying household. Full LIHEAP guide.
  • State-specific gas programs — Ohio PIPP Plus caps gas bill at 6% of income; NJ USF; PA CAP rates; MA Fuel Assistance. Browse all gas assistance.
  • Utility hardship programs — Atmos Sharing the Warmth, CenterPoint Care, Spire DollarHelp, Columbia Gas HeartShare, National Grid CARE. Usually faster than LIHEAP.
  • Winter shutoff moratoriums — most cold-weather states protect you from disconnection November–April. State-by-state guide.
  • Payment plans — every utility offers these. The earlier you call, the better the terms.

Longer-term: structural fixes

If your home consistently has high winter gas bills year over year, the underlying issue is usually one of three things:

  • Insulation deficient. Most pre-1980 U.S. homes have poor wall insulation and inadequate attic insulation by current standards. WAP (Weatherization Assistance Program) addresses this for low-income households at no cost; everyone else pays $3,000–$8,000 with 5–10 year payback.
  • Old heating system. Furnaces older than 15 years often run at 70–80% efficiency vs 95%+ for new high-efficiency models. Federal IRA tax credit covers up to $2,000 for replacement.
  • Heat pump conversion candidate. In mild and moderate climates with reasonable electricity prices, switching from gas heating to a high-efficiency heat pump can lower annual heating costs 30–50%. Federal IRA rebates up to $8,000 plus $2,000 tax credit. The math depends heavily on your climate and rates.

Frequently asked questions

How much should a normal winter gas bill be?

Wildly variable. A 1,500 sqft well-insulated home in moderate winter (like Atlanta) might run $80–$120 in January. The same home in Minneapolis or Boston might run $200–$350. A poorly insulated 2,500 sqft home in Buffalo or Burlington can hit $400–$600. Compare to your own previous winters as the most reliable baseline.

Why was my January bill so much worse than December?

January is usually the coldest month statistically, AND many utilities bill 30+ days behind, so January's bill often covers a longer cold-weather period than December's. The combination produces the typical "January spike" pattern.

Can my utility raise prices without warning?

The supply portion (commodity gas cost) flows through automatically with limited notice — wholesale prices change weekly. The delivery portion (regulated charges) requires PUC approval and gets advance public notice. You won't generally see surprises on delivery charges; supply portion can swing significantly month-to-month.

Should I get budget billing?

Yes for most households — it averages your annual gas costs into 12 equal monthly payments, eliminating the winter shock. Doesn't reduce total cost, just smooths it. Some utilities reconcile annually; others quarterly. Watch for "true-up" bills if your usage is significantly higher than the budget assumed.

Is it cheaper to set my thermostat lower and use space heaters?

Almost never. Electric resistance space heaters convert 1 unit of electricity to 1 unit of heat. Gas heating is much cheaper per unit of heat in most U.S. markets. Lowering your thermostat 2–3°F is the right move; replacing whole-house gas heat with space heaters typically increases your total bill significantly.

What's a "fuel adjustment charge"?

Some utilities show the supply-price flow-through as a separate line item called "fuel adjustment," "purchased gas adjustment," or "PGA." It reflects the difference between the utility's projected supply cost and actual wholesale price. When wholesale gas prices spike, this line item grows.

How fast does insulation pay back?

Attic insulation upgrades typically pay back in 3–7 years through reduced heating and cooling. Wall insulation in older homes (where it's worth doing) is 5–10 years. Air sealing is fastest — often 1–3 year payback. WAP makes these free for income-qualified households.

What if I'm renting — can I do anything?

Yes. Tenant-paid efficiency improvements (smart thermostats, weather stripping, draft blockers) work whether you own or rent. WAP can sometimes work in rental units with landlord cooperation. And LIHEAP covers renters who pay their own gas bill. The structural fixes (insulation, furnace replacement) require landlord buy-in.
