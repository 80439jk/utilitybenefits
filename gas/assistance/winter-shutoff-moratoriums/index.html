Winter Shutoff Moratoriums by State

Most cold-weather states prohibit residential utility shutoffs during heating season — but rules vary widely. Here's the state-by-state breakdown for 2025–2026, and what you have to do to actually invoke the protection.

Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Most cold-weather U.S. states prohibit utility companies from disconnecting residential heating service during winter months — typically November through April. Rules vary by state: some protect all residential customers automatically, others only protect income-qualified customers, and some require active enrollment in a payment plan. Texas, Florida, and other warm-weather states have no winter moratorium because cold weather isn't life-threatening. Hot-weather states sometimes have summer cooling moratoriums instead. The most important rule across all states: you have to communicate with your utility — moratoriums protect against involuntary disconnection, not against bills accruing.

How winter moratoriums work

A winter shutoff moratorium is a state law or Public Utility Commission rule that prohibits utilities (gas, electric, sometimes water) from disconnecting service for non-payment during designated cold-weather months. They exist because hypothermia and cold-related illness from no-heat situations are public health emergencies, especially for elderly, disabled, and very young household members.

Three structural patterns:

  • Automatic / blanket protection — applies to all residential customers regardless of income. Examples: Massachusetts (with restrictions), Minnesota.
  • Income-qualified protection — applies only to households below a state-defined income threshold (often the LIHEAP threshold). Examples: New York, Pennsylvania, Wisconsin.
  • Conditional protection — applies only if the customer has actively enrolled in a payment plan or notified the utility. Examples: Illinois, Ohio.

Weather-triggered moratoriums

Several states (notably Missouri, Kansas, Virginia, Colorado) use temperature triggers rather than fixed date ranges. Disconnections are prohibited when forecast temperatures drop below a threshold (typically 32°F) for a defined number of days. These can apply outside the typical Nov–April window if weather warrants.

What moratoriums DON'T do

  • They don't forgive your bill. The bill keeps accruing. When the moratorium ends, you owe everything that accumulated. Without LIHEAP or a payment plan to address the arrearage, you may face shutoff in May or June.
  • They don't protect you if you don't communicate. Most states require you to actively engage your utility — call, request hardship status, enter a payment plan. Silent customers can still be disconnected in some states.
  • They don't apply to all utilities equally. Most cover gas and electric heating. Water service is sometimes separate. Internet and phone are not typically covered.
  • They don't apply to commercial accounts. Residential service only.

What to do if you're at risk of winter shutoff

  1. Contact your utility immediately. Don't wait. Tell them you're at financial risk and ask about cold weather protections, payment plans, and hardship programs. Most utilities have dedicated lines for this.
  2. Apply for LIHEAP Crisis. Most states process within 18–48 hours when truly urgent. Application is through your local Community Action Agency or state energy assistance office.
  3. Apply for state-level programs. PIPP Plus in Ohio, USF in NJ, CARE in CA, etc. Some of these meet the requirement for moratorium protection by themselves.
  4. Apply for utility-specific hardship. Atmos Sharing the Warmth, CenterPoint Care, Spire DollarHelp, National Grid CARE, Columbia Gas WarmChoice, etc.
  5. Document everything. Keep records of every call, every application, every promised payment plan. If a utility wrongfully disconnects you, this paper trail is critical for PUC complaints.
  6. If wrongfully disconnected, file a PUC complaint immediately. State Public Utility Commissions move quickly on cold weather rule violations. Disconnections in violation of moratorium are typically reversed within 24–48 hours.

Summer cooling moratoriums (warm-weather states)

Hot-weather states sometimes have summer cooling protections rather than winter heating moratoriums:

  • Arizona — APS, TEP cannot disconnect when forecast temperatures are extreme (typically ≥95°F).
  • Texas — Public Utility Commission of Texas requires utilities to offer payment plans during summer for vulnerable customers; LIHEAP Crisis processes cooling shutoffs as emergencies.
  • Nevada — Cooling protections during extreme heat advisories.
  • Florida — No formal moratorium, but utilities operate hardship programs year-round and LIHEAP-FL prioritizes cooling crises.

Frequently asked questions

Will my utility automatically apply the moratorium?

It depends on your state. In some (Massachusetts, Wisconsin, DC), protection is largely automatic during the date range. In most others, you must actively engage the utility — call them, enroll in a payment plan, or apply for LIHEAP. The default assumption should be that you need to act, not that you're automatically protected.

Can my heat be turned off in November in a non-moratorium state?

Yes, in states like Texas, Florida, Arizona, and others without formal cold-weather rules. Utilities in non-moratorium states can disconnect for non-payment year-round. LIHEAP and utility hardship programs are still available, but there's no statutory protection against shutoff itself.

What if my utility shuts me off illegally during the moratorium period?

File a complaint with your state Public Utility Commission immediately. PUC complaints about cold-weather rule violations move very quickly — typically within 24–48 hours. Most states require utilities to immediately reconnect with no fee if a violation is confirmed.

Does the moratorium cover bottled propane delivery refusals?

Generally no. Moratoriums typically cover utility-delivered gas and electric service. Bottled propane delivery is a vendor relationship; you can't be "shut off," but a vendor can refuse to deliver if you have an outstanding balance. LIHEAP for propane households can address this — most states pay vendors directly.

What if I'm in a non-moratorium state and can't pay?

Apply for LIHEAP Crisis (year-round in most states), engage your utility's hardship program, and call 211 for local emergency funds. Salvation Army and Dollar Energy Fund (in some states) provide gap funding. Texas, Florida, and other warm-weather states all have LIHEAP and utility hardship — just no winter shutoff statute.

Do moratoriums apply to medical equipment customers year-round?

Many states have separate, year-round medical equipment customer protections — life-support oxygen concentrators, ventilators, dialysis machines, etc. These usually require physician certification on file with your utility. Verify with your utility immediately if anyone in your household uses life-sustaining medical equipment.
