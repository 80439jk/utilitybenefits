How to Save on Your Natural Gas Bill: Complete Guide

Gas is the most seasonal utility — winter bills can run 3–5× summer bills in cold-weather states. Two paths help: switching providers (in 6 deregulated states), and qualifying for LIHEAP plus utility hardship programs. Plus the longer-term play: heat pump conversion.

SKBy Sara Khan, Senior Editor · Reviewed by Marcus Reid, Energy Policy Analyst · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The average U.S. household pays about $120/month for natural gas in winter and as little as $25/month in summer — a 3–5× swing concentrated in heating season. In the 6 deregulated gas states (Georgia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Maryland, DC), switching gas suppliers can save 5–15%. In the 44 regulated states, your only commercial path is efficiency (insulation, smart thermostats, heat pump conversion). On the assistance side, federal LIHEAP covers gas, oil, and propane bills for households at or below 150% FPL — typical benefit $400–$1,500 per heating season. Most cold-weather states also have winter shutoff moratoriums protecting customers from disconnection between roughly November and April.

The two paths to a lower gas bill

Most households can do two things to lower gas bills meaningfully: reduce usage (insulation, smart thermostats, lower thermostat setpoints, in some cases switching to heat pump heating) and qualify for assistance (LIHEAP, your state's utility-specific programs, utility hardship funds, and — in 6 states — switching gas suppliers).

The third path — switching suppliers — only works in deregulated gas states. As of 2026, that's Georgia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Maryland, and DC. Outside these states, your local gas utility (Atmos, CenterPoint, Spire, Southern Company Gas, National Grid, Con Ed, PG&E, SoCalGas, Peoples Gas, Nicor, Columbia Gas) is your only option for gas supply. Commercial savings are thinner than in deregulated electricity markets even where gas is deregulated.

The winter bill problem

Gas heating is responsible for 50–80% of total annual gas usage in cold-weather households. A January gas bill in Boston, Chicago, Minneapolis, Detroit, or Pittsburgh is typically 3–5× the August bill — sometimes more. Three things shape how badly winter hits:

  • Outdoor temperature. A degree colder = roughly 3–4% more heating use across the season. Polar vortex weeks in January 2024 and 2025 produced bills 50%+ higher than the same household saw the previous January.
  • Insulation and air sealing. A poorly sealed older home loses 30–50% of heating energy through leaks. The Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) addresses this for qualifying low-income households at no cost.
  • Heating system efficiency. A 70% AFUE older furnace burns more gas to produce the same heat as a new 95%+ AFUE model. The federal IRA tax credit covers up to $2,000 for high-efficiency furnace replacement.

The strategic page on this — "Why is my gas bill so high in winter?" — walks through diagnosis and the practical things you can actually do this season.

Deregulated gas states

In 6 states, you can shop for the gas supplier separately from the local utility (which still delivers it):

  • Georgia — most competitive deregulated gas market. Atlanta Gas Light is the local utility; suppliers include Constellation, Gas South, SCANA Energy, others.
  • Ohio — Columbia Gas of Ohio, Duke Energy Ohio, Dominion Energy Ohio service areas. Energy Choice Ohio site lists active suppliers.
  • Pennsylvania — competitive in PECO, PGW, and Columbia Gas service territories.
  • New York — National Grid, Con Edison, NYSEG, Central Hudson all have supply choice.
  • Maryland — BGE, Washington Gas, Columbia Gas areas.
  • District of Columbia — Washington Gas as the local utility, choice of suppliers.

Switching typically saves 5–15% on the supply portion of your bill. Watch for variable-rate plans that look cheaper initially but reset higher (similar to deregulated electricity dynamics).

Gas assistance landscape

Federal LIHEAP covers natural gas, propane, and heating oil — same income limits (150% FPL or 60% SMI), but benefits are often higher for oil and propane households because per-unit fuel costs are higher. Layered on top of LIHEAP, every cold-weather state has its own gas-specific assistance:

Winter shutoff moratoriums

Most cold-weather states prohibit utility shutoffs during winter months for residential customers — but rules vary widely. Some states (Massachusetts, Minnesota) protect virtually all customers November through April. Others (Texas, Florida) have no winter moratorium because cold weather isn't life-threatening. The full state-by-state breakdown is on a dedicated page: Winter shutoff moratoriums by state.

The heat pump conversion question

For households considering a major heating system upgrade, the federal IRA tax credit (Inflation Reduction Act) and state-level rebates have meaningfully changed the math. A high-efficiency air-source heat pump can replace gas heating in most U.S. climates, with up to $8,000 in federal rebates plus $2,000 in tax credits. The economics work best in mild and moderate climates and where electricity is relatively cheap.

Heat pump conversion is not always the right answer — in very cold climates with high electricity prices, a high-efficiency gas furnace may still cost less to operate. Run the numbers for your specific climate zone and utility rates before deciding.

Heating oil and propane

If you heat with oil or propane instead of natural gas, most of the same dynamics apply but with sharper price volatility. Heating oil prices follow crude oil; propane is regional. Both qualify for LIHEAP — and benefits are often higher per unit than gas/electric LIHEAP because the cost per BTU is higher.

Frequently asked questions

Why did my January gas bill double?

Three usual suspects: cold-weather usage (a -10°F snap can double daily usage vs +30°F), supply price increases (gas spot prices spiked in winter 2024 and 2025), or a billing/meter issue. Compare your therms used (not just dollars) to the same month last year to isolate which. The full diagnostic walkthrough is on our winter gas bill page.

Can I switch gas suppliers in my state?

Only in 6 deregulated states: Georgia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Maryland, and DC. In all other states, your local utility is your only option for gas supply (though you may have efficiency rebates and assistance programs available regardless).

Does LIHEAP cover natural gas?

Yes. LIHEAP is fuel-agnostic — it covers natural gas, propane, heating oil, and electric heat. Apply through your state's energy assistance office; benefit usually paid directly to your gas utility.

What's a winter shutoff moratorium?

A state law or PUC rule that prohibits utilities from disconnecting residential customers for non-payment during cold-weather months. Rules vary — some states cover all residential customers, some only income-qualified, some require you to be on a payment plan. See our state-by-state guide.

Should I switch to a heat pump?

Depends on your climate, your current heating system's age, and your local electricity vs gas prices. Heat pumps are generally cheaper to operate in mild and moderate climates and where electricity is relatively cheap. Federal IRA rebates and tax credits significantly improve the upfront economics ($8K rebates + $2K tax credit for many households).
