How to Switch Natural Gas Suppliers

Six states allow gas-supplier shopping. Here's how it works, what savings to realistically expect, and what to avoid.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Energy & utilities research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Six states allow consumers to shop for natural gas suppliers: Georgia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Maryland, and Washington DC. Switching takes 10 minutes online — your local utility (Atlanta Gas Light, Columbia Gas, PECO, Con Ed, BGE, Washington Gas, etc.) keeps delivering and billing; only the supply line changes. Typical savings: 5–15% on the supply portion of your bill, which is roughly half the total. For most households that's $50–$200/year — meaningful but smaller than electric supplier shopping or efficiency upgrades.

The 6-step gas switch

  1. Confirm you can shop. Only GA, OH, PA, NY, MD, and DC allow residential gas-supplier choice. If you're elsewhere, your gas utility is your only option — focus on efficiency, weatherization, and assistance.
  2. Pull a recent gas bill. You'll need: your account number, your local utility name (Atlanta Gas Light, PECO, etc.), your service address, and your last 12 months of usage in therms or CCF.
  3. Find your state's apples-to-apples site. See the table below. State PUC sites are unbiased and free.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month plans. Skip variable-rate, month-to-month, and "introductory" plans. Same trap as electricity — variable rates are usually a customer-retention penalty.
  5. Compare on cost-per-therm at your usage tier. Most plans price per-therm; some include a fixed monthly customer charge that affects low-usage households more.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract expiration so you can shop again before the auto-rollover.

Georgia is special: there is no default supply

Georgia's gas market is unique. Unlike the other 5 deregulated states (where you can default to your utility's standard service), Georgia requires every household to choose a supplier. Atlanta Gas Light only delivers — they don't sell gas. If you don't pick a supplier, you're auto-assigned by the PSC to a "Provider of Last Resort" at typically the highest available rate.

For Georgia specifically, the most active suppliers are:

  • Gas South — typically competitive on 12-month fixed
  • Constellation — large national supplier, broad plan options
  • SCANA Energy — historically dominant in GA
  • Walton EMC Natural Gas — co-op-affiliated, often competitive
  • Infinite Energy
  • Georgia Natural Gas

Compare these specifically on GA PSC's price disclosure site. Georgia residents cannot stay on default — you must pick.

Watch-fors specific to gas

  • Variable-rate roll-offs. Same problem as electric — fixed plans roll to punitive variable rates at expiration. Set a calendar reminder.
  • Customer-charge gotchas. Some plans look low-rate but bury a $9.99/month customer charge. Low-usage households pay more on these plans than on plans with no customer charge.
  • Door-to-door enrollment. NY, IL, and PA tightened gas door-to-door rules in 2023–2024. If pitched at your door, decline and verify on the PUC site.
  • Termination of CAP enrollment when switching. If you're on a utility CAP rate (PA, MD), switching to a competitive gas supplier may end your CAP eligibility. Check before switching.
  • Bundled gas + electric pitches. Often higher per-unit rates than shopping each separately. Compare individually.

FAQ

Will switching gas suppliers interrupt my service?

No. Same pipes, same meter, same utility. Only the supply line on your bill changes. Transition happens at your next meter read.

What's the difference between supply and delivery on a gas bill?

Supply is the natural gas itself (sold by suppliers). Delivery is the pipes that bring it to you (run by your local utility). In deregulated states, you can shop the supply portion; the delivery portion stays with your utility.

How do I read therms vs CCF on my gas bill?

Therms is the energy unit (100,000 BTU per therm). CCF is the volume unit (100 cubic feet). One CCF is approximately 1.025 therms — they're nearly equivalent. Most plans price per-therm; some price per-CCF. Compare apples-to-apples on the PUC site.

Can I shop gas if I'm with PGW (Philadelphia Gas Works)?

PGW is municipally-owned and operates differently from the rest of PA's deregulated gas market. PGW customers have limited shopping options. Check PA PUC for current PGW choice availability.

What if I'm in a deregulated state but on a CAP rate?

Don't shop. CAP rates (PECO, PGW, Washington Gas, BGE for income-qualified) are typically the lowest available rates and apply to default supply. Switching to a competitive supplier ends CAP eligibility. Gas assistance →

Are there \"green\" natural gas plans?

Some suppliers offer carbon-offset gas plans where the supplier purchases offsets equivalent to the gas you use. The offsets are typically forestry-based and have variable additionality. The premium is usually $0.05–$0.10/therm. Most independent reviewers consider these largely greenwashing.

Can I switch back to default supply?

Yes in OH, PA, NY, MD, DC. Default supply is always available. In Georgia, there is no default — you must always have a chosen supplier.
