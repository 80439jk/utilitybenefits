Heat Pumps vs. Gas Furnaces: 2026 Decision Guide

For most U.S. households, replacing a gas furnace with an electric heat pump is now the highest-ROI energy move available. The math, the climate considerations, and the federal stacking opportunities.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Energy & utilities research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Modern heat pumps operate at 250–400% efficiency vs. gas furnaces at 80–95%. In moderate climates, heat pumps cut total heating costs by 30–50% even after accounting for higher electricity prices. In cold climates (below 0°F regularly), modern cold-climate heat pumps still work but a hybrid system (heat pump + small gas backup) is often the right answer. Federal IRA stacking covers $14,000+ of installation costs for income-qualified households via 25C tax credit + HEEHRA rebate + state energy office rebates + utility rebates. For most households this is now the single biggest energy-savings move available.

How heat pumps work (the brief version)

A heat pump is just a reversible air conditioner. In summer, it pulls heat out of your house. In winter, it pulls heat into your house from the outside air. The "outside air is cold" objection is a misunderstanding — even -10°F air contains plenty of heat energy from a thermodynamic standpoint, and modern heat pumps efficiently extract it.

The efficiency math:

  • Resistance heater (electric baseboard, electric furnace): 100% efficient. 1 kWh in, 1 kWh of heat out.
  • Modern gas furnace: 90–98% AFUE. Roughly 95% of the gas energy becomes useful heat.
  • Modern heat pump: 250–400% COP. 1 kWh in, 2.5–4 kWh of heat out (because most of the energy comes from the outside air, not the electricity).
  • Modern cold-climate heat pump at -15°F: 200–250% COP (still beats resistance heating by 2–2.5×).

Operating cost math

The real question is total annual heating cost. This depends on climate (heating degree days), local energy prices, and system efficiency. Rough 2026 numbers for a 2,000 sq ft home with 70 million BTU/year of heating demand:

The pattern: heat pumps win in moderate climates by a wide margin (Atlanta, Charlotte, Dallas, most of CA). Heat pumps win modestly in cold climates (Boston, Chicago, Detroit) — by maybe $200–$400/year. Hybrid systems win in extreme cold (Minneapolis, Burlington VT, Fargo) — heat pump runs most of the year, small gas backup handles the polar-vortex weeks.

Federal IRA stacking math

The 2022 Inflation Reduction Act created the most generous heat pump incentive program in U.S. history. The available stack:

For an income-qualified household installing a $16,000 cold-climate heat pump system:

  • HEEHRA rebate: $8,000
  • State energy office: $2,000
  • Utility rebate: $1,500
  • Federal 25C tax credit: $2,000
  • Net cost: $2,500 (84% covered)

For a non-income-qualified household installing the same system:

  • State energy office: $2,000
  • Utility rebate: $1,500
  • Federal 25C tax credit: $2,000
  • Net cost: $10,500 (34% covered)

HEEHRA and state programs roll out at different paces. CA, NY, MA, CO, MN, GA, ME launched first. Most others launching through late 2025 and into 2026. Check your state energy office website for current availability.

When a gas furnace still makes sense

  • Your existing furnace is recent (under 10 years old) and working well. Replacing a young furnace doesn't make economic sense. Wait until end of life, then evaluate heat pump vs new furnace.
  • You live in extreme cold (regularly below -15°F) and don't want a hybrid system. Even modern cold-climate heat pumps lose efficiency at extreme cold; a hybrid is usually better but adds complexity.
  • Your electric service is undersized. Many older homes have 100A panels. A heat pump conversion may require a $2,000–$4,000 panel upgrade. HEEHRA covers this for income-qualified households; otherwise it's an additional cost.
  • You're moving in <5 years. Payback periods are 5–12 years depending on stacking. If you won't be there to recoup, the math weakens.
  • You have free fuel. Some farms and rural homes have free or very-cheap natural gas, propane, or wood. Heat pumps still beat resistance heat but lose to free fuel.

When a heat pump is the obvious answer

  • Your existing furnace is 15+ years old. End of life is replacement time anyway — heat pump is now the obvious choice in most climates.
  • You live in a moderate climate. Most of CA, the Southeast (excluding TX deep south), the mid-Atlantic, the Pacific Northwest — heat pumps dominate the operating cost math.
  • You have an existing AC unit that's also old. A heat pump replaces both AC and furnace with one system. Combined replacement is cheaper than separate units.
  • You're income-qualified for HEEHRA. 84% covered makes the math compelling regardless of climate.
  • You care about climate impact. Heat pumps have ~50% lower emissions than gas heating in most U.S. grids, declining further as the grid decarbonizes.

Cold-climate heat pump models worth knowing

Not all heat pumps perform the same in cold weather. The leaders for cold-climate performance (rated for -15°F or colder):

  • Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat / H2i — long track record, available in ducted and ductless; widely installed in MA, ME, MN.
  • LG XCool / Multi V — strong cold-climate performance; aggressive pricing.
  • Carrier Greenspeed Infinity — wider U.S. distribution; ducted-focus.
  • Bosch IDS — mid-priced with good cold-climate ratings.
  • Trane XV / XL Series — wide distribution; somewhat behind Mitsubishi/LG on extreme cold.

Talk to a local installer about which models they support — installer experience matters more than spec-sheet differences. NEEP (Northeast Energy Efficiency Partnerships) maintains a public list of cold-climate heat pump models.

FAQ

Can a heat pump really keep up in below-zero weather?

Modern cold-climate heat pumps (NEEP-listed models) operate efficiently down to -15°F to -20°F. Below that, efficiency drops but they still work. For climates with regular extended periods below -15°F, a hybrid system (heat pump + small gas furnace backup) is the typical solution. Maine, Vermont, and Minnesota all have growing heat-pump installation bases — they work in cold weather.

Will my electric bill go up if I switch to a heat pump?

Yes, your electric bill goes up — but your gas bill goes to near-zero (just water heating and cooking, if those are still gas). Total energy bill typically drops 30–50% in moderate climates, 10–25% in cold climates. Run the numbers for your specific situation before deciding.

What about water heating?

Heat pump water heaters exist and are now mainstream — 250–300% efficiency vs. gas water heaters at 60–80%. HEEHRA covers up to $1,750 for HP water heaters; federal 25C covers $600. Stack with utility rebates. If you're converting heating, do the water heater at the same time.

How long does heat pump installation take?

Typical retrofit: 2–4 days of work, plus possibly an electrical panel upgrade (1 day). Permits and contractor scheduling can stretch the calendar to 4–8 weeks from quote to completion.

What's a hybrid system?

A heat pump paired with a smaller gas furnace as backup. Heat pump handles 80–95% of the heating year (above ~20°F outdoor temp); gas furnace kicks in only during extreme cold. Captures most of the heat pump efficiency advantage while keeping gas as a safety net for polar-vortex weeks. Common in cold-climate retrofits.

Can renters do this?

Generally no — heat pump installation requires homeowner authorization. Renters can ask landlords, especially when an existing furnace fails. Some states have landlord-targeted incentive programs to encourage HP installations in rental properties.

What if I'm in an apartment?

Mini-split (ductless) heat pumps are the typical apartment solution. One outdoor unit feeds 1–4 indoor units, no ductwork needed. Easier to install in older buildings. Owners and condo boards can install; renters need landlord cooperation.
