Lower Your Natural Gas Bill: The 2026 Playbook

Five paths to a lower gas bill — from shopping suppliers in the 6 deregulated states to heat pump conversion to assistance programs.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

For most U.S. households, gas bills are 50–80% heating. The biggest savings come from weatherization (sealing air leaks, adding insulation — often $300–$600/year), furnace efficiency upgrades (federal IRA covers up to $2,000), and heat pump conversion (often eliminates gas heating entirely — federal stack often $10K+). In the 6 deregulated gas states (GA, OH, PA, NY, MD, DC), shopping suppliers can save another 5–15%. Income-qualified households often get $500–$1,500/year from federal LIHEAP and state utility CAP programs.

How gas bill savings actually work

Your gas bill has two parts: supply (the natural gas itself, sold by suppliers) and delivery (the pipes that bring it to you, run by your local utility). In regulated states (most of the U.S.), your utility owns both. In deregulated states (GA, OH, PA, NY, MD, DC), the supply portion is competitive — you can shop for a supplier while your utility continues delivering and billing.

The savings math is different from electricity:

  • Supply savings are smaller in gas than electricity. Gas wholesale markets are tighter and retail margins are thinner. Typical shopping savings: 5–15% of supply portion (which is half your bill at most).
  • Heating efficiency is the bigger lever. Most U.S. households spend 50–80% of their gas budget on heating. A 95%+ AFUE furnace burns 25% less gas than a 70% AFUE older unit. Weatherization typically cuts heating use 15–25%.
  • Heat pumps change the math entirely. An electric heat pump replaces gas heating with electric heating — but at 3–4× the efficiency of a resistance heater. In moderate climates (most of the U.S. outside the polar plains), heat pumps cost 30–50% less to run than even efficient gas furnaces.

Weatherization (the highest-ROI gas savings move)

For most households, the single biggest gas savings opportunity is air sealing and insulation. The numbers:

  • Average home loses 25–40% of heating energy to air leaks through windows, doors, attic hatches, electrical outlets, and recessed lighting. Sealing these leaks typically costs $300–$1,500 and saves $200–$500/year.
  • Inadequate attic insulation is the second-biggest leak. Most pre-2000 homes have R-19 to R-30 attic insulation when modern code calls for R-49 to R-60. Adding insulation typically costs $1,000–$3,000 and saves $300–$600/year.
  • Duct leaks waste 20–30% of conditioned air. Sealing ducts typically costs $500–$2,000 and saves $200–$400/year.

Two paths to pay for this:

  1. If income-qualified (typically 200% FPL): Free via the federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP). Apply through your state energy office or LIHEAP intake. Typical WAP project: $5,000–$10,000 of work, $0 cost to household. Apply →
  2. If not income-qualified: Federal IRA Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (25C) covers $1,200/year of insulation, sealing, and audit costs. Most utilities also offer rebates of $500–$2,000 for major weatherization work. Stack everything.

Furnace efficiency upgrade

Replacing an old (70–80% AFUE) gas furnace with a high-efficiency (95%+ AFUE) model saves 15–25% on gas heating costs. For a household with a $2,000/year gas bill, that's $300–$500/year.

2026 economics:

  • New 95% AFUE gas furnace: $4,000–$8,000 installed
  • Federal 25C tax credit: up to $600 (for qualifying high-efficiency models)
  • Utility rebates: $200–$1,500 typical
  • Net cost: $2,500–$6,500
  • Payback: 8–15 years

If your existing furnace is 15+ years old and approaching end of life, the upgrade math is straightforward — replace at end of life with a high-efficiency model. If your furnace is younger, the case is weaker — heat pump conversion is often a better next step.

Heat pump conversion (the big move)

Heat pumps move heat rather than generate it — operating at 250–400% efficiency vs. resistance heating's 100% or gas furnaces' 80–95%. In moderate climates, a heat pump cuts total heating energy use 50–70% compared to gas heating, even after accounting for the higher per-unit cost of electricity vs gas.

Assistance programs

Income-qualified households (typically at or below 200% federal poverty level) can stack:

  • Federal LIHEAP covers heating fuel costs (gas, oil, propane, electric, wood) — typical $300–$1,500/year. Gas assistance hub →
  • State-specific gas programs — CARE for SoCalGas customers, USF for NJ gas customers, gas CAP rates from PECO/PGW/Columbia in PA, EUSP for some MD households.
  • Crisis funds for shutoff threats and tank-fuel emergencies.
  • Winter shutoff moratoriums in cold-weather states protect against disconnection during heating season. State-by-state moratoriums →

FAQ

Why are gas savings smaller than electricity savings?

Gas wholesale markets are tighter than electric markets — fewer suppliers, smaller margins. Retail competitive margins on gas supply are typically 5–15%, vs. 15–30% on electricity. The bigger gas savings opportunities are efficiency and heat pump conversion, not supplier shopping.

Should I get a heat pump if I'm in a very cold climate?

Modern cold-climate heat pumps perform efficiently down to about -15°F, which covers most of the U.S. except the polar plains (ND, MN northern, etc.). Where outdoor temps drop below that, a hybrid system (heat pump + small gas furnace backup) handles the extreme days. Talk to a contractor familiar with cold-climate models — Mitsubishi Hyper-Heat, LG XCool, Carrier Greenspeed are leading options.

Do gas suppliers offer fixed-rate plans like electricity?

Yes in deregulated states. 12-month and 24-month fixed-rate gas plans work the same way — predictable per-therm pricing for the contract term. Variable-rate gas plans have the same problems as variable electricity plans (predatory pricing). Stick to fixed.

What if I rent?

If your gas account is in your name, you can shop in deregulated states. Heat pump conversion requires landlord involvement. Weatherization is often available via WAP for income-qualified renters with landlord cooperation.

How much does weatherization actually save?

Typical 15–25% reduction in heating costs for an under-insulated, leaky home. For a $2,000/year gas heating bill, that's $300–$500/year. WAP-funded projects often produce larger savings because they take a comprehensive approach.

How do I know if my furnace is old enough to replace?

Furnaces typically last 15–20 years. Check the manufacturer label inside the unit. If it's 15+ years old and you're seeing rising bills, frequent service calls, or yellow flame (instead of blue) on natural gas, it's time. Replace at end of life with a 95%+ AFUE model — or jump to a heat pump.
