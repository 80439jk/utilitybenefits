What replaced ACP? The honest answer.

Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Nothing federal replaced the Affordable Connectivity Program. ACP ended on June 1, 2024 when funding ran out and Congress did not reauthorize it. The closest still-active federal program is Lifeline, which pays $9.25/month (vs ACP's $30/month). ISP-specific low-cost programs — Comcast Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Internet Assist — fill some of the gap at the carrier level. A handful of states (NY, CA, MA) have stood up partial replacements. If you lost ACP, you most likely qualify for Lifeline plus an ISP-specific program; together they typically replace $15–$30 of the $30 ACP benefit.

What ACP was, and why its ending matters

The Affordable Connectivity Program was a federal benefit that paid up to $30/month — or $75/month on tribal lands — directly to internet service providers on behalf of qualifying low-income households. Created in late 2021 as a successor to the pandemic-era Emergency Broadband Benefit, ACP at its peak served 23 million households, making it the largest federal broadband subsidy in U.S. history.

ACP funding ran out in mid-2024. Congress considered several reauthorization bills — the most prominent was the bipartisan Affordable Connectivity Program Extension Act introduced in early 2024 — but none passed. ACP-enrolled households received a final partial benefit in May 2024 and zero benefit thereafter. The program officially wound down on June 1, 2024.

The short timeline

May 2021
Emergency Broadband Benefit (EBB) launches

Pandemic-era federal benefit pays up to $50/mo for home internet. Roughly 9 million households enroll.

December 2021
ACP replaces EBB

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act creates ACP at $30/mo, $75/mo on tribal lands. Funded with $14.2 billion.

2022–2023
Enrollment grows to ~23 million households

Becomes the largest federal broadband program ever. Funding starts to run out in late 2023 as enrollment grows faster than projected.

February 2024
FCC announces wind-down

Last full-benefit month is April 2024. New enrollments freeze.

May 2024
Final partial benefit

Households receive a partial ACP benefit (typically ~$14, prorated) for May.

June 1, 2024
ACP officially ends

No further federal benefit paid. Households revert to ISP retail pricing or apply to alternatives.

What's actually available now (the alternatives)

Three categories of alternatives have partially filled the gap. None at ACP's scale or benefit level. In rough order of how many households can use them:

1. Lifeline (federal)

Lifeline is the FCC's pre-existing federal program that pays $9.25/month toward home internet OR phone (not both per household). On tribal lands, the benefit is $34.25/month — the only federal program where the post-ACP benefit comes close to ACP-era levels. Lifeline existed before ACP and continues after it.

Eligibility is income at or below 135% of the federal poverty level (vs 200% for ACP) or participation in SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, Veterans Pension, or Federal Public Housing. Most ACP-eligible households also qualify for Lifeline.

2. ISP-specific low-cost programs

Independent of Lifeline, several major ISPs run their own low-income tiers. These existed before ACP but expanded modestly afterward:

  • Comcast Internet Essentials — $9.95/month for ~100 Mbps. Eligibility via SNAP, Medicaid, WIC, Pell Grant, or Lifeline.
  • AT&T Access — $5–$30/month depending on tier and address. Eligibility via SNAP, SSI, National School Lunch, or Lifeline.
  • Spectrum Internet Assist — $24.99/month for 30 Mbps. Eligibility via SSI for adults 65+ or National School Lunch.
  • Cox Connect2Compete — $9.95/month for households with K-12 students and SNAP/equivalent.
  • T-Mobile Project 10Million — free 100GB/year hotspot for K-12 students at qualifying schools.
  • Optimum Advantage, WOW! Internet Basics, Frontier Lifeline — additional regional programs.

The crucial detail: you can typically combine an ISP-specific program with Lifeline, because Lifeline can be applied to your phone bill while the ISP discount applies to your internet. This is how most former ACP households reconstruct $20+/month in savings post-ACP.

3. State programs

A small number of states stood up partial ACP replacements after federal funding ended. The most notable:

  • New York — ConnectALL — guarantees $15/month broadband for qualifying low-income households via participating ISPs (Spectrum, Optimum, Verizon Fios). Closest thing to a state-level ACP equivalent.
  • California — California LifeLine adds state subsidies on top of federal Lifeline. The state's broader Affordable Internet Initiative is rolling out additional subsidies through 2027.
  • Massachusetts, New Jersey, Vermont — emerging state-level programs at smaller scale.

The federal BEAD program (Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment) is allocating $42.45 billion to states for broadband infrastructure — but BEAD funds infrastructure deployment, not household subsidies, so it doesn't directly replace ACP. State-level low-income subsidies are likely to expand as BEAD-funded networks come online in 2026–2027.

Who was affected, and how badly

Independent surveys of former ACP enrollees from 2024 and 2025 found:

  • ~13% of former ACP households dropped home internet entirely after losing the benefit.
  • ~28% downgraded to a slower or cheaper plan.
  • ~40% kept their plan but reported having to cut other household expenses to afford it.
  • ~19% were unaffected, typically because they qualified for an ISP-specific program before ACP ended.

Senior households (65+), households with K-12 students, and rural households were disproportionately affected because ACP enrollment skewed toward those demographics.

What to do if you were on ACP

  1. Check Lifeline eligibility immediately. If you qualified for ACP, you likely qualify for Lifeline — the income threshold is lower (135% FPL vs 200% FPL), but the categorical eligibility shortcuts (SNAP, Medicaid, SSI) cover most former ACP households.
  2. Check what ISP-specific program is available at your address. Internet Essentials (Comcast/Xfinity), AT&T Access, Spectrum Internet Assist, and Cox Connect2Compete cover most U.S. ZIP codes between them. Apply directly to whichever serves your area.
  3. Stack them where possible. If you have phone service through Lifeline and home internet through Internet Essentials, that's typically $30+ in monthly savings — close to what ACP provided.
  4. If you live in NY, CA, MA, NJ, or VT, check state-level programs in addition to federal options.
  5. If you're on tribal land, federal Tribal Lifeline at $34.25/month is much closer to ACP-era benefits than standard Lifeline.

Will ACP come back?

Unclear. As of May 2026, several reauthorization bills have been introduced in Congress, but none have passed either chamber. The political dynamics are challenging: ACP's bipartisan support eroded as the program's cost grew, and current legislative priorities have not included broadband subsidies at scale.

The most realistic path back is partial: a smaller, more targeted federal benefit (likely $15–$20/month, narrower eligibility) potentially paired with the BEAD infrastructure rollout starting in 2026. Industry groups, including major ISPs that benefited from ACP enrollment, have lobbied for some form of return.

Households that lost ACP should plan as though it's not coming back, while watching for legislative news. Subscribe to FCC and NTIA mailing lists if you want the official notifications.

Frequently asked questions

Can I still apply for ACP?

No. ACP enrollment closed in February 2024 and the program officially ended on June 1, 2024. Any site that promises ACP enrollment in 2026 is misleading at best. The legitimate alternatives are Lifeline, ISP-specific low-cost programs, and state programs.

Is Lifeline the same as ACP?

No. Lifeline is a separate, smaller, longer-running federal program. It pays $9.25/month (vs ACP's $30) and has stricter income eligibility (135% FPL vs ACP's 200% FPL). Lifeline existed for decades before ACP and continues after ACP ended. Most former ACP households qualify for Lifeline.

How is "$9.25 from Lifeline" supposed to replace "$30 from ACP"?

It's not, by itself. The reason Lifeline + an ISP-specific program is the recommended stack is that they cover different costs. Lifeline applied to phone service plus Comcast Internet Essentials at $9.95/month for internet typically replaces $20–$30 of the $30 ACP benefit. The honest answer is that the federal safety net for low-income broadband shrank significantly after ACP ended.

I lost service when ACP ended — can I get reconnected?

Most ISP-specific low-cost programs accept new enrollees regardless of whether you currently have service. Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, and Spectrum Internet Assist will install new service at the low-cost tier for qualifying households. Apply directly with the ISP.

Do I need to live in a "low-income" area to qualify?

No. The eligibility is household-level, not ZIP-level. A household earning under 135% FPL (Lifeline) or qualifying via SNAP/Medicaid/SSI qualifies regardless of neighborhood. ISP-specific programs use similar household-level eligibility.

Are ACP refunds available?

No. Households received the benefit they qualified for through May 2024. There are no retroactive refunds for households that wanted to stay enrolled but couldn't access service after the program ended.

What if my ISP doesn't offer a low-cost program?

Most major U.S. ISPs do offer one — Comcast, AT&T, Spectrum, Cox, Optimum, T-Mobile, Verizon, Frontier all have programs. If you're with a smaller regional ISP, your options are: Lifeline applied directly to the bill, switching to a major ISP that does offer a low-cost program, or state-level programs if you live in NY, CA, MA, NJ, or VT.

Is there an income calculator for these programs?

Yes — our eligibility check uses your state, household size, and rough income to determine which programs you qualify for. Categorical eligibility (SNAP, Medicaid, SSI) lets most households skip the income paperwork.
