Lifeline: The Federal $9.25/Month Phone or Internet Discount

Lifeline is the FCC's long-running federal program that pays $9.25/month toward home internet or phone (or $34.25/month on tribal lands). After ACP ended in 2024, it became the primary federal broadband subsidy still active.

Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Lifeline is a federal program administered by the FCC and the Universal Service Administrative Company (USAC) that pays $9.25/month toward home internet OR phone service for qualifying low-income households. On federally recognized tribal lands, the benefit is $34.25/month. Eligibility is income at or below 135% of the federal poverty level, or participation in SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, Veterans Pension, or Federal Public Housing. Apply at lifelinesupport.org.

1. What is Lifeline?

Lifeline is the United States' oldest active federal communications subsidy program, established by the FCC in 1985 under the Reagan administration. It originated as a phone-only program (the "lifeline" being basic landline service for low-income households) and expanded to include broadband internet in 2016. It's funded through the Universal Service Fund — a small surcharge on most U.S. phone bills — and administered by USAC, a nonprofit accountable to the FCC.

Lifeline serves roughly 6 million households nationwide as of 2026 — significantly less than ACP at its peak, but stable and not subject to the same kind of funding cliff that ended ACP.

2. The benefit amounts

  • Standard Lifeline: $9.25/month off home internet OR phone service.
  • Tribal Lifeline: $34.25/month for households on federally recognized tribal lands.
  • One-or-the-other: A household gets ONE Lifeline benefit and applies it to either phone or internet — not both.
  • One per household: Lifeline is a household-level benefit, not per-person.

Compared to the ACP era, Lifeline alone replaces only a portion of what ACP provided. The standard recommendation post-ACP is to apply Lifeline to phone service and stack an ISP-specific low-cost program (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Internet Assist) for home internet — those are independent of Lifeline and can be combined. 3. Eligibility

Income eligibility

You qualify based on income if your household income is at or below 135% of the federal poverty level (FPL). As of 2026 (48 contiguous states):

Categorical eligibility (the easier path)

You automatically qualify regardless of income if any household member receives any of the following:

  • SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program / Food Stamps)
  • Medicaid
  • Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
  • Federal Public Housing Assistance
  • Veterans Pension or Survivors Pension

For tribal residents, additional categorical paths include Bureau of Indian Affairs General Assistance, Tribal TANF, Food Distribution Program on Indian Reservations, and Tribal Head Start.

Most households take the categorical path because it's faster and avoids paystub paperwork. If you receive SNAP, Medicaid, or SSI, you should not need to document income at all.

4. How to apply

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or the National Verifier preview at lifelinesupport.org.
  2. Gather documents — see Section 5 below. If categorical, you only need proof of the qualifying program.
  3. Apply through the National Verifier — the FCC's centralized eligibility portal at checklifeline.org. Most states use this. Exceptions: California, Texas, and Oregon run their own state-level Lifeline portals.
  4. Wait for verification — typically 24–72 hours for categorical applications, longer for income verification.
  5. Choose a participating provider — see Section 6. Once verified, you contact the provider to apply the discount.
  6. Recertify each year — Lifeline requires annual recertification; USAC sends reminders. If you miss recertification, the benefit lapses.

5. Documents you'll need

For categorical eligibility:

  • One document showing current participation in a qualifying program (SNAP letter, Medicaid card, SSI award letter, etc.)
  • Government-issued photo ID
  • Proof of address (utility bill, lease, etc.)

For income-based eligibility:

  • Pay stubs for the last three months
  • Most recent federal tax return (or letter from the IRS if not required to file)
  • Statement of any benefits (Social Security, unemployment, etc.)
  • Government-issued photo ID
  • Proof of address

6. Participating providers

Hundreds of carriers participate in Lifeline. The right one for your household depends on whether you want the discount on home internet or phone, and what's available at your address.

For home internet

Most large ISPs participate, including Xfinity/Comcast, AT&T, Spectrum/Charter, Cox, Optimum, Frontier, and many regional carriers. Some run their own enhanced low-cost programs (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access) that can layer with Lifeline applied to phone service.

For phone

For wireless phone, the largest Lifeline-participating providers are MVNOs (mobile virtual network operators) that offer free service plus a free phone. The biggest:

  • SafeLink Wireless — TracFone subsidiary; one of the largest Lifeline providers nationally
  • Assurance Wireless — Virgin Mobile / T-Mobile MVNO
  • Q Link Wireless — multi-state Lifeline carrier
  • StandUp Wireless, TruConnect, Life Wireless, Cintex Wireless

The phone-vs-internet decision is one of the most consequential choices in this program — see Section 8 below.

8. The phone-or-internet decision

Lifeline pays for one or the other per household. The question of which is mostly about what your household actually needs and what other discounts you can stack:

  • Apply Lifeline to phone if: you need a working mobile phone and don't already have one, or if your home internet is already covered by an ISP-specific program (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, etc.) that you don't need to stack Lifeline on top of.
  • Apply Lifeline to internet if: you already have a phone you can afford, your home internet is your main expense to reduce, and your ISP doesn't offer a separate low-cost tier.

For most former ACP households, applying Lifeline to phone (often through a free-government-phone MVNO) and getting internet through an ISP-specific low-cost program is the highest total benefit.

9. Related programs and how they stack

  • ISP-specific low-cost internet programs (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Assist) — independent of Lifeline; can be combined with Lifeline applied to phone.
  • State Lifeline programs (CA, TX, OR) — supplement federal Lifeline.
  • Tribal Lifeline — enhanced $34.25/month version on tribal lands.
  • Tribal Broadband Connectivity Program — distinct from Lifeline; funds infrastructure rather than household discounts.
  • LIHEAP and state utility programs — completely separate from Lifeline (different agencies, different domain) but most Lifeline-eligible households also qualify for LIHEAP.

10. What if I don't qualify?

"Not qualified for Lifeline" rarely means "no help available":

  • Income just above 135% FPL: ISP-specific programs (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access) often have higher cutoffs.
  • Income too high for assistance: the savings track — switching providers, negotiating, downgrading speed tiers — usually saves more than Lifeline would have provided.
  • Senior or disabled: some ISPs run senior- or disability-specific tiers separate from Lifeline.

11. Frequently asked questions

Is Lifeline the same as ACP?

No. Lifeline is a separate, smaller, longer-running program. Lifeline pays $9.25/month vs ACP's $30, has stricter eligibility (135% FPL vs 200%), and uses different verification. Lifeline existed for decades before ACP and continues after ACP ended.

Can I get Lifeline for both phone and internet?

No. One Lifeline benefit per household, applied to one service. The workaround for households needing help with both: apply Lifeline to phone, and apply for an ISP-specific low-cost internet program separately.

Does my whole household have to qualify?

Eligibility is at the household level — typically based on the lowest-income household member's situation or any qualifying categorical participation. One person on SNAP qualifies the household.

What does "free government phone" mean?

It's the household-facing label for Lifeline applied to wireless phone service via an MVNO. Companies like SafeLink and Assurance provide a free or near-free phone plus a monthly plan covered by the Lifeline subsidy. The technical name is still Lifeline.

Will Lifeline affect my taxes or other benefits?

No. The Lifeline benefit is not considered taxable income and does not affect SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, or other benefit calculations.

How long does the application take?

Categorical applications typically verify within 24–72 hours. Income-based applications can take longer, especially if documentation needs to be reviewed manually. Plan for 1–2 weeks total from application to discount appearing on your bill.

What happens if I miss the annual recertification?

Your Lifeline benefit lapses and your provider switches you back to retail pricing. You can reapply, but there's typically a gap month. Watch for USAC reminders (they email and mail) about 60 days before your annual deadline.

Can I switch Lifeline providers?

Yes. You can transfer your Lifeline benefit between participating providers, but only one provider can apply your benefit at a time, and there's typically a 60-day waiting period between transfers.

What if my provider doesn't participate in Lifeline?

You'll need to switch to a participating provider, or — if you're using Lifeline for internet and your current ISP doesn't participate — consider applying Lifeline to phone instead and using an ISP-specific low-cost internet program for your existing carrier.
