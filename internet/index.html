How to Save on Your Home Internet Bill: Complete Guide

Two paths lower your internet bill: switching to a cheaper plan or qualifying for assistance you may not know exists. The post-ACP landscape is more confusing than it should be — here's what's still available.

MRBy Marcus Reid, Senior Editor · Reviewed by Priya Sharma, Telecom Policy Analyst · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The average U.S. household pays about $78/month for home internet, but rates vary widely by ZIP code, provider, and how long you've been on your current plan. The fastest savings come from renegotiating your existing bill (typically $15–$30/month) or switching to a fiber or 5G home internet provider where available. If your household qualifies for Lifeline (income at or below 135% of the federal poverty level, or receiving SNAP/Medicaid/SSI), you may also qualify for low-cost ISP programs that replaced parts of the ACP — Comcast Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, and Spectrum Internet Assist all run $10–$30/month.

The two paths to a lower internet bill

Most articles about saving on home internet either (a) tell you to switch providers — useful if you have real options in your ZIP code, less useful if Spectrum or Xfinity is your only realistic choice, or (b) walk through endless tips about Wi-Fi placement that won't actually move the needle on your monthly bill. Both miss the bigger picture.

The savings track and the assistance track work in parallel. Most U.S. households pay more for internet than they need to and are leaving assistance dollars on the table. A typical example: a household earning around 130% of the federal poverty level — common for retirees on Social Security alone, or single parents in low-cost-of-living areas — usually qualifies for both Lifeline (a $9.25/mo discount) and an ISP-specific low-cost program (Internet Essentials at $9.95/mo, AT&T Access starting at $5/mo). Stacking those two against a $80/mo retail plan produces $60+/month in savings, year after year.

Why your internet bill keeps going up

Three patterns account for almost every "my internet bill jumped" story:

1. The promotional cliff

Most ISP advertised prices ($30/mo, $50/mo, $70/mo) are 12-month promotional rates that revert to regular pricing — typically $20–$40 higher per month — automatically at month 13. ISPs are not legally required to remind you. A promotional $50/mo Xfinity plan often becomes an $85/mo plan overnight. The fix: call retention, ask for the new-customer rate, threaten to leave. About 70% of the time you'll get a renewed promo or matched rate.

2. Hidden fees that aren't actually hidden

Equipment rental ($15/mo for a router that's $80 to buy outright), broadcast TV fees on bundled plans, regional sports fees, "internet infrastructure surcharge" — these add 15–25% to advertised prices in most markets. Buying your own modem/router pays for itself in five months. Switching to internet-only (and streaming via Netflix/Hulu/etc.) often saves $30–$50/month versus a triple-play bundle.

3. Bundle traps

"Get internet free with TV and phone!" — except the bundled price minus the value of what you'd actually use is usually $30–$50/month higher than internet alone. Most households we hear from could drop the phone line (they don't use it) and trim a TV tier and cut $400–$600/year without losing anything they value.

The post-ACP landscape

The Affordable Connectivity Program (ACP) was a federal program that paid up to $30/month — $75/month on tribal lands — toward home internet for qualifying low-income households. At its peak it served 23 million households. Federal funding for ACP ran out in mid-2024 and Congress did not reauthorize it. Despite a lot of confusion in 2024 and 2025, no federal program has replaced ACP at the same scale.

Households that lost ACP have three real options today, in rough order of size:

  1. Lifeline — federal, FCC-administered, predates ACP. Pays $9.25/mo (or $34.25/mo on tribal lands) toward phone or internet. Smaller benefit but still active.
  2. ISP-specific low-cost programs — Comcast Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Internet Assist, Cox Connect2Compete, T-Mobile Project 10Million, others. Usually $10–$30/month for a basic plan.
  3. State and tribal programs — New York's ConnectALL, California broadband initiatives, Tribal Broadband Connectivity Program. Highly variable by state.

How much speed do you actually need?

ISPs sell on speed because it's the only metric that's easy to advertise. The honest answer: most households are paying for 2–3× more speed than they use.

  • 1–2 people, basic streaming and browsing: 100 Mbps is plenty.
  • 4-person household, multiple streams + work-from-home: 300–500 Mbps comfortably.
  • Heavy gaming, multiple 4K streams, large file transfers: 1 Gbps starts to matter — but only if your devices and Wi-Fi can actually use it.

If you're paying for gigabit and your peak usage is one Netflix stream and a Zoom call, you're paying for unused capacity. Downgrading to a 300–500 Mbps plan typically saves $15–$25/month with no perceptible difference.

State-specific notes

Internet is much less state-regulated than electricity, but state-level assistance programs vary, and some states have meaningful broadband initiatives:

  • New York — ConnectALL is the state's post-ACP response; offers $15/month broadband for qualifying households via participating ISPs.
  • California — California Lifeline stacks state subsidies on federal Lifeline; the most generous market in the country for low-income broadband.
  • Massachusetts, New Jersey — emerging state broadband programs filling pieces of the ACP gap.
  • Federal RDOF — Rural Digital Opportunity Fund is building out infrastructure in underserved rural areas; if you live in one, your options may be expanding.

Frequently asked questions

What replaced ACP?

Nothing federally. The Affordable Connectivity Program ended mid-2024 and Congress has not passed a replacement. Lifeline, ISP-specific low-cost programs, and a few state initiatives have partially filled the gap, but none at ACP's scale or benefit level. The full breakdown is on our "What replaced ACP?" page.

Can I get Lifeline for both my phone and my home internet?

No. Federal Lifeline is one-or-the-other per household — you can apply the discount to phone OR internet, not both. However, you may be able to combine Lifeline (applied to phone) with an ISP-specific low-cost internet program (like Internet Essentials), which is independent of Lifeline. A specialist can confirm based on your specific situation.

How much can negotiating my bill actually save?

$15–$30/month is typical when calling retention with a defensible alternative ("I'm thinking about switching to T-Mobile 5G Home Internet for $50"). The savings come from re-applying a promotional rate or matching a competitor's offer. Most ISPs would rather keep you at a discount than lose you.

Should I buy my own modem/router?

Almost always yes. ISP equipment rental is typically $15/month, or $180/year. A capable modem/router combo costs $100–$150 outright and is fully compatible with most major cable ISPs. The payback is usually under 7 months. The exception is fiber providers like Verizon Fios that include the equipment in the plan price.

Is satellite internet worth it for rural households?

Starlink has changed the calculus — it's typically faster (100–200 Mbps) and lower latency than HughesNet or Viasat, though still expensive at $80–$120/month plus a $599 hardware fee. For households with no terrestrial option, it's often the best available choice. The Tribal Broadband Connectivity Program and federal RDOF are gradually expanding fixed-line options in rural areas.

What if my only ISP option is Spectrum or Xfinity?

Most U.S. households have only 1–2 real options for cable internet. The best moves are: (1) negotiate hard at the end of every promo period, (2) drop bundled services you don't use, (3) buy your own modem, and (4) check if you qualify for the ISP's own low-cost program — Internet Essentials and Spectrum Internet Assist exist precisely because cable companies face regulatory pressure to offer low-income tiers.
