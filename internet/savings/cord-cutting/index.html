Cord-Cutting in 2026: Replace Cable, Save Real Money

Standalone internet plus selective streaming usually beats cable bundles by $40–$80/month. The 2026 math, the pitfalls, and when bundles still win.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Telecom & broadband research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Most cable+internet bundles cost $150–$220/month. Standalone internet ($55–$80) plus selective streaming ($30–$60) typically totals $85–$140/month — a savings of $50–$80/month or $600–$960/year. The math gets harder if you watch lots of live sports (regional sports networks, NFL, MLB packages) or local news, but for most households, cord-cutting wins. Free over-the-air antenna gets you the local broadcast networks for $0. Free streaming services (Pluto, Tubi, Roku Channel, YouTube) cover most of the rest.

What to replace cable with

Live TV streaming (the closest cable analog)

  • YouTube TV ($73/mo) — most channel options, unlimited DVR, 6 accounts. The default cord-cutter pick.
  • Hulu Live TV ($77/mo) — bundles Hulu's on-demand library. Good for households that want both.
  • Sling TV ($40/mo for Orange or Blue, $60 for both) — fewer channels but cheaper. Good for selective viewers.
  • FuboTV ($85/mo) — sports-heavy. Good if you watch lots of soccer, regional sports.
  • DirecTV Stream ($75–$110/mo) — ESPN+RSN coverage strongest of the bunch but pricey.

On-demand streaming

Pick 2–3 max. Rotating subscriptions (subscribe → watch → cancel → wait → resubscribe) is now standard practice. Most have monthly billing with no contract.

  • Netflix — broad library, original content. $7.99–$23 depending on tier.
  • Disney+, Hulu, Max bundle ($26/mo) — strongest bundle in 2026 since the Disney-Hulu-Max consolidation.
  • Amazon Prime Video — included with Prime ($14.99/mo) which also covers shipping.
  • Apple TV+ ($9.99) — small but high-quality library.
  • Paramount+ ($7.99–$12.99) — CBS shows + Star Trek + sports for some markets.
  • Peacock ($7.99 with ads, $13.99 ad-free) — NBC + Peacock originals + Premier League.

Free options (often overlooked)

  • Over-the-air antenna ($25–$40 one-time) — gets you ABC, CBS, NBC, Fox, PBS, CW, and dozens of subchannels in HD for free, forever. Most cord-cutters underestimate how much TV is still broadcast over-the-air.
  • Pluto TV, Tubi, Roku Channel, Freevee, Plex — ad-supported free streaming with surprisingly large catalogs.
  • YouTube — primary content, plus tons of free TV via channel pages.
  • Local library streaming — Hoopla, Kanopy, free with library card.

When cable bundles still win

  • Heavy local sports viewer. Regional Sports Networks (RSNs) carry local NBA/NHL/MLB games and are inconsistently available on streaming services. If you watch your local team's regular-season games, cable is often the only way to see them all.
  • Live local news consumer. Most live local news is on broadcast TV (free via antenna), but if you watch lots of cable news (CNN, Fox News, MSNBC), the streaming options exist (Sling, YouTube TV, etc.) but at a price.
  • Bundle discount math works in your favor. Some markets have cable + internet bundles where the marginal cost of cable TV is <$30/month — at that price, sometimes worth keeping for the convenience.
  • Multi-room cable boxes. Streaming requires a smart TV or streaming device per TV. If you have 4+ TVs and don't want devices on each, cable's marginal convenience can be worth it.

The cord-cutting process

  1. Audit your current usage. List the channels you actually watch in a typical month. Most cable bundle subscribers watch 10–15 channels regularly out of 200+ available.
  2. Pick a live TV streaming service that covers those channels. Use YouTube TV's channel finder or similar tools. If your channels aren't all available, decide what you can live without.
  3. Add 1–2 on-demand services. Don't subscribe to everything — rotate as needed.
  4. Get an OTA antenna. Even if you have streaming for live TV, antenna is a free backup and gets you local broadcast in HD.
  5. Buy your own modem and router. Cable TV typically requires a specific modem; if you're dropping TV, you can use any approved DOCSIS modem. Saves $10–$15/month.
  6. Call your ISP and request internet-only. Some ISPs raise the internet-only rate when you drop TV. Negotiate. Use the negotiation playbook: How to negotiate →
  7. Schedule the cable disconnect. Time it for the end of your billing cycle. Return any rented equipment.

Watch out for

  • Internet rate hikes when you drop TV. Some ISPs charge $20–$40 more for internet-only. Negotiate or switch.
  • Streaming password sharing crackdowns. Netflix, Disney+, Max have all moved against password sharing. Plan for one household per account.
  • RSN coverage gaps. Regional sports networks (Bally, NBC Sports Regional, MASN, etc.) are inconsistently on streaming. Verify before cutting.
  • Streaming service price hikes. Most major streamers raised prices 10–25% in 2024–2025. Re-audit your bundle every 6 months.
  • Buffering / quality issues with marginal internet. 4K streaming needs at least 25 Mbps stable. If your internet is flaky, cord-cutting can feel worse than cable.

FAQ

Will I miss cable?

Most former cable subscribers don't. The exceptions: heavy live-sports viewers (especially RSN-dependent), and 24-hour cable news junkies who don't want to use a streaming app. Most people find their actual viewing habits map cleanly to streaming.

What about my landline?

Most cable bundles include a landline you don't use. Drop it. If you genuinely need a home phone, VOIP services like Ooma ($5/month) or Magic Jack ($35/year) work over your internet.

Can I keep my cable email address?

If your ISP also provides your email (@comcast.net, @charter.net, etc.) you'll likely lose access if you switch ISPs. Migrate to Gmail or another provider before switching.

Do I need a smart TV?

No. A $30 Roku Express, $40 Amazon Fire Stick, or $50 Chromecast turns any HDMI TV into a smart TV. Many cord-cutters prefer this anyway because the streaming devices update faster than smart TV operating systems.

What's the cheapest possible cord-cutting setup?

Standalone internet ($55) + OTA antenna ($30 one-time) + free streaming services + Netflix Basic ($7.99) = $63/month for a competent TV experience. Saves $90+/month vs. cable bundle.

What if my internet's only option is cable?

You can still cord-cut TV — just keep internet from the cable provider. They'll try to bundle, push back. Or check if 5G home internet is now available at your address. Negotiation scripts →
