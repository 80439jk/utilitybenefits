How to Negotiate Your Internet Bill: 2026 Scripts

The 15-minute phone call that saves $15–$30/month. What to say, leverage to use, and when to actually walk.

SKBy Sara Khan · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Call your ISP's retention or "loyalty" department (not regular customer service). Reference a competitor's specific current price. Say you're considering switching but want to give them a chance to keep your business. Most successful calls produce $15–$30/month off for 12 months ($180–$360/year). Best leverage in 2026: T-Mobile or Verizon 5G Home Internet at $50/month, or fiber at your address if newly available. Call once a year, around when promotional rates expire.

The 15-minute call: step by step

  1. Pull up your most recent bill. Note your current monthly rate, contract end date, and any equipment fees.
  2. Check competitor pricing for your address. The 30-second version: BroadbandNow.com or each competitor's "check availability" page. Note specific competitor + price + speed.
  3. Call your ISP's main customer line. Press 0 or "agent" until you reach a human. When asked about your call, say: "I'm thinking about canceling my service." This routes you to retention.
  4. State your goal. "My bill is $X/month. I see [Competitor] is offering [Y speed for $Z/month]. I'd like to stay if you can match or beat that — can you do anything?"
  5. Wait for their offer. They'll usually offer something modest first ($5–$10 off). Politely decline: "Thanks, but that's still meaningfully more than [Competitor]. Can you do better, or should I move forward with switching?"
  6. Negotiate for the spread. Most reps have authority to drop $20–$30/month for retention. Some can apply a fresh 12-month promo rate. The longer you've been a customer, the more flexibility they have.
  7. Confirm the new price in writing. Ask them to email a confirmation. Save it.
  8. Set a calendar reminder for 12 months out. The new promo rate will expire — call again at the same time next year.

What to say (verbatim scripts)

Opening

"Hi. I've been a customer for [N] years and I want to stay, but my bill has gotten too high. I'm seeing [T-Mobile 5G / Verizon Fios / AT&T Fiber / etc.] offering [speed] for $[price]. I'd love to stay with you, but I need a comparable price. Can you help me?"

If they offer something small

"I appreciate that, but it doesn't get me to where I need to be. The [competitor] offer is locked in — I'd be paying $[X] less per month, every month. Can you escalate to retention or a manager who can do more?"

If they say "we can't match that"

"Okay, I understand. Can you go ahead and start the cancellation process? I'd like to set the disconnect date for [date that gives competitor enough time to install]."

This usually triggers an immediate "let me check what I can do" — most reps have unused authority they only deploy when a customer is actually leaving.

If they actually let you cancel

Then go ahead and switch. The competitor offer is real. Some ISPs (especially monopoly providers in low-competition markets) genuinely won't negotiate — that's a signal that switching is the better move.

Strongest leverage points in 2026

  • 5G home internet at $50/month. T-Mobile, Verizon, and AT&T all offer 5G home internet for $50–$80/month with no contract, no equipment fee, and 200–400 Mbps typical. This is the universal leverage — works anywhere with strong 5G signal. Cable retention departments take it seriously.
  • Newly available fiber. If Verizon Fios, AT&T Fiber, Frontier Fiber, Google Fiber, or local muni-fiber became available at your address recently, that's massive leverage. Cable knows fiber wins on price + reliability.
  • Promotional rates from same provider. Cable companies often have current promotional rates for new customers that are dramatically below what existing customers pay. Mention specifically: "I see your new-customer rate is $[X]. Why am I paying $[Y]?"
  • Tenure. Long-term customers (5+ years) typically get bigger discounts because their lifetime value is high.
  • Multiple services. If you also have cable TV, phone, or wireless with the same company, mention you're considering moving the bundle.

When to call

  • 30 days before your promo rate expires. Easiest negotiation — they're trying to keep you from rolling off.
  • 30 days after a rate increase. Annual rate hikes (3–8%) are the easiest leverage point. Reference the increase specifically.
  • When competitor pricing changes meaningfully. Major fiber expansion, new 5G home internet launch, etc.
  • Annually, regardless. Even if nothing has changed, calling once a year is high-ROI. Worst case: 15 minutes lost. Best case: $300 saved.

What NOT to do

  • Don't bluff a competitor offer that doesn't exist. Reps can verify by zip code. If you say "T-Mobile is $40/month" and it's actually $50, you lose credibility.
  • Don't get angry. The rep has discounts to give and will give them if you're persistent and polite. Anger makes them defensive.
  • Don't accept an offer that requires a longer contract you don't want. Some retention offers re-up you for 24 months. Make sure you understand what you're committing to.
  • Don't accept a "credit" instead of a rate change. One-time credits ($50, $100) are nice but don't fix the underlying high rate. Push for the monthly rate reduction.
  • Don't forget about equipment fees. If they reduce your service rate but the modem rental is still $15/month, you haven't really saved as much as you think. Buy your own modem and router.

After the call

  1. Verify your next bill. The new rate should appear on your next statement. If it doesn't, call back immediately and reference your confirmation email.
  2. Set a 12-month calendar reminder. The new promo will expire — repeat the process.
  3. Buy your own modem and router if you haven't already. Saves another $10–$15/month forever.
  4. Drop your speed tier if you don't need it. Most households can run 200 Mbps comfortably; some retention offers come with a speed downgrade you might not need.

If they really won't budge: switch

Sometimes the answer is no. Monopoly cable providers in low-competition markets occasionally won't negotiate. That's the moment to actually switch. Steps:

  1. Schedule installation with the new provider before canceling. You don't want gaps.
  2. Note your contract end date with the existing ISP — early cancellation typically incurs a $10–$15 per remaining month early termination fee.
  3. Return any rented equipment within the time window your old ISP requires (typically 30 days). Failure to return = $100–$200 unreturned-equipment fees.
  4. Update auto-pay info with the new provider.
  5. Cancel the old ISP after the new one is up and running.

FAQ

How often should I call?

Once a year, typically when your current promo rate is about to expire. Some aggressive negotiators call every 6 months. The retention agents have notes on past negotiations — calling too often (monthly) can hurt your standing.

What if my ISP doesn't have a retention department?

Most do, even if not labeled that way. Try saying "I'm canceling my service" — that always routes to retention. If you genuinely can't reach anyone with discount authority, the ISP is signaling the answer is no.

Do I need to be threatening?

No. Polite + persistent works better than aggressive. The rep wants the call to end with a happy customer; your job is to make the happy outcome require a real discount.

What if I just signed up?

You're already on the promotional rate. Wait until 60 days before your promo expires — that's when leverage is highest.

Can I negotiate by chat or email?

Sometimes. Phone is most effective because retention reps have higher discount authority than chat agents. If phone is genuinely impossible, chat works as a fallback — explicitly ask for retention or a supervisor.
