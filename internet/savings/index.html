Why your internet bill keeps going up

Three quiet drivers:

  1. Promo rates expire. Almost every cable and fiber plan starts at a promotional rate (often 12–24 months) and rolls off to a "standard" rate that's typically $20–$40 higher. Most consumers don't notice for months.
  2. Annual rate hikes. Most ISPs raise standard rates 3–8% per year. Customers who never call retention pay the full hike.
  3. Equipment fees. Modem rental, router rental, "broadcast TV fee" (cable bundles), "regional sports fee," and various other line items often add $15–$30/month. Many can be eliminated by buying your own equipment or unbundling.

The fix in all three cases is the same: call retention every 12 months. Most ISPs would rather discount than lose you.

Switching ISPs

The big switch opportunities in 2026:

  • Fiber where it's newly available. Verizon Fios, AT&T Fiber, Frontier Fiber, Google Fiber, Ziply, Quantum Fiber, and local muni-fiber projects are aggressively expanding. If fiber is now at your address (was not 2 years ago), the math has changed dramatically — typically faster service for $20–$40/month less than cable.
  • 5G home internet. T-Mobile Home Internet ($50/month), Verizon 5G Home ($50–$80), and AT&T Internet Air ($55) are now serious competitors to cable. Best fit: suburban and rural households with strong 5G signal and modest streaming needs (under 1 Gbps speeds typically). Worst fit: dense apartment buildings (signal issues) or households with heavy gaming/4K-streaming/video-call usage requiring sub-20ms latency.
  • Local fiber upstarts. Smaller fiber operators (Sonic, Ting, EPB Fiber Optics, Chattanooga, etc.) often beat the national players on price and customer service.
  • Cable competition where it exists. Comcast/Spectrum/Optimum/Cox rarely overlap territories. Where they do, switching back and forth on promo cycles is a common pattern.

Are you paying for too much speed?

Most households genuinely need 100–300 Mbps download. ISP marketing pushes 1 Gbps+ tiers, but actual usage rarely benefits from speeds beyond a few hundred Mbps. The math:

Use caseRealistic need
1–2 person household, basic streaming + work100–200 Mbps download
3–4 person household, multi-stream + video calls200–400 Mbps download
Heavy gaming (latency matters more than speed)200 Mbps with low latency
4 people simultaneously streaming 4K + working500 Mbps download
Content creator uploading large files1 Gbps with symmetric upload
Most households thinking they need gigabitUsually overpaying

If you're on a 1 Gbps plan and not a content creator with heavy upload needs, you're probably paying $20–$40/month more than you need to. Try the next tier down for a month — most households can't tell the difference.

Hidden fees to fight

  • Modem/router rental ($10–$15/month): buy your own. Decent DOCSIS 3.1 cable modem + Wi-Fi 6 router pays for itself in 8–12 months. Approved-modem lists are on each ISP's website.
  • "Broadcast TV fee" / "Regional sports fee" ($10–$25/month): only applies to cable TV bundles, but ISPs often add it to internet+TV plans. Cancel TV to eliminate.
  • "Network access fee" / "Internet infrastructure fee": made-up line items. Push back or threaten to switch — these are usually negotiable.
  • Data caps and overage fees: Comcast, Cox, AT&T, and others have 1.2 TB caps in some markets with $10/50GB overage. Most households stay under, but heavy 4K-streaming + gaming households can blow past. Unlimited add-ons usually cost $30/month — usually cheaper to just switch ISPs.
  • Early termination fees: $10–$15 per remaining contract month. If you're switching, time the cancellation to your contract end.

If you're income-qualified

The ACP (Affordable Connectivity Program) ended in May 2024. ISPs and the federal government have rolled out replacement programs:

  • Comcast Internet Essentials — $9.95/month or $29.95/month for 100/200 Mbps. Available to households on Lifeline programs (SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, etc.).
  • AT&T Access — starting at $5/month. Income limit varies by tier.
  • Spectrum Internet Assist — $24.99/month for 30 Mbps. Available to households with seniors on SSI or kids on free/reduced lunch.
  • Cox Connect2Compete — similar structure, income-qualified.
  • Optimum Advantage Internet — $14.99/month, income-qualified.
  • Federal Lifeline — $9.25/month discount on top of any of these. Lifeline guide →

FAQ

How much can I really save by negotiating?

$15–$30/month is typical for a successful retention call — that's $180–$360/year. The savings come from re-applying a promotional rate or matching a competitor's offer. Detailed scripts: How to negotiate →

Is 5G home internet really as good as cable?

For most households: yes, at lower price. For heavy gamers or 4K streamers in dense areas: not always. T-Mobile, Verizon, and AT&T 5G home internet plans are $50–$80/month for 200–400 Mbps typical. Best fit: suburban houses with strong 5G signal. Worst fit: dense apartment buildings (signal issues), rural areas without 5G, or households needing <20ms gaming latency.

Should I buy my own modem and router?

Yes. ISP rental fees are $10–$15/month — $120–$180/year. A decent modem + Wi-Fi 6 router costs $200–$300 and lasts 5+ years. Payback in 8–12 months. ISPs publish approved-modem lists on their support sites.

What if I have only one ISP available?

Even monopoly ISPs negotiate. The retention department's KPI is customer churn — they have authority to offer credits and promotional rates. Mention you're considering 5G home internet, fixed wireless, or just dropping internet entirely. Scripts →

Can I keep my email address if I switch ISPs?

If you have an @comcast.net, @att.net, @charter.net, or similar ISP email, you'll likely lose access when you cancel. Migrate to Gmail or another free service before switching. (Most ISP email is also less reliable for important account recovery anyway.)

What's the post-ACP landscape?

The federal Affordable Connectivity Program ended May 2024. Replacement options: ISP-specific low-cost programs (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Assist), federal Lifeline ($9.25/mo discount), and state-specific programs in CA, NY, MA. Detailed guide: What replaced ACP? →
