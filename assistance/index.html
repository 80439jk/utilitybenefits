Utility Bill Assistance Programs: Every Path in One Place

Federal, state, and utility-specific programs for households at or below 200% of the federal poverty level. Most qualifying households stack 3–6 programs and don't realize they qualify for half of them.

SKBy Sara Khan, Senior Editor · Reviewed by Marcus Reid, Energy Policy Analyst · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The main assistance programs by utility: LIHEAP (federal energy assistance, $400–$1,500/yr), Lifeline ($9.25/mo phone or internet discount), state energy programs (CARE, PIPP, USF, EUSP, A-60, EAP, etc. — often larger than LIHEAP), ISP low-cost programs (Comcast Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Internet Assist), Medicaid HCBS waivers (cover medical alert in 30+ states), VA programs (Aid & Attendance, others), and state water CAP rates. Most households at 200% FPL qualify for $1,500–$3,500/year in stacked benefits.

LIHEAP — the federal energy backbone

LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program) is the federal program that helps eligible low-income households pay heating, cooling, and energy crisis bills. Available in every state. Income limit: 150% of the federal poverty level OR 60% of state median income (whichever is higher). Categorical eligibility: SNAP, SSI, TANF (and in many states Medicaid) recipients qualify automatically.

Annual benefits typically range $400–$1,500/year, with crisis components for shutoff threats. Apply through your state energy office or local Community Action Agency.

Lifeline — phone and internet

Lifeline is the federal $9.25/month discount on phone OR internet for households at 135% of the federal poverty level or receiving SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, WIC, or living on tribal lands. Layers with ISP-specific low-cost programs (Comcast Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, etc.) and "free government phone" providers (SafeLink, Assurance, Q Link).

State programs (often larger than LIHEAP)

State-specific programs often pay more than federal LIHEAP and have higher income cutoffs:

  • California — CARE/FERA: 30–35% bill discount on PG&E, SCE, SDG&E. CA guide →
  • Ohio — PIPP Plus: caps electric bill at 6% of household income. OH guide →
  • New Jersey — USF: monthly bill credit up to $150. NJ guide →
  • Pennsylvania — CAP rates from PECO/PPL/Duquesne: caps bill at 4–10% of income. PA guide →
  • New Hampshire — EAP: 8–76% monthly electric discount depending on tier. NH guide →
  • Maryland — EUSP: monthly bill credit. MD guide →
  • Washington DC — UDP: discounts on Pepco, Washington Gas, DC Water. DC guide →
  • Rhode Island — A-60: permanent income-qualified rate-tier reduction. RI guide →

Medical alert + Medicaid HCBS

For seniors and disability households, Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers cover medical alert systems (PERS — Personal Emergency Response Systems) in 30+ states. Apply through your state Medicaid agency.

VA Aid & Attendance provides cash benefits to qualifying veterans that can pay for medical alerts and other in-home services.

Categorical eligibility shortcuts

Most assistance programs have an income test PLUS categorical eligibility — meaning you can skip the income paperwork if you already participate in:

  • SNAP (food stamps) — qualifies for LIHEAP, Lifeline, Internet Essentials, and most state programs
  • SSI (Supplemental Security Income) — qualifies for LIHEAP, Lifeline, most state programs
  • TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) — qualifies for LIHEAP, Lifeline
  • Medicaid — qualifies for Lifeline, Internet Essentials; varies by state for LIHEAP
  • WIC (Women, Infants, Children) — qualifies for Lifeline
  • Veterans / Survivors Pension — qualifies for Lifeline; specific VA programs available
  • Free / Reduced-Price School Lunch — qualifies for Spectrum Internet Assist, some state programs
  • Tribal residency — additional Lifeline tier, additional ISP low-cost programs

If you're enrolled in any of the above, your eligibility paperwork for utility assistance is largely already done.

If you're in crisis (shutoff threat)

Most states have crisis assistance programs with 18–48 hour processing times for active shutoff threats:

  • LIHEAP Crisis (federal) — for energy shutoffs
  • State-specific crisis funds — names vary (CRISIS in PA, ECIP in MO/NC, etc.)
  • Utility hardship programs — Reliant CARE, TXU Energy Aid, Dollar Energy Fund, Salvation Army REACH
  • 211 — dial 211 from any phone for local emergency utility assistance referrals
  • Cold-weather and heat-wave shutoff moratoriums in most states
