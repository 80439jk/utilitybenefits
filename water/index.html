Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The average U.S. household pays about $70/month for water and sewer combined — but bills vary 5× by city (Las Vegas at the low end, Seattle and San Francisco at the high end). When water bills spike dramatically, the cause is almost always a leak — most commonly a leaking toilet flap (which can waste 200+ gallons/day silently). Water utilities are municipal monopolies — no provider switching exists. Federal LIHWAP (the water-specific assistance program created during COVID) ended in 2023 with no replacement. The remaining assistance landscape is mostly city-level: Customer Assistance Programs (CAPs) at major utilities, leak forgiveness programs, and state-level water assistance in CA, AZ, NV, NJ, PA. Conservation rebates for water-efficient fixtures, drought-tolerant landscaping, and smart irrigation are concentrated in drought-prone Western states.

Three structural realities that shape water bills

Water is the most different utility from electricity and gas — three things make it unusual:

  • No provider switching. Your water comes from a municipal utility (NYC DEP, LADWP, Phoenix Water Services, etc.) or a regional water authority. There's no deregulation, no shopping. The savings track is mostly about reducing usage and detecting leaks.
  • No federal assistance backbone. LIHWAP (the federal Low-Income Household Water Assistance Program) ran from late 2021 through mid-2023 with $1.1B in COVID-era funding, then ended. No federal replacement has passed Congress. Assistance is now mostly municipal-level.
  • Bills are dollarized differently. Most water bills include a fixed service charge plus tiered usage rates that get steeper as usage rises (to encourage conservation). A small leak that doubles your usage can quadruple your bill because it pushes you into expensive top tiers.

Why your water bill is "suddenly" so high (almost always a leak)

If your water bill jumped 2× or more month-over-month with no usage changes, the answer is almost always a leak. The most common culprits:

  • Leaking toilet flapper — by far the most common. A worn toilet flap that doesn't seal properly causes the toilet to silently refill all day. Can waste 200+ gallons per day. Diagnostic: put food coloring in the toilet tank, wait 15 minutes without flushing — if color appears in the bowl, the flap is leaking. Fix: $5 replacement flapper, 10 minutes of work.
  • Running irrigation / sprinkler controller stuck — second most common. Sprinkler controllers fail to a "stuck on" state surprisingly often. Check controller display.
  • Burst pipe (rare but expensive) — usually visible. Check for damp spots in walls, ceilings, basement.
  • Water heater leak — slow leaks at the bottom of the tank, often visible as rust or sediment.
  • Outdoor faucet drip — minor by itself but can add up.

The post-LIHWAP landscape

The federal Low-Income Household Water Assistance Program (LIHWAP) was created during COVID to help low-income households with overdue water bills. It ran from late 2021 through mid-2023 with a one-time $1.1 billion appropriation. When that funding ran out, Congress did not renew it.

What remains:

  • Customer Assistance Programs (CAPs) at major water utilities — discount tiers for low-income customers, payment plans, arrearage forgiveness. Available at most large urban water utilities (NYC DEP, LADWP, Phoenix, Philadelphia, Detroit, etc.).
  • State-level programs in a small number of states (California Water Resilience Funding, NJ Water Assistance, PA Customer Assistance Program rules).
  • Lead Pipe Replacement Assistance through federal IIJA funding ($15B for lead service line replacement, distributed through state revolving funds).
  • Senior/disability/veteran discounts at some utilities.
  • 211 emergency assistance for shutoff prevention.

Conservation rebates (concentrated in West)

Conservation rebates pay you to install water-saving fixtures or convert lawns to drought-tolerant landscaping. Concentrated in drought-prone states:

  • WaterSense toilets and showerheads — federal rebate eligibility, plus utility-specific rebates ($75–$200 typical).
  • "Cash for Grass" / turf removal — Las Vegas pioneered this; now offered in much of CA, AZ, NM. $1–$3 per square foot of grass converted to xeriscape. Las Vegas SNWA pays up to $3/sq ft.
  • Smart irrigation controllers — utility rebates of $80–$200 for WiFi-connected controllers that respond to weather data.
  • Greywater systems — rebates available in CA, AZ; legality varies by state for whole-home systems.
  • Pool covers — rebates in some Western utilities; reduce evaporation losses.

Lead pipe replacement assistance

The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) provided $15B for lead service line replacement nationally. Most cities distribute this through their water utility:

  • Free or subsidized lead service line replacement for income-qualified households in many cities (Chicago, Newark, Pittsburgh, Detroit, others).
  • Lead Service Line Inventory programs require utilities to identify and notify customers with lead pipes by 2024.
  • EPA's revised Lead and Copper Rule requires faster replacement timelines.

If you live in an older home (pre-1986) and haven't been notified about your service line material, contact your water utility — most have free testing programs.

Frequently asked questions

Why is my water bill so much higher this month?

Almost always a leak. Most common: leaking toilet flap. Diagnostic: food coloring in tank, wait 15 minutes — if color reaches the bowl without flushing, the flap leaks. $5 part, 10 minute fix. Other common causes: stuck irrigation controller, burst pipe (visible damage), failed water heater. Full diagnostic guide.

Can I switch water providers?

No. Water and sewer are municipal monopolies — your water utility is determined by where you live within a city or county. There's no deregulation, no shopping. The savings path is reducing usage and detecting leaks.

What replaced LIHWAP?

Nothing federally. LIHWAP ran from 2021–2023 with one-time COVID funding, then ended without renewal. The remaining options are city-level Customer Assistance Programs (CAPs), state programs in a few states (CA, NJ, PA), and 211 referrals for emergency assistance.

Can my water utility shut me off?

Yes, but most cities have shutoff moratoriums or formal hardship procedures that delay disconnection. CAP enrollment usually pauses shutoff. Check your specific city's rules at our water assistance hub or call your utility directly.

Is septic system maintenance covered by anything?

Generally no — septic system pumping ($300–$600 every 3–5 years) is a homeowner expense. Some rural electric cooperatives offer septic loan programs. USDA Rural Development has loan/grant programs for major repairs in some rural counties.

What about lead pipes in my home?

If you live in pre-1986 housing and haven't received notification from your utility, contact them about Lead Service Line Inventory. Most cities now offer free service line inspection. Federal IIJA funding makes replacement free or subsidized for many income-qualified households.

Are conservation rebates worth it?

In drought-prone Western states, often yes. Las Vegas SNWA's "Cash for Grass" pays enough to cover most of the conversion cost. Smart irrigation controllers pay back in 1–2 years through reduced water bills. WaterSense toilets save $100+/year on water and qualify for rebates that often cover most of the toilet cost. Outside the West, rebates are smaller and less impactful.
