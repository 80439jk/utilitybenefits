Water bill assistance: what replaced LIHWAP and what's actually available

Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Nothing federal replaced LIHWAP. The federal Low-Income Household Water Assistance Program ran from 2021 through 2023 with one-time COVID funding ($1.1 billion total) and ended when funds ran out. Congress did not renew it. The remaining water assistance landscape is mostly municipal: Customer Assistance Programs (CAPs) at most major water utilities, leak forgiveness programs, state-level programs in CA, NJ, PA, and federal lead pipe replacement assistance through the 2021 IIJA. Most cities have shutoff prevention pathways and 211 emergency assistance for water bills.

What was LIHWAP?

The Low-Income Household Water Assistance Program (LIHWAP) was a federal benefit created through COVID emergency legislation (Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021, plus American Rescue Plan supplements). It paid up to roughly $2,500 per household toward water and wastewater bills for low-income households facing hardship. At peak it served about 200,000 households nationally.

LIHWAP funding totaled $1.1 billion across two appropriations, distributed by HHS through the same state agencies that administer LIHEAP. Unlike LIHEAP (which has been continuously funded since 1981), LIHWAP was structured as a one-time COVID-era response. When funds ran out in mid-2023, no replacement appropriation followed.

The political dynamics around LIHWAP renewal have been similar to ACP — bipartisan recognition that water affordability is a problem, no agreement on funding levels or program structure for a permanent version.

What's actually available now

1. City Customer Assistance Programs (CAPs)

Most major U.S. water utilities operate their own Customer Assistance Programs — discount tiers for low-income customers, payment plans, sometimes arrearage forgiveness. These are the largest source of water assistance now that LIHWAP is gone.

2. State water assistance programs

A small number of states operate their own water assistance programs:

  • California — Water Resilience Funding through state appropriations; California Water Affordability Act provides framework for assistance programs at investor-owned water utilities.
  • New Jersey — NJ Water Assistance Program, narrow eligibility but real benefit for qualifying households.
  • Pennsylvania — PUC requires Customer Assistance Program rules at investor-owned water utilities (similar to gas/electric CAP).
  • Oregon — Oregon Low-Income Home Water Assistance Program at some utilities.

3. Lead pipe replacement assistance

The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act allocated $15 billion for lead service line replacement nationally, distributed through state Drinking Water State Revolving Funds. Most cities offer:

  • Free or subsidized lead service line replacement for income-qualified households (Chicago, Newark, Pittsburgh, Detroit, Milwaukee, Cincinnati, others).
  • Free water testing for lead in older homes.
  • Lead Service Line Inventory programs that identify whose pipes need replacement.

The EPA's revised Lead and Copper Rule (LCRR/LCRI) requires utilities to identify and replace lead service lines on accelerated timelines. Households in pre-1986 housing who haven't been notified should contact their water utility about lead testing.

4. Senior, veteran, and disability discounts

Many water utilities offer discount programs for specific demographic categories:

  • Senior discounts — typical at major urban utilities. Usually 25%–50% off water charges for seniors meeting income limits.
  • Veteran discounts — some utilities (varies widely).
  • Disability discounts — usually requires SSI/SSDI documentation.
  • Medical condition baselines — some utilities (notably in California) provide higher water baselines for households with qualifying medical conditions (e.g., dialysis at home).

Water shutoff prevention

Facing water shutoff?

Most major cities have shutoff moratoriums, payment plan options, or emergency funds. CAP enrollment usually pauses shutoff. A specialist can engage all available pathways simultaneously.

Water shutoff rules vary city by city, but most major cities have at least one of these protections:

  • Formal shutoff moratoriums — Detroit, Newark, and a handful of cities have ongoing or seasonal shutoff prohibitions.
  • Pre-shutoff notice requirements — most cities require 30+ days notice and offer payment plans before shutoff.
  • Medical/vulnerable household protections — households with infants, seniors, or medical conditions often qualify for delays or alternatives.
  • Emergency funds via 211 — local emergency utility assistance for households in crisis.

Leak forgiveness

Most major water utilities have leak forgiveness programs that adjust bills affected by documented leaks. The standard process:

  1. Identify and fix the leak (most commonly a toilet flapper — see our diagnostic guide).
  2. Document the fix (receipt for new flapper, photos, etc.).
  3. Contact your water utility's customer service.
  4. Request a leak adjustment. Most utilities will rebill at average usage rates.
  5. Watch for required forms (some utilities require Plumber's Affidavit; others accept self-reports).

Categorical eligibility shortcuts

Many city CAPs use the same categorical eligibility shortcuts as LIHEAP and Lifeline. If your household receives any of:

  • SNAP / Food Stamps
  • Medicaid
  • SSI
  • TANF
  • Federal Public Housing Assistance

...you typically qualify for water CAPs without separate income paperwork. Bring documentation of the program you're enrolled in.

Frequently asked questions

Is there any federal water assistance program now?

No federal household-level water bill assistance program exists as of May 2026. LIHWAP ran from 2021 to 2023 with COVID-era funding and was not renewed. The federal IIJA funds infrastructure (lead pipe replacement) but not household bills.

Will LIHWAP come back?

Unclear. Several reauthorization bills have been introduced in Congress but none have passed. The political dynamics are similar to ACP — recognition of need, no agreement on funding. Plan as though it's not coming back; watch for legislative news.

How do I find my city's water assistance program?

Three paths: (1) call your water utility's customer service and ask about "low-income discount" or "Customer Assistance Program," (2) check your utility's website (look for "bill help" or "assistance" links), or (3) call 211 from any phone for routing.

Do these programs require U.S. citizenship?

Most don't. Water utility CAPs typically require proof of residency in the service area and income qualification, not citizenship. Verify with your specific utility — rules vary.

Can renters qualify?

Yes when you pay your own water bill. If water is included in your rent, the regular benefit is harder to access. Some cities have specific multifamily affordability programs that work through landlords.

What about wells or septic systems?

Households with private wells generally don't have water bills (you pay for the well, pump, and electricity). Septic systems similarly are a homeowner expense — pumping costs, repairs, etc. USDA Rural Development has loan/grant programs for septic and well repair in some rural counties.

What if my water bill is in my landlord's name?

City CAPs typically require the bill to be in your name. If utilities are bundled into rent, your options are: (1) ask your landlord if they participate in any multifamily affordability program, (2) check your state for tenant-specific assistance programs, or (3) request to put the bill in your own name (requires landlord cooperation).

Is sewer assistance separate from water assistance?

Often combined. Most municipal utilities bill water and sewer together; CAP programs typically cover both. In a few cities, sewer is billed separately and may have its own assistance program.
