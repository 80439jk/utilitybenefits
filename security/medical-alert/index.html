Medical Alert Systems: Best PERS, Medicaid Coverage, and VA Benefits

Personal Emergency Response Systems are the area where home security meets benefits assistance. Many seniors qualify for partial or full coverage through Medicaid HCBS waivers (state-dependent) or VA programs. Here's the honest breakdown.

Medical alert systems (Personal Emergency Response Systems, or PERS) cost $25–$50/month for in-home models and $40–$60/month for mobile/GPS models with fall detection. Medicaid covers PERS in many states through Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers — typically as part of long-term care benefits for seniors aging in place. VA benefits cover medical alerts for veterans receiving Aid and Attendance, Housebound benefits, or VA long-term care services. Some Medicare Advantage plans include medical alert as a supplemental benefit. The best providers in 2026: Medical Guardian, MobileHelp, Bay Alarm Medical, Lively, and LifeFone.

What a medical alert system actually does

A PERS — Personal Emergency Response System — is a wearable button (pendant, wristband, or watch) connected to a 24/7 monitoring center. Press the button, and an operator opens a two-way voice channel through a base station and dispatches emergency services if needed.

Modern PERS adds:

  • Fall detection — accelerometer and gyroscope detect a fall and auto-trigger help even if the user can't press the button (typical $5–$15/month upcharge).
  • GPS tracking — mobile units work outside the home, with location reported to the monitoring center on activation. Important for active seniors and dementia/wandering risk.
  • Medication reminders — built into some apps and devices.
  • Health monitoring integration — newer devices connect to glucose monitors, blood pressure cuffs, etc.

The core value proposition is the same as decades-old PERS: a fall, heart event, or other emergency triggers help fast even if the senior is alone and can't reach a phone.

Medicaid coverage by state

Some states cover PERS through Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers — programs that help Medicaid-eligible seniors and disabled adults remain at home rather than entering nursing facilities. The benefit is usually structured as a service rather than a cash benefit: Medicaid pays the PERS provider directly.

States with strong PERS coverage through Medicaid HCBS

  • New York — covered through MLTC (Managed Long-Term Care) plans and HCBS waivers
  • California — covered through Multipurpose Senior Services Program (MSSP) waiver
  • Massachusetts — covered through Frail Elder Waiver and others
  • Minnesota — covered through Elderly Waiver and Alternative Care
  • Wisconsin — covered through IRIS and Family Care
  • Connecticut — covered through Connecticut Home Care Program for Elders
  • Pennsylvania — covered through Aging Waiver and OBRA Waiver
  • Illinois — covered through Community Care Program (CCP)
  • Michigan — covered through MI Choice Waiver
  • Ohio — covered through PASSPORT and Assisted Living Waiver
  • Texas — covered through STAR+PLUS
  • Florida — covered through Statewide Medicaid Managed Care Long-Term Care

Medicaid PERS eligibility (general rules)

  • Be Medicaid-eligible. Income and asset limits apply; varies by state. Generally aimed at seniors below 100–300% FPL.
  • Demonstrate need for nursing-home-level care that the household is avoiding by aging in place. Usually requires functional assessment.
  • Enroll in the relevant HCBS waiver program. Application typically through your state Medicaid office or local Area Agency on Aging.

VA medical alert benefits for veterans

Veterans receiving any of the following may qualify for VA-covered medical alert systems:

  • Aid and Attendance benefit — pension supplement for veterans needing help with activities of daily living. Annual benefit $14,000+ for single veterans, $25,000+ for married veterans. Can be applied toward medical alert costs.
  • Housebound benefit — for veterans confined substantially to home due to disability.
  • Veteran-Directed Care (VDC) — VA program where eligible veterans manage a personal budget that can include PERS.
  • Geriatric and Extended Care services — broader VA long-term care benefits that may include PERS.

The path varies — sometimes VA buys the device directly, sometimes the veteran is reimbursed through Aid and Attendance. Talk to your VA medical center's social work team or our specialists.

Medicare and Medicare Advantage

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) does NOT cover medical alert systems. This is one of the persistent confusions in this category — Medicare pays for hospital stays and doctor visits, not preventive monitoring devices.

Medicare Advantage plans (Part C) sometimes cover PERS as a supplemental benefit. Coverage varies dramatically by plan and region. If you're on Medicare Advantage, check your plan's Summary of Benefits or call member services to ask about "personal emergency response systems" or "medical alert."

Long-term care insurance (private LTC policies) often covers PERS — read your policy.

If coverage doesn't apply

If neither Medicaid, VA, Medicare Advantage, nor LTC insurance covers a medical alert in your situation, the practical options:

  • Pay out of pocket. $25–$60/month is meaningfully less than the cost of a single ER visit from a fall. The math usually works.
  • No-monthly-fee options. Some devices (Apple Watch with fall detection + cellular plan, certain Lively devices) provide PERS-like functionality without ongoing monitoring fees — though without 24/7 monitoring center.
  • Spouse plan. Most providers offer two-person plans that add a second pendant for $5–$15/month — half the cost of two individual subscriptions.
  • Senior discounts. AARP members get 10–15% discounts at several major providers.

What about the Apple Watch?

Apple Watch (Series 4 and later) has fall detection. Watch with cellular plan can call 911 and notify emergency contacts. Fall detection auto-calls 911 if the wearer doesn't respond within 60 seconds.

Pros vs traditional PERS: more capable device, no separate monthly monitoring fee (just the cellular plan), socially normal to wear.

Cons: Requires user comfort with technology, requires charging daily (vs months of battery for typical PERS), no dedicated 24/7 monitoring center personnel who know the user's situation.

Reasonable for tech-comfortable seniors. For seniors with cognitive decline or who won't reliably keep a watch charged, traditional PERS is more reliable.

Frequently asked questions

Does Medicare cover medical alerts?

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) does not. Some Medicare Advantage plans (Part C) include PERS as a supplemental benefit — varies by plan. Always verify with your specific Advantage plan's member services.

Will Medicaid pay for my parent's medical alert?

Possibly, depending on your state. Medicaid HCBS waivers in many states cover PERS for seniors who would otherwise need nursing-facility care. Eligibility requires Medicaid enrollment plus a functional assessment. Talk to your state Medicaid office, local Area Agency on Aging, or our specialists.

What's the difference between in-home and mobile PERS?

In-home PERS uses a base station with limited range (typically 600–1,500 feet from the base). Works only at home. Mobile/GPS PERS uses cellular and works anywhere — important for active seniors who go out, and for households where the base would have spotty coverage.

Do I need fall detection?

Recommended for users with mobility issues, balance concerns, or history of falls. Fall detection auto-triggers help when a fall is detected, even if the user can't press the button (unconscious, broken arm, etc.). $5–$15/month upcharge typically; usually worth it.

How do I avoid contracts?

Most major providers (Medical Guardian, MobileHelp, Bay Alarm Medical, LifeFone) offer no-contract month-to-month service. Avoid Life Alert (3-year contracts) unless their specific device matters to you.

What happens if my parent presses the button accidentally?

The monitoring center will call them through the base station's two-way voice. If they confirm it's a false alarm, no further action. False alarms are common and don't trigger fees. The system is designed for over-triggering rather than under-triggering — better safe than sorry.

Can I get a free trial?

Bay Alarm Medical and a few others offer 30-day money-back trials. Useful if you want to test home coverage and your parent's comfort with the device before committing.

What if my parent has dementia?

GPS-equipped devices with caregiver-facing apps (Aloe Care, Lively) are best for households where the senior may wander. Some providers integrate with caregiver phones to alert when the user leaves a defined area. Combine with home automation (door sensors, etc.) for higher safety.
