Does Medicaid Cover Medical Alert Systems in Washington, D.C.?

Yes via EPD Waiver through Elderly & Persons with Disabilities (EPD) Waiver. The application path, income/asset limits, wait lists, and state-specific alternatives.

SKBy Sara Khan · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

In Washington, D.C., Medicaid yes via epd waiver through Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers. Eligibility requires Medicaid enrollment plus a functional assessment confirming nursing-facility level of care. Income limit: 300% FBR · Asset limit: $4,000 (DC has higher asset limit than most states). Wait list: Generally minimal. Apply through DC Department of Health Care Finance (DHCF) or your local Area Agency on Aging.

Washington, D.C.-specific notes

DC's EPD Waiver covers PERS for elderly and disabled DC residents. DC also has a higher asset limit ($4,000 vs $2,000 federal default), broadening eligibility. DACL operates the Safe at Home program — additional in-home safety modifications including PERS — for seniors above Medicaid limits.

How to apply in Washington, D.C.

  1. Contact your local Area Agency on Aging — they're the front door for HCBS waiver applications. AAAs serve every county.
  2. Confirm Medicaid eligibility — you must be enrolled in Washington, D.C. Medicaid, or eligible to enroll under the waiver income/asset limits.
  3. Functional assessment — a nurse or assessor reviews activities of daily living (ADLs) to confirm nursing-facility level of care need. Required for HCBS eligibility.
  4. Care plan development — a case manager builds a service plan including PERS, home care, and other approved services.
  5. Service start — once approved, PERS provider is selected and service begins. Medicaid pays the provider directly.

VA benefits for Washington, D.C. veterans

Washington, D.C. veterans may qualify for medical alert coverage through:

  • VA Aid & Attendance — cash benefit of up to $2,727/month for qualifying wartime veterans needing daily assistance. Can pay for PERS service.
  • VA Housebound Benefit — for veterans substantially confined to their home; lower benefit but easier qualifying criteria.
  • VA Geriatrics & Extended Care — direct VA-provided medical alert systems for qualifying veterans through their VA medical center.
  • VA Homemaker / Home Health Aide Program — services-based program available through VA medical centers.

Apply for VA benefits through VA.gov or your local VA medical center.

Alternatives if you don't qualify for Medicaid

If you don't qualify for Washington, D.C. Medicaid (income too high, assets too high, or on a long wait list), other paths exist:

  • State Aging Department programs — DC Department of Aging and Community Living (DACL) operates state-funded long-term services that often include PERS for seniors above Medicaid limits. Sliding fee scale common.
  • Older Americans Act services — federally funded through Area Agencies on Aging. Often cover PERS for seniors 60+ regardless of income.
  • Medicare Advantage plans — some Part C plans include PERS as a supplemental benefit. Verify with your specific plan's member services.
  • Long-term care insurance — most LTC policies cover PERS as a covered service.
  • Direct purchase — Medical Guardian, MobileHelp, Bay Alarm Medical, and others run $25–$50/month with no contract. Provider comparison →

Washington, D.C.-specific FAQ

Where do I apply for HCBS waiver in Washington, D.C.?

Through your local Area Agency on Aging or directly through DC Department of Health Care Finance (DHCF). The state aging agency (DC Department of Aging and Community Living (DACL)) maintains a directory of local AAAs at dacl.dc.gov.

What's the wait list situation in Washington, D.C.?

Generally minimal. Apply early regardless — even short wait lists can grow.

What's the income limit for Washington, D.C. Medicaid HCBS waivers?

300% FBR. Asset limit: $4,000 (DC has higher asset limit than most states). Spousal impoverishment protections apply if married — speak with a Medicaid specialist or elder-law attorney to understand how spousal income/assets are counted.

Can my parent qualify for both Medicaid HCBS and VA Aid & Attendance?

Yes — they're separate programs. Aid & Attendance is a cash benefit through VA; Medicaid HCBS is a services benefit through Washington, D.C. Medicaid. Many veterans receive both. Note that Aid & Attendance counts as income for Medicaid eligibility.

What if my parent isn't eligible for Medicaid yet but assets are above limit?

Several paths: spend down medical and care expenses (Medicaid spend-down rules vary by state), Medicaid asset protection trusts (require 5-year lookback, see an elder-law attorney), or rely on alternative programs (DC Department of Aging and Community Living (DACL) state-funded services, VA benefits, Medicare Advantage). A specialist can help map options.

Does Washington, D.C. Medicaid cover fall detection?

The state's HCBS waiver covers PERS — the specific equipment (in-home only, mobile/GPS, fall detection) is determined by the case manager based on assessed need. Most cases approve fall detection for users with mobility/balance concerns.

What if my parent moves to Washington, D.C. from another state?

Washington, D.C. Medicaid eligibility starts at residency. If your parent had HCBS in another state, they re-apply in Washington, D.C.. Some states have reciprocity for the functional eligibility assessment, which can speed re-enrollment.

Are there Washington, D.C.-specific medical alert providers?

Washington, D.C. Medicaid contracts with regional and national PERS providers — your case manager will assign one based on your county. Outside Medicaid, the major national providers (Medical Guardian, MobileHelp, Bay Alarm Medical, Lively, LifeFone) all serve Washington, D.C..
