Lower Your Home Security Cost: The 2026 Playbook

Modern DIY systems make $30–$80/month traditional pro monitoring largely optional. Plus insurance discounts that pay for the system. And Medicaid/VA coverage for medical alerts.

SKBy Sara Khan · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Modern DIY systems (Ring, SimpliSafe, Wyze, eufy) cost $200–$500 upfront with $0–$25/month monitoring — half to one-third the cost of professional systems (ADT, Vivint at $30–$80/month) for comparable protection. Self-monitoring is genuinely free on Ring, Wyze, and eufy. Most homeowners insurance gives 5–20% discounts for installed monitored systems — typically $50–$300/year, often paying for the equipment within 1–2 years. For medical alerts, Medicaid covers in some states via Home and Community-Based Services waivers; VA Aid & Attendance covers for qualifying veterans.

Self-monitoring vs. professional monitoring

Professional monitoring means a 24/7 monitoring center receives alerts and dispatches police. Self-monitoring means you receive the alert on your phone and decide what to do. The case for self-monitoring:

  • You save $20–$50/month forever. Self-monitoring is free or nearly free; pro-monitoring runs $20–$80/month depending on system.
  • False alarms are mostly your problem either way. Pro monitoring often delays calling police (typical 30–60 second wait for verification) and many municipalities charge $50–$200 fines for false police dispatches. Self-monitoring lets you handle verification yourself.
  • Most break-ins happen quickly. By the time pro monitoring calls police and police arrive (typically 5–15 minutes), the burglary is over. Self-monitoring with cameras gives you the same evidence and faster personal awareness.
  • Smartphones are now reliable enough. Modern alarm apps work over cellular when WiFi is down. The reliability gap that justified pro monitoring 10 years ago has mostly closed.

The case for professional monitoring (where it still makes sense):

  • You travel frequently and don't always have your phone.
  • You have specific high-value items (jewelry, art) that warrant police-grade response.
  • Your insurance discount specifically requires UL-certified pro-monitoring (some carriers do — check your policy).
  • You want medical-alert backup integrated with your security system.

The homeowners insurance discount

Most homeowners insurance carriers give discounts of 5–20% for installed monitored security systems. The discount tiers:

System levelTypical discount
No system0% (baseline)
Local alarm only (no monitoring, makes noise)2–5%
Self-monitored smart system (Ring, Wyze)5–10%
Professionally monitored DIY system (Ring Alarm Pro, SimpliSafe Pro)10–15%
UL-certified central station monitoring (ADT, Vivint, traditional pro)15–20%
Add fire monitoring + smoke + CO + water+2–5% additional

For a $1,500/year homeowners policy, a 15% discount = $225/year — typically pays for the security system within 1–3 years.

To claim the discount:

  1. Install your system.
  2. Request a "certificate of monitoring" from your monitoring provider — most issue these on request via the customer portal.
  3. Submit the certificate to your homeowners insurance carrier with a discount application.
  4. The discount applies to your next renewal — sometimes pro-rated to the current period.

Renters insurance discounts are smaller (2–5%) but still real. Same process.

Free or near-free security options

For households with very limited budgets:

  • Smartphone-as-camera apps (Alfred, Manything, Presence) — turn an old phone into a security camera. Free.
  • Wyze v3 cameras — $25–$40 each. App is free. Stack 3–4 around the house for full coverage at $100–$160 total.
  • Ring Doorbell + neighborhood watch — many neighborhoods now have shared Ring/Nextdoor watch groups that effectively crowdsource awareness.
  • Smart lights (cheap on Amazon) on timer — visible occupancy is the cheapest theft deterrent.
  • Window/door alarms from hardware stores — $5–$15 each, battery-powered, loud. Won't call police but creates noise that disrupts most burglars.

Medical alert savings

For seniors and disability households, medical alert systems (PERS — Personal Emergency Response Systems) are often partially or fully covered by:

  • Medicaid Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers — covers PERS in 30+ states for qualifying participants. Apply through your state Medicaid agency.
  • VA Aid & Attendance — provides cash benefit for qualifying veterans that can pay for medical alert systems.
  • VA Geriatrics Extended Care — provides medical alert systems directly for qualifying veterans.
  • State-specific aging programs — many states' Department of Aging programs cover or subsidize medical alerts.
  • Long-term care insurance — most policies cover medical alerts.

FAQ

Is self-monitoring really enough?

For most households, yes. Modern smartphone notifications + cellular backup are reliable. The main reasons to pay for professional monitoring: frequent travel, specific high-value items, or an insurance discount that explicitly requires UL-certified central station monitoring.

Will I lose my discount if I drop pro monitoring?

Often yes. The largest discount tiers (15–20%) require UL-certified pro monitoring. Self-monitored systems still get smaller discounts (5–10%). Run the math — sometimes paying $20/month pro monitoring saves $50/month insurance discount.

Are smart-home/Apple-HomeKit/Google-Home setups good security?

Yes for awareness; no for response. They lack the alarm-sensor + cellular-backup integration of dedicated security systems. Best as a supplement (smart locks, door sensors, lights) layered on top of a dedicated alarm.

What about renters?

DIY systems are renter-friendly — adhesive mount, no drilling, takes 30 minutes to install and uninstall. Renters insurance discounts (2–5%) are smaller than homeowners but still real.

How does Medicaid PERS coverage work?

Most states cover PERS through Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) Medicaid waivers. The waiver pays for in-home services that allow elderly/disabled residents to stay home rather than move to a facility. PERS is typically included as a covered service. Apply through your state Medicaid agency.

Are there security systems for $0/month genuinely?

Yes — Wyze and self-monitored Ring/eufy/Arlo. You pay for equipment ($100–$400) but no recurring fees. Functionality is mostly the same as paid tiers; you give up advanced features like cloud video storage and pro monitoring.
