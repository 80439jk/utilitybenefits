Quick Answer · Speakable schema

The average U.S. household pays about $144/month for cell phone service for 2 lines on a major carrier. Switching to an MVNO (mobile virtual network operator) like Mint Mobile, Visible, or US Mobile typically cuts that 50–60% with the same coverage. If your household qualifies for Lifeline (income at or below 135% FPL, or receiving SNAP/Medicaid/SSI), you can get a free phone and free monthly service through carriers like SafeLink, Assurance Wireless, or Q Link — this is what most people mean when they search "free government phone." Lifeline is one-or-the-other for phone or internet per household.

The two paths to a lower phone bill

Cell phone is the highest-volume vertical on this site for raw search traffic — "free government phone" alone is one of the most-searched assistance queries in the entire utility benefits space, bigger than any LIHEAP query. That tells you something about how widely the basic facts of the program are misunderstood.

For households that don't qualify for Lifeline, the savings track is unusually rich because the gap between what people pay on Verizon, AT&T, or T-Mobile post-paid plans and what comparable service costs on an MVNO is enormous. A typical 4-line family paying $200/month on Verizon can usually get the same coverage and data on Visible (which uses Verizon's network) for $100/month. Most households leave $1,200/year on the table.

Why most households overpay for cell phone service

Three patterns explain almost every "my phone bill seems high" story:

1. The post-paid premium

Verizon, AT&T, and T-Mobile post-paid plans charge a 50–80% premium over functionally equivalent service on their own MVNO subsidiaries (Verizon owns Visible and Total Wireless; AT&T owns Cricket; T-Mobile owns Metro and operates Mint Mobile). The premium pays for retail stores, customer service, device subsidies, and the brand experience. If you don't use those things, you're paying for them anyway.

2. Subsidized devices that aren't really subsidized

"Free iPhone with new line" deals usually require a 36-month contract on a specific plan tier where the monthly cost difference is roughly the device price. The savings show up only if you would have bought that exact plan at retail anyway. For most households, buying an unlocked phone outright (or used) and going to an MVNO is $300–$500/year cheaper.

3. Auto-renewing perks

"Disney+ on us" / "Apple Music included" / streaming bundles raise plan prices by $10–$15/month. If you don't use the perk, switching to a plan tier without it saves directly. Most major carriers don't proactively let you opt out of perks — you have to ask.

The MVNO landscape

An MVNO uses one of the three major networks (Verizon, AT&T, or T-Mobile) but at a fraction of the price. Coverage is identical to the underlying network. Customer service is usually online or by chat, not in-store.

Senior cell phone discounts

Cell phone is one of the verticals where senior-specific options actually exist and matter. The big ones:

  • T-Mobile Essentials 55+ — $40/month for 1 line, $55/month for 2 lines (one person 55+ in household)
  • Verizon 55+ Unlimited — Florida-only direct, but available nationally through partner programs
  • Consumer Cellular — AARP-partnered, no contract, plans starting around $20/month with simplified billing
  • Lively (formerly Jitterbug) — designed for seniors who want simplified phones with health features; plans from $20/month

If you're 60+ and on a major carrier post-paid plan, switching to Consumer Cellular or T-Mobile 55+ typically saves $30–$50/month with no loss of service.

Lifeline (free government phone)

For households earning under 135% of the federal poverty level — or receiving SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, Veterans Pension, or Federal Public Housing — the federal Lifeline program covers a free phone and free monthly service through MVNO carriers. The phone is usually a basic Android smartphone; the service is typically unlimited talk and text plus 4–25 GB of data per month depending on the carrier.

The honest-answer page on this lives at "Free government phone" — what it is and how to get one. The single most useful page for anyone whose household qualifies.

Phone or internet — the Lifeline decision

One Lifeline benefit per household. You apply it to phone OR internet, not both. Most households we hear from default to phone (it manifests as a "free phone + free service" through SafeLink or Assurance, which feels more tangible than a $9.25/month discount on home internet). But the right answer depends on what you actually need.

Detailed decision guidance is at the phone-vs-internet decision page. The short version: if you need a working phone and don't have one, apply Lifeline to phone and get internet through an ISP-specific low-cost program (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Assist) which is independent of Lifeline and stackable.

State-specific notes

Cell phone service is much less state-regulated than electricity — the federal carriers and MVNOs serve all states with similar offerings. Two states matter for state-level benefit stacking:

  • California — California LifeLine adds state subsidies on top of federal Lifeline. The most generous combined benefit in the country for low-income wireless service.
  • Texas — Texas Lifeline runs alongside federal; some provider variations.

Frequently asked questions

Is "free government phone" real?

Yes. The technical name is Lifeline, a federal FCC program that subsidizes phone or internet service for low-income households. Carriers like SafeLink, Assurance Wireless, and Q Link participate by providing a free phone plus free monthly service to qualifying households. The full breakdown is on our free government phone guide.

How much can switching to an MVNO actually save?

Typical savings are $50–$80 per month for a 2-line family, $100–$150 for a 4-line family. The math: most households pay $80–$120 per line on post-paid Verizon/AT&T/T-Mobile plans; comparable MVNO service is $25–$45 per line for unlimited talk/text/data. Coverage is identical because MVNOs use the same towers.

Do MVNOs work with iPhones?

Yes. Any unlocked iPhone (or a phone bought from a major carrier whose installments you've paid off) works with any MVNO on the underlying network. Bringing your own phone to an MVNO is the norm — most don't even offer device financing.

Can I get Lifeline for both phone and internet?

No. One Lifeline benefit per household, applied to phone OR internet. The workaround: apply Lifeline to phone, then apply for an ISP-specific low-cost internet program (Internet Essentials, AT&T Access, Spectrum Assist) which is independent of Lifeline. Most households can stack these.

What if I have bad credit?

MVNOs don't run credit checks for their prepaid plans, which is most of what they sell. Switching to an MVNO is the simplest workaround for credit issues. For Lifeline, credit isn't a factor — eligibility is based on household income or program participation, not credit history.

How do I keep my phone number when switching?

Number portability is federally required. To keep your number, sign up with the new carrier first and request a transfer using your current account number and PIN. Don't cancel your old service before the transfer completes — once cancelled, the number is harder to recover.
