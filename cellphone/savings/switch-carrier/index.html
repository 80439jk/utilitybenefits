How to Switch Cell Phone Carriers

15-minute walkthrough for switching to an MVNO or new carrier. Port your number, keep your phone, no service interruption.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Telecom & broadband research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Switching takes ~15 minutes online. You need: your current account number, account PIN, and the phone number you want to keep. The new carrier handles the port; don't cancel your old service first. Ports complete in 1–4 hours typically. eSIM activation is instant; physical SIM ships in 1–3 days. If your phone is locked, get it unlocked from your current carrier first (federal law requires unlock for paid-off devices).

7-step switch

  1. Pick your new carrier. See Best MVNOs of 2026. Confirm the network covers your home and work addresses on its parent's coverage map.
  2. Verify phone compatibility. Most phones from 2018+ work on every network. Check the new carrier's "BYOD compatibility" checker — enter your phone's IMEI (dial *#06# or look in Settings → About).
  3. Unlock your phone if locked. Federal law requires carriers to unlock paid-off devices on request. Start the unlock 7–14 days before switching to allow processing time.
  4. Get your account info from current carrier. Account number (on the bill) and account PIN (call current carrier, ask for "transfer PIN" or "account PIN"). Both required to port.
  5. Sign up with new carrier. Most use eSIM activation now — pick a plan, enter the account info, scan a QR code. Service typically activates within 30 minutes. Physical SIM users get a SIM in 1–3 days.
  6. The port completes. Old service ends, new service begins, your number stays the same. Ports take 1–4 hours typically. Do not cancel your old service yourself — the port handles cancellation.
  7. Verify everything works. Make a test call, send a test text, check data. Confirm voicemail transferred (sometimes you need to set it up fresh on the new carrier).

The number port (what's actually happening)

Number porting is a federally-mandated process where carriers transfer phone numbers between networks. The new carrier initiates the port using your current carrier's account info. Your old service stays active until the port completes — at that exact moment, your number switches networks and your old service ends.

Why the new carrier handles the port (not you):

  • If you cancel old service first, your number goes to a "released" state that can't always be recovered.
  • The new carrier has access to wholesale porting systems that consumers don't.
  • Federal law requires the new carrier to handle the port at no cost to you.

If your phone is locked

Carrier-locked phones can only run on the carrier they're locked to until you unlock them. To check: try a different carrier's SIM in the phone — if it doesn't work, you're locked.

Federal law (the 2014 Unlocking Consumer Choice and Wireless Competition Act) requires carriers to unlock paid-off devices on request, free of charge. Process:

  1. Confirm device is paid off. If you're still on a 36-month subsidized payment plan, you're not eligible to unlock until paid off (or you pay the remaining balance).
  2. Request unlock from current carrier. Each carrier has an online form: Verizon Unlock, AT&T Unlock, T-Mobile Unlock. Submit IMEI.
  3. Wait 1–7 days for unlock confirmation.
  4. Switch carriers normally.

eSIM vs. physical SIM

Most modern phones support eSIM (digital SIM, no physical card). eSIM activation is faster — typically 5–10 minutes from sign-up to working service. Physical SIMs ship in 1–3 days.

eSIM-capable phones (most of these support it):

  • iPhone XS and newer (2018+)
  • Google Pixel 2 and newer
  • Samsung Galaxy S20 and newer
  • Most newer Motorola, OnePlus, Sony devices

U.S. iPhone 14+ are eSIM only — no physical SIM slot. They can only activate via eSIM.

Switching a family plan

Family plan migration is the most complex switching scenario but still doable in 30 minutes:

  1. Decide whether to keep family on one MVNO or split. Some families benefit from mixing — heavy data users on Verizon-network MVNO, light users on T-Mobile-network. Most just go to one MVNO.
  2. Get account info for each line. Account number and PIN for each line being ported. (If you're on a single-account family plan, the PIN is the same.)
  3. Activate new lines on new carrier. Some MVNOs have multi-line family discounts (Visible's Party Pay, US Mobile's family plans). Others let each member sign up individually.
  4. Port the numbers in. Same process as single-line. Each line ports independently.
  5. Final billing on old carrier. The old carrier sends a final pro-rated bill. If you had a multi-line discount, the final bill may not include it — be prepared for a slightly higher final amount.

Watch out for

  • Trade-in / device payment plans. If you owe money on a phone, you'll get a final bill for the remaining balance when you cancel. Plan for this — it can be $400–$1,000+ for a recent flagship.
  • Lost subsidies. Some "free phone" deals require staying with the carrier for the full 36 months — leaving early triggers a payback. Check before switching.
  • Voicemail loss. Voicemail messages don't always transfer. Save anything important before switching.
  • iMessage/RCS lock. If you're switching iPhone → Android, deregister iMessage at apple.com/myaccount/messages first or you'll miss texts from iPhone users for weeks.
  • Family discount loss. If you're on a family plan and only some members switch, the remaining members may lose the family discount.
  • Apple Watch / Wear OS cellular. Verify the new carrier supports your wearable before switching.

FAQ

Will I lose service during the port?

Briefly — typically 30 seconds to 5 minutes during the actual cutover. Calls in progress get dropped. Texts sent during the cutover usually arrive but can be delayed. Plan the switch for a quiet time of day.

How fast is number porting?

Typically 1–4 hours for residential ports. Sometimes 24–48 hours during high-demand periods. Federal rules require ports to complete within 24 hours.

What if my port fails?

Most port failures are wrong account number or PIN. The new carrier will email you. Provide correct info and the port retries automatically. If it keeps failing, call the new carrier's port support line.

Can I switch back if I don't like the new carrier?

Yes — port back the same way. Most MVNOs have no contract; the only friction is repeating the process. If you paid annually (Mint), you don't get a refund.

What about my subsidized iPhone?

If it's paid off, unlock it and bring it to the new MVNO. If you're still in the 36-month payment plan, you owe the remaining balance when you cancel — usually $400–$1,000. Sometimes worth paying off and switching anyway given the MVNO savings.

Does the new carrier need to ship me a SIM?

Only if your phone doesn't support eSIM. Most modern phones do. eSIM activation is instant; physical SIM takes 1–3 days.
