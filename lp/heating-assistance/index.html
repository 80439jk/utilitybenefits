Common questions

Who qualifies for LIHEAP?

Federal eligibility is set at 150% of the federal poverty guidelines (some states go up to 200%). Households receiving SNAP, SSI, or TANF often qualify automatically. Income includes wages, benefits, child support, and most retirement income.

How much can I receive?

The average regular LIHEAP benefit is $400–$700 per household. Crisis benefits — for households facing imminent shut-off or out of fuel — can reach $1,500 or more. The exact amount depends on your state, income, household size, fuel type, and the program component.

When can I apply?

LIHEAP application windows vary by state. Most states open in October or November and run through March or April. Some states accept applications year-round; some open earlier for elderly or disabled applicants. We show your state's exact dates.

Can I get help if I'm already behind on my bill?

Yes. LIHEAP's "crisis component" is specifically for households facing shut-off, out of fuel, or with active disconnect notices. Many states will fast-track these applications and pay your utility directly within 24–48 hours.

What if I rent?

You can still apply. If heat is included in your rent, LIHEAP can pay the landlord directly. If you pay your own heating bill — even on an electric, gas, oil, propane, or wood account — you apply normally and the benefit goes to your utility or fuel supplier.

Is there help besides LIHEAP?

Yes. Most states run a supplemental program funded by the state or utility companies. Many utilities also run their own hardship-grant programs (e.g., "Operation Round Up," "NeighborShare," etc.). And 30+ states have winter shut-off moratoriums that legally prevent disconnection during cold months.
