Minnesota EAP: Heating Help with the Country's Strongest Cold Weather Rule

Minnesota's Energy Assistance Program is administered through DOC and 30 local Service Providers. Plus the strongest blanket cold-weather shutoff protection in the U.S. — Oct 1 through Apr 30, all residential, automatic.

By Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Minnesota's Energy Assistance Program (EAP) is the federal LIHEAP, administered by DOC through 30 local Service Providers (Community Action Agencies and tribal organizations). Income limit: 60% State Median Income. Annual benefits typically $300–$1,400, with up to $1,200 per crisis episode. Minnesota has the strongest cold-weather rule in the U.S. — from October 1 through April 30, residential utilities cannot disconnect any household for non-payment, regardless of income. Protection is automatic if you contact your utility.

Minnesota's blanket Cold Weather Rule

Minnesota's CWR is the strongest in the country: blanket protection from October 1 through April 30 for all residential customers, regardless of income or program enrollment, as long as you contact your utility before disconnection. This 7-month protection window is longer than any other state. After April 30, utilities can resume disconnections — at which point EAP enrollment, crisis funds, and utility hardship programs become important to address arrears.

Minnesota's heating costs are among the highest in the U.S. due to cold winters. EAP benefits are correspondingly larger than warm-weather states. Most rural MN households heat with propane or natural gas; Twin Cities with natural gas; outstate with mixed fuels.

Minnesota eligibility

Minnesota uses 60% of State Median Income — a state-specific threshold that's typically more generous than 150% FPL. Categorical eligibility shortcuts apply: SNAP, SSI, TANF, and (in many states) Medicaid recipients qualify automatically without income paperwork.

How to apply in Minnesota

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or contact your local administering agency.
  2. Gather documentation — see Section 4 below. If categorical (SNAP, SSI, TANF), you can skip income paperwork.
  3. Apply — through your local Community Action Agency, county DSS, or state online portal.
  4. Wait for approval — typically 30–45 days for regular benefits, 18–48 hours for crisis applications.
  5. Benefit paid directly to utility — or fuel vendor for oil/propane households.

Documents you'll need

  • Government-issued photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for all household members
  • Proof of income for the past 30 days, OR proof of categorical participation (SNAP, SSI, TANF letter)
  • Most recent utility bill
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • For crisis: shutoff notice or proof of empty fuel tank

Minnesota-specific FAQ

How does Minnesota's Cold Weather Rule work?

Blanket protection — between October 1 and April 30, residential utilities cannot disconnect any household for non-payment. You must contact your utility to invoke (no enrollment required, just communication). Protection is automatic upon contact.

Where do I apply for EAP?

Through your local Service Provider — find yours at mn.gov/commerce. 30 Service Providers cover all 87 MN counties. Most have online applications.

Does MN EAP cover propane?

Yes. Minnesota EAP covers all heating fuels including propane, oil, wood, and electric. Rural propane households often see larger per-fill benefits.

What is Heat Share?

Salvation Army's Minnesota emergency heating assistance program — partners with most MN utilities. Provides emergency utility assistance often when EAP funds are tapped out.

Can EAP pay for furnace repair?

Yes. Minnesota EAP includes Energy Related Repair (ERR) for furnace, boiler, and heating system repairs/replacements for income-qualified households. Apply through your Service Provider with a contractor's estimate.

What is Reach Out for Warmth?

A donation-funded supplemental program created to provide emergency heating help when state and federal LIHEAP funds are tapped out. Funded by individual donors, foundations, and some utilities.

Can renters qualify?

Yes if you pay your own utility bill. Tenant utilities bundled into rent: regular benefit harder; crisis components may still apply.

How much will my heating bill be in MN winter?

Twin Cities natural gas household: $200-$350/month in deep winter. Outstate propane or oil: $400-$700/month. Northern MN with electric heat: $400-$600/month. EAP plus utility hardship typically covers a meaningful portion for low-income households.
