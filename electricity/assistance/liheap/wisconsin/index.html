Wisconsin WHEAP: Heating Help, Crisis, and Furnace Repair

Wisconsin Home Energy Assistance Program (WHEAP) covers regular heating help, crisis intervention, and furnace/boiler repair or replacement. Plus one of the strongest blanket cold-weather rules in the country.

By Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Wisconsin's federal LIHEAP is the Wisconsin Home Energy Assistance Program (WHEAP), administered by DOA. Three components: regular heating benefit ($300–$700), crisis intervention (up to $700 for emergencies), and furnace/boiler repair or replacement (up to $4,500). Income limit: 60% State Median Income. Wisconsin has one of the strongest cold-weather shutoff rules in the country — blanket protection November 1 through April 15 for all residential customers, automatic, no enrollment required.

Wisconsin's blanket Cold Weather Rule

Wisconsin's Cold Weather Rule (CWR) is one of the strongest in the country: from November 1 through April 15, residential utilities cannot disconnect any household for non-payment, regardless of income or program enrollment. Protection is automatic. After April 15, utilities can resume disconnections — at which point WHEAP enrollment, crisis funds, and utility hardship programs become important to address accumulated arrears.

Wisconsin's furnace/boiler component is also unusually generous: up to $4,500 for heating system repair or replacement for income-qualified households. Apply through your local WHEAP agency with a contractor's estimate.

Wisconsin eligibility

Wisconsin uses 60% of State Median Income — a state-specific threshold that's typically more generous than 150% FPL. Categorical eligibility shortcuts apply: SNAP, SSI, TANF, and (in many states) Medicaid recipients qualify automatically without income paperwork.

How to apply in Wisconsin

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or contact your local administering agency.
  2. Gather documentation — see Section 4 below. If categorical (SNAP, SSI, TANF), you can skip income paperwork.
  3. Apply — through your local Community Action Agency, county DSS, or state online portal.
  4. Wait for approval — typically 30–45 days for regular benefits, 18–48 hours for crisis applications.
  5. Benefit paid directly to utility — or fuel vendor for oil/propane households.

Documents you'll need

  • Government-issued photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for all household members
  • Proof of income for the past 30 days, OR proof of categorical participation (SNAP, SSI, TANF letter)
  • Most recent utility bill
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • For crisis: shutoff notice or proof of empty fuel tank

Wisconsin-specific FAQ

When does WHEAP open?

October 1 for vulnerable households (60+, disabled, kids under 6) and a week or two later for general applications. Heating-season window through May 15. Crisis Intervention available year-round.

How does Wisconsin's Cold Weather Rule work?

Blanket protection — between November 1 and April 15, residential utilities cannot disconnect any household for non-payment regardless of income or program enrollment. Automatic; no enrollment needed.

Can WHEAP pay for a new furnace?

Yes. WHEAP includes a furnace repair/replacement component for income-qualified households — up to $4,500 per system. Apply through your local WHEAP agency with a contractor's estimate.

Where do I apply?

Through your local WHEAP agency — find yours at homeenergyplus.wi.gov. Most counties have a designated agency.

Does WHEAP cover oil and propane?

Yes. WHEAP covers all heating fuels including propane, oil, wood, and electric. Rural propane and oil households often see larger per-fill benefits.

What is Focus on Energy?

Wisconsin's statewide energy efficiency program — free or subsidized efficiency upgrades (LED bulbs, weatherization, smart thermostats) for income-qualified households. Stacks with WAP.

Can renters qualify in Wisconsin?

Yes if you pay your own utility bill. Tenant utilities bundled into rent: regular WHEAP harder; emergency components may still apply.

What happens after April 15?

Utilities can resume disconnections for non-payment. By then, WHEAP enrollment, crisis funds, and utility hardship programs should have addressed any accumulated arrears. Engage your utility before April 15 to set up payment plans if needed.
