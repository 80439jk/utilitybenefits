Delaware LIHEAP: Heating, Cooling, and Crisis Help Statewide

MR
Reviewed by Marcus Reid Energy Policy Analyst · former NARUC staff
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Delaware's federal LIHEAP is administered by DHSS through local State Service Centers. Income limit: 200% FPL — among the more generous in the country. Three components: regular heating benefit ($300–$1,500), crisis intervention (up to $1,500 for shutoffs, deliveries, broken equipment), and a summer cooling benefit (up to $400 for AC units, repairs, or bill help). Two main utilities — Delmarva Power and Chesapeake Utilities — run additional hardship programs that stack.

Why Delaware's 200% FPL limit matters

Delaware sets its LIHEAP income threshold at 200% of the federal poverty level — well above the federal default of 150%. For a family of four, that's about $62,400/year — meaningfully expanding the pool of eligible households compared to neighboring states. If you've been told you make too much for LIHEAP elsewhere, run the numbers on Delaware's threshold first.

Delaware also runs a meaningful summer cooling benefit separate from heating — recognizing that mid-Atlantic summers are humid and dangerous for elderly and medically vulnerable residents. The cooling benefit can pay for an AC unit, AC repair, or summer bills.

Delaware eligibility

Delaware uses 200% of the federal poverty level. Categorical eligibility shortcuts apply: SNAP, SSI, TANF, and (in many states) Medicaid recipients qualify automatically without income paperwork.

How to apply in Delaware

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or contact your local administering agency.
  2. Gather documentation — see Section 4 below. If categorical (SNAP, SSI, TANF), you can skip income paperwork.
  3. Apply — through your local Community Action Agency, county DSS, or state online portal.
  4. Wait for approval — typically 30–45 days for regular benefits, 18–48 hours for crisis applications.
  5. Benefit paid directly to utility — or fuel vendor for oil/propane households.

Documents you'll need

  • Government-issued photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for all household members
  • Proof of income for the past 30 days, OR proof of categorical participation (SNAP, SSI, TANF letter)
  • Most recent utility bill
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • For crisis: shutoff notice or proof of empty fuel tank

Delaware-specific FAQ

What's Delaware's LIHEAP income limit?

200% of the federal poverty level — about $62,400/year for a household of four. That's meaningfully more generous than the federal default of 150% FPL. A lot of households who don't qualify in PA or NJ do qualify in DE.

Where do I apply?

Through your local DHSS Division of State Service Centers office (Wilmington, Newark, Dover, Georgetown, etc.). Online and phone applications also available.

When does Delaware LIHEAP open?

Heating-season window: November 1 through March 31. Crisis Intervention available year-round. Summer cooling: June through September.

Does Delaware have a winter moratorium?

Not a strict statutory moratorium, but Delaware PSC requires utilities to offer payment plans and delay disconnections for income-qualified households between November 15 and March 31.

Does LIHEAP cover propane and oil?

Yes. Delaware LIHEAP covers all heating fuels including natural gas, electric, propane, fuel oil, kerosene, and wood. Sussex County rural propane households often see larger per-fill benefits.

What is the Good Neighbor Energy Fund?

Statewide nonprofit utility assistance fund administered by Catholic Charities Delaware. Funded by donations and utility customer round-ups. Can pay arrears that LIHEAP doesn't cover.

Can renters qualify in Delaware?

Yes if you pay your own utility bill. Tenant utilities bundled into rent: regular benefit harder; crisis components may still apply.

What's the summer cooling benefit?

Up to $400 for income-qualified DE households for summer AC bills, AC unit purchase/repair, or fan purchase. Especially important for elderly and medically vulnerable residents during heat waves.
