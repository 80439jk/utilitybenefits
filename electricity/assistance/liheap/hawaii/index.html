Hawaii LIHEAP: Energy Credit and Emergency Crisis Intervention

Hawaii's federal LIHEAP runs as an Energy Credit (one-time annual benefit) plus Emergency Crisis Intervention. Hawaii has the highest electricity costs in the country — LIHEAP benefits matter even more here.

By Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Hawaii's federal LIHEAP runs differently from most states: a brief annual open enrollment window (typically 2 weeks in June or July) for the Energy Credit ($200–$800 one-time benefit paid to your utility), plus year-round Emergency Crisis Intervention (up to $1,500 for shutoffs and emergencies). Income limit: 150% FPL. Hawaii has the highest residential electricity rates in the country — LIHEAP enrollment combined with HECO/MECO/HELCO/KIUC bill assistance and rooftop solar programs is often the difference between affordability and crisis.

Why Hawaii LIHEAP applications close so fast

Hawaii's residential electric rates run $0.30–$0.45/kWh — roughly 2–3× the U.S. average. A typical 700 kWh-per-month household pays $250–$315 just for electricity. Add gas where applicable and water rates among the country's highest, and utility costs consume a much larger share of household income than on the mainland.

Hawaii's federal LIHEAP allocation is limited and the state runs the Energy Credit on a brief open enrollment window — typically two weeks in June or July. Once the application window closes, the only available LIHEAP component is Emergency Crisis Intervention (year-round, but requires a verified shutoff or emergency). Plan ahead: watch DHS announcements and apply on the first day enrollment opens.

Hawaii eligibility

Hawaii uses 150% of the federal poverty level. Categorical eligibility shortcuts apply: SNAP, SSI, TANF, and (in many states) Medicaid recipients qualify automatically without income paperwork.

How to apply in Hawaii

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or contact your local administering agency.
  2. Gather documentation — see Section 4 below. If categorical (SNAP, SSI, TANF), you can skip income paperwork.
  3. Apply — through your local Community Action Agency, county DSS, or state online portal.
  4. Wait for approval — typically 30–45 days for regular benefits, 18–48 hours for crisis applications.
  5. Benefit paid directly to utility — or fuel vendor for oil/propane households.

Documents you'll need

  • Government-issued photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for all household members
  • Proof of income for the past 30 days, OR proof of categorical participation (SNAP, SSI, TANF letter)
  • Most recent utility bill
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • For crisis: shutoff notice or proof of empty fuel tank

Hawaii-specific FAQ

Why is Hawaii's LIHEAP enrollment so brief?

Hawaii's federal LIHEAP allocation is small relative to demand. DHS runs a brief Energy Credit enrollment window (typically 2 weeks in June or July) to allocate funds fairly. Once closed, only Emergency Crisis Intervention is available — year-round but requires a verified shutoff or emergency.

When does Hawaii's Energy Credit application open?

Typically late June or early July. DHS announces the exact dates each year. Apply on day one — funding is limited and applications often close within 2 weeks.

Where do I apply?

Online during the open window at humanservices.hawaii.gov, in person at DHS offices, or through community partner agencies in each county.

What's the income limit?

150% of the federal poverty level — about $46,800/year for a family of four. Categorical eligibility (SNAP, TANF, SSI) shortcuts apply.

Does LIHEAP cover gas in Hawaii?

Yes. Hawaii LIHEAP covers electric, propane, and natural gas. Most HI households use electric for cooking and heating; rural Big Island households often use propane.

Does Hawaii have a shutoff moratorium?

No statewide moratorium. HECO and other utilities encourage payment arrangements but can disconnect for non-payment year-round. LIHEAP enrollment, KIUC member assistance, or hardship programs are the practical protections.

Can renters qualify in Hawaii?

Yes if you pay your own utility bill. Tenant utilities bundled into rent: regular Energy Credit harder; Crisis Intervention may still apply for verified emergencies.

What about community solar?

HECO and KIUC run community solar programs that allow income-qualified customers to enroll without rooftop installation — provides bill credits at discounted rates. Stacks with LIHEAP.
