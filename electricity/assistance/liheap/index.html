LIHEAP: Complete Guide to Energy Bill Assistance

The Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP) is the federal backbone for utility bill help — heating, cooling, and energy crisis intervention in every state.

SKBy Sara Khan, Senior Editor · Reviewed by Marcus Reid, Energy Policy Analyst · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

LIHEAP (Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program) is a federal program that helps eligible low-income households pay heating and cooling bills and provides emergency energy crisis assistance. Most states allow households earning up to 150% of the federal poverty level — or 60% of state median income — to qualify, with benefits typically ranging from $400 to $1,500 per year. Apply through your state's LIHEAP office.

1. What is LIHEAP?

LIHEAP is a federal block-grant program established in 1981, funded by the U.S. Department of Health & Human Services and administered by each state. The program reaches roughly 5–6 million households per year nationwide. Federal money flows to states; states decide eligibility specifics, benefit amounts, and how to deliver the help (direct vendor payments, debit cards, or checks).

2. What LIHEAP covers

  • Heating assistance — primary use case in cold-weather states. Pays for natural gas, electric heat, oil, propane, wood, etc.
  • Cooling assistance — increasingly important in Southern and Southwestern states; pays toward summer electric bills.
  • Crisis intervention — emergency funds when a household faces shutoff or no heat/cooling. Available year-round in most states.
  • Weatherization referrals — connects qualifying households to the federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP).
  • Energy-related home repairs — broken furnaces, AC units, water heaters in some states.

3. Eligibility (income limits)

The federal baseline is 150% of the federal poverty level (FPL) or 60% of state median income (SMI) — whichever is higher. States can also use categorical eligibility: if you receive SNAP, SSI, TANF, or in some states Medicaid, you automatically qualify.

4. How much LIHEAP pays

Regular benefits typically range from $400 to $1,500 per year, depending on:

  • Household income (lower income = larger benefit, in tiers)
  • Household size
  • Heating fuel type (oil and propane often get higher benefits than electric)
  • Climate zone (cold-weather states pay more in heating; hot-weather states more in cooling)
  • Whether anyone in the household is "vulnerable" — elderly, disabled, or under age 6

Crisis benefits are usually paid on top of regular benefits and can range from $200 to $800+ depending on the state and the urgency.

5. How to apply (HowTo schema)

  1. Confirm your state's program name — federal name is LIHEAP, but it's called CEAP in Texas, HEAP in New York, MEAP in Michigan, etc. See Texas (CEAP) example →
  2. Check the application window — most states open heating-season applications October–November. Crisis applications are year-round.
  3. Gather your documents — see Section 6 below.
  4. Apply online, by mail, or in person — through your state's energy assistance office or local Community Action Agency.
  5. Submit and wait for confirmation — processing times range from a few days (crisis) to 30–45 days (regular).
  6. Receive your benefit — usually paid directly to your utility company or fuel vendor.
  7. Reapply each year — LIHEAP is not automatic; benefits don't carry over.
  8. Stack with state and utility programs — once LIHEAP is approved, look at state programs (Section 11 below) and utility hardship programs.

6. Documents you'll need

  • Photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for everyone in the household
  • Proof of income for the last 30 days (pay stubs, benefits letters, etc.)
  • Recent utility bill (or fuel delivery receipt for oil/propane)
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • If applying for crisis: shutoff notice or empty fuel tank documentation

7. When to apply

Heating season: Most cold-weather states open applications in October or November. Funds run out — apply as early as possible.

Cooling season: Texas, Florida, Arizona, and other Southern states often run separate cooling-season windows starting in spring.

Crisis assistance: Available year-round in most states. If you have a shutoff notice or no heat/cooling, apply immediately — most states process crisis applications within 18–48 hours.

9. Related programs and alternatives

  • Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) — federal program offering free home efficiency improvements; LIHEAP applicants are commonly referred.
  • PIPP / PIPP Plus — Ohio, PA, IL, NJ. Caps your bill at a percentage of household income.
  • Utility hardship programs — Reliant CARE, TXU Energy Aid, Dollar Energy Fund, Salvation Army REACH.
  • 211 — dial 211 from anywhere in the U.S. for local emergency utility assistance.
  • Senior, veteran, and disability programs — see the assistance hub.

10. What if I don't qualify?

"Not qualified" almost never means "no help available." Several common scenarios:

  • Income just above LIHEAP threshold: state programs (CARE, USF, CAP rates) often have higher cutoffs.
  • Income too high for assistance: the savings track — switching providers, efficiency rebates — usually saves more than LIHEAP would have paid.
  • Senior or disabled: separate programs based on age or disability status, often easier to qualify for.
  • Veteran: VA-related and state veteran utility funds.

11. Frequently asked questions

Is LIHEAP a one-time payment or ongoing?

LIHEAP is paid annually. You apply each year — benefits don't roll over. Most states pay it as a single lump-sum credit to your utility account once approved.

Can I get LIHEAP if I'm renting?

Yes, as long as you pay your own utility bill. If utilities are included in your rent, the regular LIHEAP benefit is harder to receive — but the crisis component and indirect assistance may still be available.

Does LIHEAP cover gas, propane, or oil heat?

Yes. LIHEAP is fuel-agnostic. In fact, oil and propane benefits are often higher than electric or natural gas benefits because the unit costs are higher.

What if utilities are included in my rent?

Some states allow "indirect" LIHEAP for renters whose landlords pay utilities. Rules vary widely. The eligibility check or a specialist can confirm for your state.

Can I receive LIHEAP and SNAP together?

Yes. SNAP enrollment also gives you categorical eligibility for LIHEAP in most states, meaning you skip the income paperwork.

How long does processing take?

Regular LIHEAP applications take 30–45 days in most states. Crisis applications are processed within 18–48 hours when a shutoff is imminent.

Can I reapply every year?

Yes — and you should. Eligibility doesn't carry over. Apply in October–November when most states open the heating-season window.

Does LIHEAP affect my taxes?

No. LIHEAP benefits are not considered taxable income.

What happens if I'm denied?

Every state has an appeals process — typically 30 days from the denial letter. Most denials are documentation issues that can be cured. Talk to a specialist if you've been denied.

Is there emergency LIHEAP for shutoffs?

Yes — LIHEAP's Crisis component is funded year-round in most states for shutoff threats. Apply immediately if you have a shutoff notice. Many utilities will pause disconnection while a crisis application is pending.

Does LIHEAP cover deposits or reconnection fees?

In some states, yes. Crisis funds in particular are flexible. State rules vary.

How do undocumented household members affect eligibility?

The applicant must have a Social Security number. Other household members' status generally does not affect eligibility, though some states ask for SSNs for everyone in the household for income verification.
