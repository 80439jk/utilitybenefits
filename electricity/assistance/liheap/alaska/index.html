Alaska Heating Assistance: Federal HAP, State AKHAP, and Power Cost Equalization

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Energy & utilities research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Alaska runs two heating programs in parallel: federal HAP (Heating Assistance Program — Alaska's LIHEAP) at 150% FPL, and the state-funded AKHAP (Alaska Heating Assistance Program) for households between 150–225% FPL who don't qualify for HAP. Annual benefits range $200–$1,800, with much higher per-household amounts than most states because of Alaska's heating costs. Rural communities also benefit from Power Cost Equalization (PCE) — a state subsidy that caps residential electric rates in 192 communities served by small utilities.

Why Alaska's heating math is different

Most Alaska households heat with fuel oil, propane, or wood — not natural gas. Anchorage residents have natural gas, but Fairbanks, the Bush, and most of southeast Alaska burn fuel oil at $4–$8 per gallon. A 250-gallon tank fill can run $1,000–$2,000. That's why Alaska HAP/AKHAP benefits are dramatically higher than a Lower-48 state like Texas — the federal allocation formula accounts for heating degree days and fuel costs.

Beyond heating, the Power Cost Equalization (PCE) program is unique to Alaska. PCE subsidizes the per-kWh cost of residential electricity in 192 rural communities where diesel-generated power costs $0.50–$1.00/kWh. The subsidy applies to the first 750 kWh per month — automatically, no application needed. Just live in a PCE-eligible community.

How to apply in Alaska

  1. Confirm eligibility — use this site's eligibility check or contact your local administering agency.
  2. Gather documentation — see Section 4 below. If categorical (SNAP, SSI, TANF), you can skip income paperwork.
  3. Apply — through your local Community Action Agency, county DSS, or state online portal.
  4. Wait for approval — typically 30–45 days for regular benefits, 18–48 hours for crisis applications.
  5. Benefit paid directly to utility — or fuel vendor for oil/propane households.

Documents you'll need

  • Government-issued photo ID for the head of household
  • Social Security cards (or numbers) for all household members
  • Proof of income for the past 30 days, OR proof of categorical participation (SNAP, SSI, TANF letter)
  • Most recent utility bill
  • Lease or mortgage statement
  • For crisis: shutoff notice or proof of empty fuel tank

Alaska-specific FAQ

What's the difference between HAP and AKHAP?

HAP is Alaska's federal LIHEAP — 150% FPL income limit, federally funded. AKHAP is the state-funded sibling program for households 150–225% FPL who don't qualify for HAP. Both administered by Alaska DPA through one application.

Does Alaska have a winter shutoff moratorium?

No statutory moratorium. The Regulatory Commission of Alaska (RCA) encourages utilities to offer payment plans and avoid winter shutoffs but doesn't prohibit them. HAP enrollment is the practical protection.

How does Power Cost Equalization (PCE) work?

PCE subsidizes residential electric rates in 192 Alaska communities where diesel-generated power is expensive. The subsidy applies to the first 750 kWh per month and shows up automatically on your bill. No application needed — just live in a PCE community.

Where do I apply for HAP?

Online through Alaska DPA at health.alaska.gov/dpa, by mail, or in person at a local DPA office. The same application covers both HAP and AKHAP.

Does HAP cover wood heat?

Yes. HAP covers all heating fuels including fuel oil, propane, natural gas, electric, wood, and pellets. Wood households often receive a flat seasonal benefit since cord pricing varies.

Can renters qualify in Alaska?

Yes if you pay your own heating fuel. Tenant utilities bundled into rent: the regular benefit is harder to access, but HAP can sometimes pay rent directly when heat is included.

What is the Senior Benefits Payment Program?

Cash payment ($76–$250/month) to qualifying Alaska seniors based on income — administered by DPA. Many seniors stack Senior Benefits with HAP to cover heating costs.

How big are Alaska HAP benefits compared to other states?

Much larger. Alaska's per-household average benefit is among the highest in the country because the federal funding formula weights heating degree days and fuel costs. A typical Fairbanks oil-heat household can receive $1,200–$1,800.
