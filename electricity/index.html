Quick answer

The average U.S. household pays about $137/month for electricity, but bills vary 3–4× by state and season. If you live in one of the 14 deregulated states, switching providers can save 10–30%. If your household earns under 150% of the federal poverty level, LIHEAP and state programs can pay $400–$1,500/year toward your bill. Most households qualify for one or the other — many qualify for both.

The two paths to a lower electric bill

Most articles about saving on electricity assume you live in a deregulated market — meaning you can shop for a retail electric provider the way you shop for car insurance. That's true for about a third of U.S. households, mostly in Texas, the Mid-Atlantic, and New England. The other two-thirds live in regulated states with one local utility and no shopping option.

For both groups, the assistance side of the ledger matters more than the savings side. The federal LIHEAP program alone reaches 5–6 million households a year. State programs like CARE in California, PIPP Plus in Ohio, and USF in New Jersey often pay more than LIHEAP and have higher income cutoffs. A household at 175% of the federal poverty level — too high for LIHEAP — may still qualify for several state and utility-specific programs.

Why your electric bill went up (the short version)

If your bill jumped by more than 20% in the last six months, one of three things is usually responsible: your plan rolled off a fixed-rate contract onto a variable rate, your utility's fuel adjustment or transmission charges increased, or your household usage changed (new appliance, new resident, weather extremes).

The fix depends on which one. For a contract roll-off in a deregulated state, switching to a new fixed plan is usually a 10-minute fix that locks in 12–24 months of price stability. For utility rate hikes (a regulated-state issue), look at time-of-use plans if your utility offers them, or assistance if you qualify. For usage changes, an energy audit often pays for itself within a year.

Are you in a deregulated state?

Switching providers only works in states where the legislature unbundled the local utility from the energy supplier. As of 2026, that includes:

  • Texas — most competitive market in the country (most metros)
  • Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, New York, New Jersey — Mid-Atlantic deregulation
  • Massachusetts, Connecticut, Maryland, Delaware, DC, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island

Outside these states, your local utility (PG&E, FPL, Duke, etc.) is your only option for electricity supply. The savings track is mostly about efficiency rebates and right-sizing usage. The assistance track works the same way everywhere.

The assistance landscape

Federal LIHEAP is the backbone — it funds heating and cooling assistance and energy crisis intervention in every state, with benefits of $400–$1,500 per year for qualifying households. Income limits are 150% of the federal poverty level or 60% of state median income, whichever is higher.

Layered on top of LIHEAP, every state has its own programs — and in many states, the state program pays more than LIHEAP and has more flexible eligibility. A few examples:

Frequently asked questions

How much can switching electricity providers actually save me?

In deregulated markets, the difference between the cheapest and most expensive 12-month fixed plans is usually 4–8¢ per kWh. For a typical 1,000 kWh household, that's $40–$80 per month, or $480–$960 per year. The savings are largest when you're rolling off a fixed-rate plan that's expired (variable-rate "month-to-month" tends to be priced as a customer-retention penalty).

Do I have to be unemployed to qualify for LIHEAP?

No. LIHEAP eligibility is based on household income relative to federal poverty level or state median income — not employment status. Many recipients work full-time. A 4-person household earning up to about $46,800 is typically eligible (varies by state and year).

Can I get LIHEAP if I'm renting?

Yes, as long as you pay your own utility bill. Even if utilities are included in your rent, you may still qualify for the energy crisis component or for indirect assistance — though the regular benefit is harder to receive. The eligibility check on this site walks through the renter scenarios.

What if I'm facing a shutoff right now?

Call us at 1-833-555-0143 immediately, or use the calculator and select "shutoff notice" — both route you to an emergency specialist. Most states have crisis LIHEAP funds available year-round, and many utilities have hardship programs that can prevent disconnection within 24–48 hours.

Are state programs better than LIHEAP?

Sometimes. CARE in California, PIPP Plus in Ohio, and USF in New Jersey often pay more annually than LIHEAP and have higher income cutoffs. Most households can stack federal and state programs, which is why we recommend the eligibility check rather than applying to one program in isolation.
