How to Switch Electricity Providers: 10-Minute Guide

A walkthrough that works in any of the 16 deregulated states. State-specific shopping sites, what to compare, and the most common mistakes to avoid.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Switching electric providers in a deregulated state takes about 10 minutes. You need a recent utility bill (for your account number and ESI ID/POD), the rate you want to switch to, and a few minutes to fill out an enrollment form. There's no service interruption — same wires, same meter, just a different name on the supply line of your bill. The new provider notifies your old provider for you. The switch takes effect at your next meter read, typically 1–6 weeks later.

The 7-step switch

  1. Confirm you can switch. Use the state list below. If you're in a regulated state (CA, FL, most of the South, Plains, and West), you can't switch suppliers — your savings track is different. See the regulated-state guide →
  2. Pull up a recent utility bill. You need: your account number, your ESI ID (Texas) or POD ID (PA, IL) or service address. Most states require this to confirm your service location.
  3. Find your state's apples-to-apples site. These are run by Public Utility Commissions and are unbiased. They show every plan available to your address with the same disclosure format. See the table below.
  4. Filter by plan length and rate type. Start with 12-month or 24-month fixed-rate plans. Skip variable, month-to-month, and "introductory" plans unless you have a specific reason.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label (or PA/IL/OH equivalent). Every legitimate plan comes with a one-page disclosure showing: rate per kWh at 500/1000/2000 kWh tiers, base charge, early termination fee, and contract length. Compare on the kWh tier closest to your actual usage.
  6. Enroll. Either through the state site (which redirects to the provider) or directly on the provider's website. Have payment info ready (most plans require auto-pay or accept ACH).
  7. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract expiration. This is the single most important step. Almost every fixed-rate plan rolls off to a punitive variable rate at expiration — the calendar reminder lets you shop again before that happens.

What to compare beyond the headline rate

The cheapest advertised rate often isn't the cheapest plan. Five things to compare on the Electricity Facts Label:

  • Rate at your usage tier. Most labels show rates at 500 kWh, 1,000 kWh, and 2,000 kWh of monthly usage. The advertised "as low as 8.9¢/kWh" usually only applies at one tier. Look at your last 12 months of bills, calculate your average monthly kWh, and pick the closest tier.
  • Monthly base charge. Some plans have a $5–$10 fixed monthly charge in addition to the per-kWh rate. Low-usage households end up paying more for "low-rate" plans because of this.
  • Bill credits at usage thresholds. A common Texas pattern: "free nights and weekends" or "$50 bill credit at 1,000+ kWh." These can be great or terrible depending on your usage. Run the numbers.
  • Contract length and early termination fee (ETF). 12-month and 24-month plans usually have $150–$250 ETFs. Make sure you can stay through the contract — moving doesn't always exempt you from the ETF.
  • What happens after the contract ends. Almost every plan rolls to a variable "default" or "month-to-month" rate. Check what that rate has historically been. The variable-rate roll-off is where most consumers get hurt.

Biggest mistakes

  • Shopping on the wrong tier. If you use 700 kWh/month, a plan advertising "8.9¢/kWh at 1,000 kWh" might actually charge you 14¢/kWh. Use the right tier.
  • Falling for variable-rate teaser plans. "1¢/kWh for 60 days" sounds great. After day 61, the rate often jumps to 18–25¢/kWh. Why variable rates are usually a trap →
  • Not setting the renewal reminder. Your provider is required to send notice before contract end, but most consumers miss it. The auto-rollover is where customers who've been "saving 25%" suddenly start paying 40% above market.
  • Door-to-door enrollment. Most door-to-door electric salespeople pitch high-margin plans the company can't sell competitively online. Several states (NY, IL, MD) tightened the rules in 2024, but the practice persists. Don't sign at the door.
  • Phone enrollment from cold calls. Same problem. If you didn't initiate the call, hang up.
  • Using a non-PUC comparison site without comparing to the PUC site. Private comparison sites earn commissions from providers and may push higher-margin plans. Always cross-check with the state PUC site.

How long does the switch actually take?

Three timelines to be aware of:

  1. Enrollment: 5–15 minutes online. The new provider runs a soft credit check (won't affect your score in most cases) and confirms enrollment.
  2. Switch effective date: Tied to your next utility meter read, which is set by your billing cycle. In most states the switch happens within 1–6 weeks. You can request expedited switching in some states (TX) for an emergency rate scenario.
  3. First bill from new provider: 1–2 billing cycles after the switch effective date. Your final bill from the old provider arrives within the same window. There's no "gap" or double-billing — just a clean handoff at the meter read.

If you rent

If your electric account is in your name, you can switch the same way an owner can. Landlords have no say.

If utilities are bundled into your rent, you can't switch — your landlord controls the supplier. You can encourage them to shop (point them at the same state site), but you can't enroll on their behalf.

When NOT to switch

  • You're behind on your bill. Most providers won't enroll customers in active arrears. Address the arrears first via your utility's hardship program, LIHEAP, or a payment plan — then shop. Assistance hub →
  • You're in the first 6 months of a fixed-rate plan you just signed. Early termination fees usually wipe out short-term savings. Wait until the last 60 days of your contract.
  • You qualify for a state assistance program with rate-discount component. CARE in California, PIPP Plus in Ohio, USF in NJ, CAP rates in PA — these can be larger savings than switching providers. Run the assistance calculation first.
  • Your current plan is a custom corporate or municipal aggregation plan. Some employers and municipalities have negotiated better rates than retail. Check your current plan documents before assuming you can do better.

FAQ

Will my utility know I switched?

Yes. The new provider notifies your utility automatically — you don't need to call anyone. Your utility continues to read your meter and bill the delivery portion; only the supply portion changes.

Can I switch back to the utility's default supply?

Yes, in every deregulated state. Default supply (sometimes called "Provider of Last Resort" or "POLR") is always available. It's usually not the cheapest option, but it's a fallback if you've had a bad experience with a retail provider.

What if I move to a different address mid-contract?

Most plans let you transfer to your new address without an early termination fee, as long as your new address is in the provider's service territory. Confirm before you move. If your new address is outside the territory, the ETF usually applies.

Are state-PUC comparison sites really unbiased?

Yes. They're run by the state Public Utility Commission and don't earn commissions. Plans are listed in standard format. The risk isn't bias, it's that the lowest-headline-rate plans aren't always the cheapest at your actual usage tier.

Can I have two electric providers — one for my home, one for an EV?

No. One supplier per meter. Some providers (especially in TX and PA) offer EV-friendly time-of-use plans that meaningfully reduce overnight charging cost. If you have an EV, prioritize EV-friendly TOU plans when shopping.

Do I need a credit check?

Most providers run a soft credit pull (no impact on your score). If you don't pass, they may require a deposit ($150–$300, refundable after 12 months on-time). Texas has prepaid options that don't require a credit check at all.
