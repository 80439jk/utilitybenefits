Fixed vs Variable Rate Electricity Plans

The single most important distinction in retail electricity. Most consumers who get burned by their electric bill are on variable-rate plans without realizing it.

SKBy Sara Khan · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Fixed-rate plans lock in a per-kWh price for the contract term (typically 6–36 months). Variable-rate plans change month-to-month at the provider's discretion — they're rarely priced competitively and are typically a customer-retention penalty for people who didn't read the renewal notice. Time-of-use (TOU) plans charge different rates for different hours of the day and can save 10–20% for households that can shift dishwasher / laundry / EV-charging timing. For most households, the right choice is a 12-month or 24-month fixed-rate plan, with a calendar reminder set for 60 days before expiration.

The three plan types

Fixed-rate plans

The provider commits to a specific per-kWh rate (e.g., 11.4¢/kWh) for the entire contract length, regardless of what happens in wholesale electricity markets. Common terms: 6, 12, 24, 36 months. Some providers offer 60-month plans. Almost all charge an early termination fee ($150–$250) if you cancel before the contract ends.

When fixed-rate makes sense: almost always. Predictable bills, immune to wholesale-price spikes, and you're not exposed to the provider's discretion on pricing.

The catch: the contract does end. Almost every fixed-rate plan rolls off to a variable "default" rate at expiration. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end so you can shop again.

Variable-rate plans

The provider can change the per-kWh rate any month, with limited or no notice. The rate is set by the provider — it's not directly tied to wholesale electricity prices. Most variable-rate plans have no contract and no early termination fee, which sounds like flexibility but in practice means you can leave at any time once you realize the rate is bad.

When variable-rate is justified: short-term (1–3 month) bridge between contracts, or a brief no-deposit no-credit-check option for someone with no other path. A few providers offer "wholesale pass-through" variable plans that are honest reflections of market prices, but these are rare and the consumer takes the upside and downside of price spikes.

The trap: "introductory" variable rates that start at 1¢/kWh for 60 days and reset to 25¢/kWh on day 61. Door-to-door and call-center salespeople push these heavily. The 1¢ rate gets the customer signed up; the 25¢ rate gets the provider rich.

The other trap: the auto-rollover from a fixed-rate plan to a variable rate at contract expiration. The new variable rate is almost always 30–80% higher than competitive fixed rates. Most consumers don't notice for 3–6 months — until a $400 winter bill arrives.

Time-of-use (TOU) plans

The price per kWh varies by hour of day. Typical structure: high "peak" rate (4pm–9pm weekdays), low "off-peak" rate (10pm–6am, weekends), and a "shoulder" rate in between. Some plans add a "super off-peak" tier for overnight (especially for EV charging).

When TOU makes sense:

  • You have an EV and can charge overnight
  • You have a smart thermostat and can pre-cool / pre-heat to avoid peak hours
  • You have flexible work hours and can run dishwasher, laundry, and oven outside peak
  • You have rooftop solar (your usage profile likely already favors TOU)
  • You're in a regulated state where TOU is one of the few ways to lower your bill

When TOU doesn't make sense:

  • You're home during peak hours and use significant cooling/heating then (e.g., elderly or work-from-home household with no flexibility)
  • Your usage is mostly small loads spread throughout the day (many older homes without major flexible loads)

Most utilities and retail providers offer free 1-year TOU pilots — try it for a year and switch back if your bill goes up.

Why variable-rate plans are usually a trap

The basic problem: variable-rate pricing isn't really about "tracking the market." It's about extracting more revenue from customers who aren't actively shopping.

State PUC investigations across multiple deregulated states have repeatedly found that variable-rate customers pay 30–80% more per kWh than equivalent fixed-rate customers in the same market. Massachusetts published a 2018 study showing residential variable-rate customers paid $253 million more than they would have on default-supply rates. Maryland, Connecticut, and New York have published similar findings.

Why? Because variable rates aren't subject to competitive pressure. Once a customer is enrolled in a variable plan, the provider's incentive is to charge as much as possible without triggering a customer call-in. Customers who don't notice their bills going up are quietly milked.

The fix is simple: never sign up for a variable-rate plan. If you're already on one, switch to a 12-month fixed plan today.

12-month vs 24-month vs 36-month

Once you've decided on a fixed-rate plan, the question is contract length. Tradeoffs:

  • 12-month plans — typically the most competitively priced because providers compete hardest at this length. You re-shop every year. Best for households that don't mind setting calendar reminders.
  • 24-month plans — usually 0.5–1¢/kWh more than 12-month, but lock you out of 24 months of potential price drops. Worth it if you don't want to re-shop annually.
  • 36-month plans — usually priced at a 1–2¢ premium. Locks in a long horizon. Worth it if wholesale market conditions are abnormally low and you want to stretch the favorable rate.
  • 60-month plans — almost never worth it. Wholesale electricity prices are volatile but tend to track natural gas; if you're locking in for 5 years you're betting against natural gas markets, which is a hard bet.

For most households, 12-month or 24-month fixed is the right choice.

FAQ

What if my fixed plan is about to expire?

Shop now. Don't let it auto-roll. Use your state's PUC apples-to-apples site, find a competitive 12-month or 24-month fixed plan, and enroll. The transition is seamless — no service interruption.

Are "indexed" or "wholesale pass-through" variable plans okay?

Sometimes. A few honest variable-rate plans pass through actual wholesale market prices with a small fixed adder. These are mostly used by businesses; residential pass-through plans exist in TX (Griddy was the most famous, before its 2021 winter-storm collapse). They're high-risk: when wholesale prices spike, your bill spikes. Most households shouldn't use them.

What's the catch with "free nights" or "free weekends" plans?

These are TOU plans where the off-peak hours are 100% discounted. The catch: peak rates are correspondingly higher. They work great for households that genuinely shift most usage to nights/weekends; they backfire for households that don't.

How often do variable rates actually change?

Most providers change variable rates monthly. Some change weekly. The provider isn't required to notify you in most states — the new rate just shows up on your next bill. This is part of why variable plans are dangerous: by the time you notice the rate moved, you've already been billed for a month at the new rate.

Can I be on a fixed-rate plan AND time-of-use?

Yes. Most providers offer fixed-rate TOU plans where the per-kWh rates at each tier are locked for the contract term. This combines the predictability of fixed with the savings of TOU. Common in TX and CA.
