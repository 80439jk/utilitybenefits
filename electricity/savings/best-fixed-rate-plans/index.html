Best Fixed-Rate Electricity Plans 2026

A framework for picking the genuinely cheapest fixed-rate plan for your household — not the one with the most prominent ad spend.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Energy & utilities research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

"Best" fixed-rate plan is household-specific. The lowest advertised rate often isn't the cheapest because of usage tiers, base charges, bill credits, and renewal terms. To find your best plan: pull last 12 months of bills, calculate average monthly kWh, then compare plans on your state's PUC apples-to-apples site at your actual usage tier. Aim for a 12-month or 24-month fixed-rate plan with a low base charge, no surprise bill-credit thresholds, and a reasonable variable-rate roll-off.

Why the cheapest advertised rate often isn't the cheapest plan

Retail electric providers advertise teaser rates the way airlines advertise fares: the headline number rarely reflects what the actual customer pays. Five common patterns:

  • Tier mismatch. A plan advertised as "8.9¢/kWh" may only hit that rate at exactly 1,000 kWh of monthly usage. At 700 kWh you might pay 14¢/kWh; at 1,500 kWh you might pay 9.5¢. Most retail plans in TX use this structure.
  • Hidden base charge. A plan with a 9.5¢ rate and a $9.99 monthly base charge costs more than a 10.5¢ plan with no base charge if you use under 1,000 kWh/month. The math:
    • 9.5¢ × 700 kWh = $66.50, plus $9.99 = $76.49
    • 10.5¢ × 700 kWh = $73.50, no base charge
    • The "more expensive" plan is actually $3 cheaper for this household.
  • Bill-credit thresholds. "$50 bill credit at 1,000+ kWh" punishes households at 999 kWh. Run the numbers at your actual usage.
  • Free-nights/weekends pricing. Off-peak hours are free, but peak hours are correspondingly higher. Great for some households, terrible for others.
  • Variable-rate roll-off after contract. A 12-month plan at 10¢/kWh that rolls to a 24¢ variable rate is worse, over 18 months, than a 12-month plan at 11¢/kWh that rolls to a 14¢ rate. Always check the post-contract default.

The 4-step shopping framework

  1. Calculate your average monthly usage. Pull your last 12 utility bills. Sum the kWh. Divide by 12. That's your "shopping tier." Round to the nearest 500 kWh tier on the comparison site.
  2. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, no variable component. On any state PUC site, you can filter. Skip prepaid, variable, and "introductory" plans unless you have a specific reason.
  3. Sort by total cost at your usage tier. Most state PUC sites let you sort by "average price at 1,000 kWh" or similar. Use that — not the headline rate.
  4. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 candidates. Confirm: rate at your tier, base charge, ETF, contract length, post-contract default rate.

Contract length: 12 vs 24 vs 36 months

  • 12-month: typically the most competitively priced. Re-shop annually. Best for households comfortable with calendar reminders.
  • 24-month: usually 0.5–1¢/kWh more. Locks you in but reduces shopping fatigue. Worth it for households that prefer set-it-and-forget-it.
  • 36-month: usually 1–2¢/kWh more. Locks in current market for a long horizon. Worth it when you believe rates are unusually low (e.g., low-natural-gas-price periods) and you want to ride the favorable curve.
  • 60-month: rarely worth it. Wholesale electricity volatility makes 5-year locks a hard bet against the market.

Green plans

Most providers offer 100% renewable plans at a 0.5–2¢/kWh premium ($5–$20/month for an average household). The renewable content is usually delivered via Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) — paper claims that match your usage to wind or solar generation elsewhere on the grid.

Some plans are priced below brown plans because the underlying renewable generation is genuinely cheap. Texas in particular has wind-heavy plans that beat fossil-heavy plans on price. Comparison: filter for "100% renewable" on PowerToChoose and check.

If true-additionality matters to you (i.e., you want to know your dollars are funding new renewable generation, not just paper RECs), look for plans that bundle a community solar share or rooftop solar buyback agreement. These are rarer but exist in CA, NY, MA, IL, MN, CO.

Setting up the renewal reminder

The single most important step in fixed-rate shopping isn't the initial enrollment — it's setting a calendar reminder to shop again before contract end.

  1. When you enroll, find the contract end date in your enrollment confirmation.
  2. Set a calendar event for 60 days before that date.
  3. The reminder text should be: "Shop electricity. Don't auto-roll. Use [state PUC site]."
  4. Set a backup reminder at 30 days before contract end.

Without this step, the auto-rollover to a variable rate is where most "I switched and saved" stories quietly turn into "my bill is $200 higher than it used to be" stories.

FAQ

Can I trust the lowest-rate plans on PowerToChoose / PAPowerSwitch?

The state PUC sites are accurate — the rates and disclosures are real. The risk isn't that the data is wrong; it's that the lowest headline rate often isn't the cheapest plan at your actual usage. Always sort by "total cost at your tier."

What if I'm not sure of my average usage?

Most utility websites (or your most recent bill) show your last 12 or 24 months of usage. If you've recently moved, use the EIA state averages: ~900 kWh/month is the U.S. residential mean. Texas/Southeast: 1,100–1,300. New England/Mountain West: 600–800.

What's a typical early termination fee?

$150–$250 for residential plans. Some plans have prorated ETFs (lower if you cancel later in the contract). A few have no ETF — these are sometimes called "no-commitment" plans and may carry a slightly higher rate as compensation.

What about prepaid plans?

Useful for households without credit access (no SSN required, no deposit). The per-kWh rate is usually 1–3¢ higher than fixed plans. Disconnection is automatic when balance hits zero — manage carefully. Common in TX.
