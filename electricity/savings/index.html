Whether you can shop for a retail electric provider depends on your state. Either way, there are real ways to cut your bill. This is the field guide.

1. How electric bill savings actually work

Your electric bill has two parts: supply (the energy itself, generated and sold by power plants) and delivery (the wires and poles that bring it to you, run by your local utility). In regulated states, your utility owns both. You can't shop for a different supplier — your only levers are usage, time-of-use rates if available, and efficiency upgrades.

In deregulated states, the legislature unbundled supply from delivery. Your local utility still owns the wires (and you can't change that), but the supply portion of your bill is now competitive. Dozens of retail electric providers compete on price, contract length, green content, and customer service. Switching is usually a 10-minute online process with no service interruption — same wires, same meter, just a different name on the supply line of your bill.

The bill keeps coming from your utility either way. The difference is who you're paying for the actual electrons.

4. What if you're in a regulated state?

In regulated states (California, Florida, the Southeast, most of the West and Plains), you can't shop for a supplier. Your local utility (PG&E, FPL, Duke, Southern Company, etc.) sets the rate, and the state Public Utility Commission approves it. The savings track is different but real:

  • Time-of-use (TOU) rates — most regulated utilities offer optional TOU plans that charge less for off-peak usage (overnight, midday). For households that can shift dishwasher / laundry / EV-charging timing, savings of 10–20% are typical.
  • Efficiency rebates — heat pumps, smart thermostats, attic insulation, LED conversion. Federal IRA tax credits stack with state and utility rebates. Total available stack often exceeds $10,000.
  • Right-sizing usage — energy audits (often free or rebated by your utility) identify the biggest waste. The average home has $200–$500/year of low-effort wins.
  • Net metering / community solar — in many regulated states, rooftop solar or community solar enrollment provides bill credits at retail or near-retail rates. Income-qualified community solar exists in IL, NY, MA, CO, MN, and others.
  • Assistance programs — if you qualify for LIHEAP, CARE, PIPP, or similar, that's often the biggest annual savings of any path. See the assistance hub →

5. What to avoid

The retail electric supply business has a long history of consumer-protection problems. Most of the bad actors target customers in deregulated markets. The patterns to watch for:

  • Variable-rate "introductory" plans — a low teaser rate for 1–3 months that resets to a punishing market rate. Most often used by aggressive door-to-door and call-center salespeople. See fixed vs variable →
  • Door-to-door sales — many states (NY, IL, MD) tightened these rules in 2024. If a salesperson at your door asks to "see your bill to verify your account," that's an enrollment script. Shut the door.
  • Early-termination fees buried in the fine print — some plans charge $150–$250 to cancel before contract end. Check before signing.
  • Prepaid plans with hidden penalties — useful for some Texas no-credit-check households, but read the disconnect-policy fine print.
  • "Green" plans that are mostly unbundled RECs — if a true-green plan matters to you, check whether it's actually buying renewable generation or just paper certificates.
  • Auto-renewal at default rates — most fixed-rate plans roll off to a variable "default" rate after the contract ends. Your provider is required to send a notice but few people read it. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before expiration.

7. When to switch

Three good moments:

  1. 60 days before your fixed-rate contract expires. Almost every fixed-rate plan rolls off to a worse variable rate. Your provider is required to send a notice but it's easy to miss. Set a calendar reminder when you sign up.
  2. When you move to a deregulated state. The default-supply rate from your new utility is rarely the best option. Shop within your first 30 days.
  3. When utility rates are announced to change. Most utilities adjust default-supply rates twice a year (commonly June 1 and December 1). The PA "Price to Compare" reset, Ohio Standard Service Offer, and similar are the moments when shopping is most likely to save you real money.

9. Frequently asked questions

Will switching providers interrupt my power?

No. Switching only changes the supply portion of your bill, not the wires. You stay on the same utility, same meter, same physical service. The transition happens at your next meter read.

Why is my variable rate so much higher than the fixed-rate offers?

Because variable rates aren't priced on the wholesale market — they're priced on what the provider thinks they can get away with charging customers who aren't shopping. Treat any variable-rate enrollment as a 60-day to-do list to switch to a fixed plan.

Do I need to call my old provider when I switch?

No. The new provider notifies your utility and your old provider as part of enrollment. You don't need to call anyone. Your final bill from the old provider arrives within 1–2 cycles.

What's a "kWh" and how does my usage compare?

A kilowatt-hour is the unit electricity is sold in. The U.S. residential average is about 900 kWh/month. Texas and Southeast averages run higher (1,100–1,300 kWh) due to AC and electric heat. New England and the Mountain West average lower (600–800 kWh) due to natural gas heating and milder summers.

Are "green" plans more expensive?

Sometimes. The premium for a 100% renewable plan is typically 0.5–2¢ per kWh ($5–$20/month for an average household). Some markets have green plans priced below brown plans because the underlying wind/solar generation is genuinely cheap. Compare plan-by-plan.

What if I rent?

You can switch as long as the electric account is in your name. If utilities are bundled into your rent, you can't change the supplier — but you can encourage your landlord to. Renters in deregulated states are often the most overcharged because they don't realize they have shopping rights.

Should I trust comparison sites?

Sometimes. State-run "apples-to-apples" sites (PowerToChoose.org in TX, PAPowerSwitch in PA, EnergyChoice.gov in OH, PlugInIllinois in IL, Power Switch in NY) are run by Public Utility Commissions and are unbiased. Private comparison sites and brokers earn commissions and may push higher-margin plans. Full comparison →

Can switching providers help if I'm behind on my bill?

Generally no — most providers won't enroll customers in active arrears. The first move when you're behind is the assistance track: LIHEAP, your utility's hardship program, and crisis funds. Once you're current, then shopping makes sense. Assistance hub →

Does shopping for electricity affect my credit?

Most providers run a soft credit check that doesn't affect your score. Some require a deposit if you don't pass — typically $150–$300, refundable after 12 months of on-time payments. Texas has prepaid options that don't require a credit check at all.

I'm on auto-pay. Can I still switch?

Yes. Set up auto-pay with your new provider. Your utility's auto-pay setup for the delivery portion of your bill stays the same.
