Illinois Electricity: PlugInIllinois, ComEd Default, and Door-to-Door Rules

Illinois has retail electricity choice through PlugInIllinois.org. ComEd's default-supply rate is often beatable; Ameren's downstate rates are more competitive. ICC tightened door-to-door rules after years of consumer complaints.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Illinois has a deregulated retail electricity market. Shop on PlugInIllinois.org — the state PUC's apples-to-apples site. Current 12-month fixed-rate plans range 6.9 – 9.5¢/kWh. Compare against your utility's default-supply rate before switching. Illinois default-supply rates ("Price to Compare") reset every June 1 and October 1 for ComEd, May 1 for Ameren. Both utilities publish the rates prominently. ComEd default has historically been higher than Ameren — meaning ComEd customers tend to see bigger savings from shopping.

What makes Illinois different

Illinois had one of the worst door-to-door electric supplier reputations in the country through the 2010s — multiple AG enforcement actions and a 2022 ICC reform overhaul. Door-to-door enrollment is now restricted (advance notice required, longer cooling-off period, stronger disclosure rules). The reform changed the math: a higher share of IL retail enrollments now happen via state PUC site or direct provider websites, not at the door.

The other distinguishing feature: PIPP (Percentage of Income Payment Plan) for ComEd customers. Income-qualified ComEd customers can enroll in PIPP, which caps the electric bill at 6% of household income. PIPP is often larger savings than any retail shopping for income-qualified Chicago-area households. Illinois LIHEAP + PIPP guide →

Downstate (Ameren territory) doesn't have PIPP — LIHEAP is the main assistance path.

Illinois utilities (these stay the same; only your supplier changes)

  • ComEd — Northern IL — Chicago metro and surrounding counties
  • Ameren Illinois — Central and Southern IL — Springfield, Champaign, Bloomington
  • MidAmerican Energy — Quad Cities area (overlap with IA)
  • Mt. Carmel Public Utility — Wabash County (small)

Illinois-specific things to watch for

  • The 2022 ICC reforms. Door-to-door is restricted but not banned. Telemarketing rules tightened. Some unscrupulous suppliers persist.
  • ComEd vs Ameren default-rate spread. ComEd default is usually higher; Ameren default is usually closer to wholesale cost. ComEd shoppers see bigger savings.
  • PIPP eligibility for ComEd customers. Don't shop your way out of PIPP. Run the income calculation first.
  • Renewable Portfolio Standard (RPS) charges. Both ComEd and Ameren bills include RPS surcharges. These don't change based on supplier; they're utility-side.
  • "Energy choice" cold callers. Many post-reform ESCO sales now happen via cold call. If you didn't initiate the call, hang up.

How to shop in Illinois

  1. Pull a recent utility bill (you'll need your account number).
  2. Calculate your average monthly kWh from the last 12 months.
  3. Go to PlugInIllinois.org and enter your service address.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, and sort by cost at your usage tier.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 options. Confirm rate at YOUR tier, base charge, ETF, and post-contract default rate.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end.

If you're income-qualified for assistance

Most Illinois households at or below 200% of the federal poverty level qualify for at least one assistance program. These often beat retail shopping savings:

  • LIHEAP — federal energy assistance, $300–$1,500/year typical. Illinois LIHEAP guide →
  • State or utility-specific discount programs — varies by state. CARE/FERA in CA, PIPP in OH/IL, USF in NJ, CAP rates in PA, EAP in NH.
  • Crisis funds — for shutoff threats and emergencies.

Illinois electricity FAQ

Why is ComEd default supply more expensive than Ameren?

Different procurement strategies and different generation mixes feeding each. ComEd serves Chicago metro from a more expensive PJM-regional supply; Ameren downstate is closer to MISO supply. The spread varies year-to-year.

How do I find my Illinois account number?

On your ComEd or Ameren bill. PlugInIllinois enrollment requires it.

Should I shop or do PIPP?

If you're income-qualified for PIPP (ComEd customer at or below 200% FPL), PIPP is almost always better than shopping. The 6%-of-income cap means very large savings vs. market rates. Illinois PIPP guide →

Are door-to-door enrollments enforceable?

Yes, but the 2022 reforms require longer cooling-off periods (10 business days) and stronger disclosure. If a door enrollment misled you, you have grounds to cancel and seek refund. ICC complaints process is at icc.illinois.gov.

What if I'm in a municipal aggregation?

Many Chicago suburbs (Oak Park, Evanston, Skokie, etc.) have negotiated municipal aggregation rates that auto-enroll residents. Similar to Ohio's NOPEC. Check your bill for the supplier name. You can opt out and shop competitively if the aggregation rate isn't competitive.

Can I shop AND have community solar?

Yes. Illinois Solar for All is the income-qualified community solar program — provides discounted enrollment with bill credits typically reducing electricity bills by $400–$800/year. Stacks with whatever supplier you're on.

What about the Climate & Equitable Jobs Act (CEJA)?

2021 IL law funding renewable transition. Includes income-qualified solar incentives and weatherization expansion. Programs roll out through 2026–2030.

Can renters in Chicago shop?

Yes, if your ComEd account is in your name. Renters with utilities in their name have the same shopping rights as homeowners.
