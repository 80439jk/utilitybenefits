Texas Electricity: How to Pick the Right Plan in the Most Competitive Market

Texas has the most competitive retail electricity market in the country — 100+ providers and dozens of plan types. The choice paradox is real. This is the field guide.

What makes Texas different

Texas is the only state where the entire grid (ERCOT) operates outside the federal interstate market. That's why Texas had the 2021 winter storm crisis — and also why retail competition is so fierce. The wholesale market clears every 5 minutes; retail providers compete on creative plan structures (free nights, bill credit thresholds, no-deposit plans, prepaid plans).

The flip side: complexity. The same headline rate can mean very different real costs depending on usage tier and bill credits. Almost every Texas plan has a usage-tier structure where the cheapest rate only applies in a narrow band (often 1,000 kWh exactly). Households outside that band pay much more.

Texas also has the most prepaid and no-deposit options, which makes it accessible for households without credit. The tradeoff is higher per-kWh rates.

Texas utilities (these stay the same; only your supplier changes)

  • Oncor — Dallas / Fort Worth / much of north and central TX
  • CenterPoint — Houston metro
  • AEP Texas — Corpus Christi / South TX / parts of West TX
  • Texas-New Mexico Power — Far West TX (El Paso area is municipal, not deregulated)

Texas-specific things to watch for

  • Bill-credit thresholds. "$50 credit at 1,000+ kWh" punishes households at 999 kWh. Run the numbers at YOUR average usage.
  • Free-nights/weekends plans. Off-peak hours are free; peak hours are correspondingly higher. Great for some households, terrible for others.
  • Provider of Last Resort (POLR) rates. The default rate when you sign up for service at a new address. Almost always more expensive than competitive plans.
  • The 2021 winter storm precedent. Variable-rate "wholesale pass-through" plans (like the now-defunct Griddy) exposed customers to $9,000+ bills during the storm. Stick to fixed-rate.
  • ETF gotchas. Some Texas plans charge $20/month early termination per remaining month — no flat fee, just a per-month charge. Check the EFL.

How to shop in Texas

  1. Pull a recent utility bill (you'll need your account number).
  2. Calculate your average monthly kWh from the last 12 months.
  3. Go to PowerToChoose.org and enter your service address.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, and sort by cost at your usage tier.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 options. Confirm rate at YOUR tier, base charge, ETF, and post-contract default rate.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end.

Texas electricity FAQ

How do I find my ESI ID?

Your Electric Service Identifier is a 17-digit number on your utility bill (Oncor, CenterPoint, AEP, TNMP) — usually in the upper-right area. PowerToChoose enrollment requires it.

What's the average Texas electric bill?

About $160/month based on EIA data — higher than the U.S. average due to summer AC usage. Most TX households use 1,100–1,300 kWh/month in summer, 600–900 in winter.

Can I switch providers if I'm in Austin or San Antonio?

No. Austin Energy and CPS Energy are municipal utilities — not deregulated. Your only option is the municipal rate. Same for Brownsville PUB and most other municipal utilities.

What happens if my provider goes bankrupt?

ERCOT has a Provider of Last Resort process. You're automatically transferred to a backup provider at default rates. No service interruption. This happens periodically (Griddy 2021, several smaller providers in 2023-24).

Should I use a prepaid plan?

Only if you can't get a credit-checked plan or you're trying to control usage tightly (the daily balance display creates strong feedback loops). The per-kWh rate is 1–3¢ higher than credit-checked plans.

How does CEAP fit with electricity savings?

CEAP is the Texas LIHEAP — federal energy assistance for households under 150% FPL. It pays $300–$1,200 toward your electric bill annually. CEAP enrollment can stack with retail provider switching. CEAP guide →

What's the difference between TXU, Reliant, and Direct Energy?

All three are major Texas retail electric providers (REPs). TXU and Reliant are the largest by customer count. Direct Energy was a top-3 player but consolidated under NRG. None is clearly "better" — compare specific plans on PowerToChoose.

Can I switch in winter / mid-storm?

Yes, switches happen at your next meter read regardless of season. There's no restriction on when you can shop — though if you're behind on bills, most providers won't enroll you until arrears are addressed.
