Pennsylvania Electricity: PAPowerSwitch, Price to Compare, and CAP Rates

Pennsylvania has a mature deregulated market via PAPowerSwitch.com. Income-qualified households can also enroll in utility CAP discount rates that stack on top of competitive shopping.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Pennsylvania has a deregulated retail electricity market. Shop on PAPowerSwitch.com — the state PUC's apples-to-apples site. Current 12-month fixed-rate plans range 8.9 – 12.5¢/kWh. Compare against your utility's default-supply rate before switching. Pennsylvania calls the default-supply rate the "Price to Compare" (PTC). It's published by each utility (PECO, PPL, Duquesne, FirstEnergy) and resets every June 1 and December 1. It's the benchmark every retail provider needs to beat. Most PTCs run 10.5–14.5¢/kWh, so most fixed-rate retail plans beat them.

What makes Pennsylvania different

Pennsylvania has the most rigorous "Price to Compare" disclosure in the country. Every utility bill shows the current PTC prominently — meaning consumers can easily benchmark their current rate against shopping alternatives. This drives more active shopping than any other deregulated state.

The other distinguishing feature: CAP (Customer Assistance Program) rates. Each major PA utility (PECO, PPL, Duquesne, FirstEnergy, PGW for gas) operates a discount-rate tier for income-qualified customers — typically 4–10% of household income capped, regardless of usage. CAP enrollment is independent of LIHEAP and stacks. For income-qualified households, CAP often beats anything available through retail shopping.

If you're income-qualified, start with CAP. If you're not, start with PAPowerSwitch.

Pennsylvania-specific things to watch for

  • Variable-rate roll-offs. PA has had multiple AG enforcement actions against providers who rolled customers from low fixed rates to predatory variable rates without clear notice. Always check the post-contract default.
  • Door-to-door sales. PA tightened door-to-door rules in 2023 but they persist. Don't sign at the door.
  • "Dual-fuel" pitches that bundle electric and gas. Often higher per-unit rates than shopping each separately.
  • Termination of CAP enrollment when switching providers. CAP rates apply to your default-supply portion. Some retail shoppers accidentally end their CAP eligibility.

How to shop in Pennsylvania

  1. Pull a recent utility bill (you'll need your account number).
  2. Calculate your average monthly kWh from the last 12 months.
  3. Go to PAPowerSwitch.com and enter your service address.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, and sort by cost at your usage tier.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 options. Confirm rate at YOUR tier, base charge, ETF, and post-contract default rate.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end.

If you're income-qualified for assistance

Most Pennsylvania households at or below 200% of the federal poverty level qualify for at least one assistance program. These often beat retail shopping savings:

  • LIHEAP — federal energy assistance, $300–$1,500/year typical. Pennsylvania LIHEAP guide →
  • State or utility-specific discount programs — varies by state. CARE/FERA in CA, PIPP in OH/IL, USF in NJ, CAP rates in PA, EAP in NH.
  • Crisis funds — for shutoff threats and emergencies.

Pennsylvania electricity FAQ

What's a Price to Compare?

The default-supply rate your utility charges if you don't shop. Published every 6 months. Every retail electric supplier needs to beat it to make sense for you. Find yours at PAPowerSwitch or on your bill.

How do I find my PA POD ID?

The Point of Delivery ID is on your utility bill. PAPowerSwitch enrollment uses it to confirm your service location.

Should I be on CAP if I qualify?

Yes — CAP rates are usually the best option for income-qualified PA households. Apply through your utility (PECO, PPL, Duquesne, FirstEnergy, PGW). Stacks with LIHEAP. PA LIHEAP + CAP guide →

Can I shop AND be on CAP?

Yes, but it's complex. CAP applies to the default-supply portion of your bill. If you switch to a retail supplier, CAP no longer applies to that portion. Most CAP-eligible households are better off staying on default supply with the CAP discount rather than shopping.

How often does the PTC change?

Every 6 months — typically June 1 and December 1. Some FirstEnergy zones reset on a slightly different schedule. The 60 days before a PTC reset is the most active shopping window.

What if I'm with PPL and they're more expensive than PECO?

Each utility's PTC is set independently. PPL has historically run higher than PECO, which means PPL customers see larger savings from shopping. Same retail providers serve both — different default-supply benchmarks.

Can I keep my retail supplier if I move within PA?

Usually yes, as long as your new address is in the same utility's service territory and the supplier serves that territory. Confirm with the supplier before moving.

What's LIURP?

Low-Income Usage Reduction Program — free home weatherization for income-qualified PA utility customers. Stacks with LIHEAP and CAP. Apply through your utility or DHS. PA assistance guide →
