Ohio Electricity: EnergyChoice.gov, Government Aggregation, and SSO

Ohio has retail electricity choice through EnergyChoice.Ohio.gov. The state also has the country's biggest "government aggregation" market — many OH residents are auto-enrolled in a city- or county-negotiated rate.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Ohio has a deregulated retail electricity market. Shop on EnergyChoice.Ohio.gov — the state PUC's apples-to-apples site. Current 12-month fixed-rate plans range 6.9 – 10.5¢/kWh. Compare against your utility's default-supply rate before switching. Ohio calls default supply the "Standard Service Offer" (SSO) — also known as Price to Compare. Each utility has its own SSO that resets monthly or quarterly. Many OH residents are auto-enrolled in government aggregation programs (city or county-negotiated rates) instead of SSO; check your bill to see your current supplier.

What makes Ohio different

Ohio is the U.S. capital of government aggregation — a structure where a city, county, or municipality negotiates a bulk electricity rate on behalf of all residents in the jurisdiction. Eligible residents are auto-enrolled (with an opt-out window) into the negotiated rate. Hundreds of Ohio jurisdictions participate, including most of greater Cleveland and Cincinnati.

If you live in Ohio and don't recognize your supplier's name on your bill, you might be in a government aggregation program. The negotiated rates are often (but not always) competitive with what you'd find on EnergyChoice. Check your bill for the supplier name and per-kWh rate.

The other distinguishing feature: PIPP Plus — Ohio's income-qualified payment plan that caps your electric bill at 6% of household income. PIPP Plus is often larger savings than any retail shopping for income-qualified households. Ohio PIPP Plus guide →

Ohio utilities (these stay the same; only your supplier changes)

  • FirstEnergy (Cleveland Electric Illuminating, Ohio Edison, Toledo Edison) — Northern OH
  • AEP Ohio — Columbus / Central / SE OH
  • Duke Energy Ohio — Cincinnati area
  • Dayton Power & Light (DP&L / AES Ohio) — Dayton area

Ohio-specific things to watch for

  • Government aggregation opt-out windows. If you don't want the auto-enrolled rate, you have a 21-day window to opt out. After that, you're on the aggregation rate by default.
  • SSO auctions twice a year. Each utility's default-supply rate resets every 6 months via PUCO auction. The 60 days before a reset is when shopping makes the most sense.
  • FirstEnergy ESH/POLR rates. If you fall off your supplier (utility-driven), you're moved to a Provider of Last Resort rate that's typically high. Stay current.
  • PIPP Plus eligibility cliff. If you're income-qualified for PIPP Plus, that's almost always better than retail shopping. Don't shop your way out of PIPP eligibility.

How to shop in Ohio

  1. Pull a recent utility bill (you'll need your account number).
  2. Calculate your average monthly kWh from the last 12 months.
  3. Go to EnergyChoice.Ohio.gov and enter your service address.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, and sort by cost at your usage tier.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 options. Confirm rate at YOUR tier, base charge, ETF, and post-contract default rate.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end.

If you're income-qualified for assistance

Most Ohio households at or below 200% of the federal poverty level qualify for at least one assistance program. These often beat retail shopping savings:

  • LIHEAP — federal energy assistance, $300–$1,500/year typical. Ohio LIHEAP guide →
  • State or utility-specific discount programs — varies by state. CARE/FERA in CA, PIPP in OH/IL, USF in NJ, CAP rates in PA, EAP in NH.
  • Crisis funds — for shutoff threats and emergencies.

Ohio electricity FAQ

How do I tell if I'm in a government aggregation program?

Check your utility bill for the supplier name. If it's not your utility (FirstEnergy, AEP, Duke, AES Ohio) and not a competitive supplier you signed up with, it's likely a government aggregation supplier. Examples: NOPEC (Northeast Ohio), SOPEC (Southeast Ohio), Energy Council Aggregation.

Should I stay in government aggregation or shop?

Compare the aggregation rate to current EnergyChoice offers at your usage tier. Often the aggregation rate is competitive (because the city negotiated bulk pricing) but not always. Check at least once a year.

How does PIPP Plus stack with shopping?

It doesn't, really. PIPP Plus applies to your default-supply portion. If you switch to a competitive supplier, PIPP Plus may not apply. For income-qualified households, PIPP Plus is almost always the better option than retail shopping. PIPP Plus guide →

What's the difference between SSO and POLR?

SSO is the default-supply rate the utility offers via PUCO auction. POLR is the higher rate you get if your retail supplier drops you and the utility has to pick you up mid-cycle. Avoid POLR by staying current with your supplier and watching for switching gaps.

How do I find my Ohio account number?

On your utility bill (FirstEnergy, AEP, Duke, AES Ohio). Required for EnergyChoice enrollment.

Can I be on competitive supply AND community solar?

Yes. Community solar is a separate product — you subscribe to a share of an off-site solar project for bill credits. Stacks with whatever supplier you're on.

What's NOPEC?

Northeast Ohio Public Energy Council — the largest government aggregation program in the U.S. Negotiates electricity and gas rates for ~225 communities in northeast OH. Most NOPEC residents are auto-enrolled.

Can renters shop in Ohio?

Yes, if the electric account is in your name. Renters with utilities in their name have the same shopping rights as homeowners.
