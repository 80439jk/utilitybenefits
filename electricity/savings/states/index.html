Electricity Shopping by State

Each state's electricity market works differently. Deregulated states let you shop for a retail provider; regulated states don't. Find your state's playbook below.

MR
Reviewed by Marcus Reid Energy Policy Analyst · former NARUC staff
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer

16 states have deregulated retail electricity markets where consumers can switch providers: Texas, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Connecticut, Maryland, Delaware, DC, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, Virginia (limited), and Michigan (limited). The other 35 states are regulated — your local utility is the only supply option, and savings come from time-of-use rates, efficiency rebates, and assistance programs.

Regulated states (no shopping; savings come from elsewhere)

In regulated states, your local utility is the only supplier. The savings track is different but real:

  • Time-of-use (TOU) rates — most regulated utilities offer optional TOU plans. Best for households with EVs, smart thermostats, or flexible schedules.
  • Efficiency rebates — federal IRA tax credits stack with state and utility rebates. Heat pumps, smart thermostats, attic insulation, LED conversion.
  • Right-sizing usage — many utilities offer free home energy audits. Average home has $200–$500/year of low-effort wins.
  • Net metering / community solar — rooftop solar or community solar enrollment provides bill credits at retail or near-retail rates.
  • Assistance programs — if you qualify for LIHEAP, CARE, PIPP, or your state equivalent, that's often the biggest annual savings of any path. See the assistance hub →

Major regulated states: California, Florida, Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Missouri, Kentucky, Indiana, West Virginia, Vermont, Washington, Oregon, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, Colorado, Utah, Nevada, Arizona, New Mexico, North Dakota, South Dakota, Alaska, Hawaii.

Most of these states have LIHEAP and state-equivalent assistance programs. Check the LIHEAP state pages for assistance options in your state.

When rates change in your state

Most state utility rates change twice a year — typically June 1 and December 1, sometimes January 1 and July 1. These are the moments when shopping is most likely to save money in deregulated states (because the new default-supply rate is the benchmark every retail provider has to beat) and when TOU enrollment makes the biggest difference in regulated states (because TOU rates change at the same time).

Sign up for your state PUC's email list to get the rate-change announcements. Most send a notice 30–60 days before the new rate takes effect.
