Massachusetts Electricity: After the AG's $253M Investigation

Massachusetts has retail electricity choice but its competitive supplier history is troubled. The Attorney General's 2018 investigation found residential customers paid $253 million more than they would have on default supply. The 2024 reforms tightened the rules — but skepticism remains warranted.

SKBy Sara Khan · Reviewed by Marcus Reid · Last verified: May 3, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Massachusetts has a deregulated retail electricity market. Shop on EnergySwitchMA.gov — the state PUC's apples-to-apples site. Current 12-month fixed-rate plans range 13.5 – 16.5¢/kWh. Compare against your utility's default-supply rate before switching. Massachusetts default supply is called Basic Service. Each utility's Basic Service rate resets every 6 months (some quarterly) via DPU-overseen procurement. MA Basic Service rates have historically been competitive — often hard to beat. Shop carefully and always benchmark against the current Basic Service rate for your utility.

What makes Massachusetts different

The MA AG's 2018 investigation remains the most influential consumer-protection report in the country: residential competitive-supply customers paid $253 million more over a 5-year period than they would have on Basic Service. That's net — accounting for the few customers who genuinely saved. The investigation found systematic predatory pricing, particularly door-to-door enrollment and variable-rate roll-offs.

Subsequent reforms (2024) banned door-to-door enrollment for residential electric supply, tightened disclosure requirements, and capped variable-rate spreads. MA is now meaningfully safer than NY or IL but the underlying economics — Basic Service is procured competitively and often hard to beat — mean shopping savings are modest in MA. Many MA households are better off on Basic Service.

The bigger savings opportunity for many MA residents is Mass Save — the state's energy efficiency program funded by utility ratepayers. Free home energy audits, deep weatherization rebates, heat pump incentives often $10,000+. Mass Save is uniquely strong vs. other state programs.

Massachusetts-specific things to watch for

  • Door-to-door is now banned in MA for residential electric supply (2024). Anyone at your door pitching electric supply is operating illegally — report to the AG.
  • Basic Service is often the best deal. Don't assume shopping always saves. Run the math against current Basic Service.
  • The variable-rate cap doesn't prevent monthly spikes. Read the fine print on any non-fixed plan.
  • Mass Save is often the bigger savings. Especially for homeowners with older heating systems.
  • Municipal utility customers can't shop. Municipal utilities (Belmont, Concord, Holyoke, etc.) are exempt from deregulation.

How to shop in Massachusetts

  1. Pull a recent utility bill (you'll need your account number).
  2. Calculate your average monthly kWh from the last 12 months.
  3. Go to EnergySwitchMA.gov and enter your service address.
  4. Filter for fixed-rate, 12 or 24 month, and sort by cost at your usage tier.
  5. Read the Electricity Facts Label for the top 3 options. Confirm rate at YOUR tier, base charge, ETF, and post-contract default rate.
  6. Enroll. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before contract end.

Massachusetts electricity FAQ

Should I shop for electric supply in MA?

Sometimes, but Basic Service is often competitive. Always compare against the current Basic Service rate for your utility before switching. The MA AG's 2018 investigation showed most residential competitive-supply customers ended up paying more, not less.

Is door-to-door enrollment legal in MA?

No — banned for residential electric supply since 2024. If someone pitches you at your door, they're operating illegally. Report to the MA AG.

What's Mass Save?

Massachusetts statewide energy efficiency program — funded by utility ratepayers. Offers free home energy audits, deep weatherization rebates (up to 75% covered), and heat pump incentives often $10,000+ for qualifying households. Often the largest savings opportunity in MA. masssave.com

How does MA Fuel Assistance fit?

Massachusetts Fuel Assistance / HEAP — federal LIHEAP at 60% State Median Income, paying $700–$1,800 typical. Income-qualified households should apply. Stacks with Mass Save. MA Fuel Assistance guide →

How often does Basic Service reset?

Eversource and National Grid reset their Basic Service rates every 6 months — typically January 1 and July 1. Unitil resets quarterly. The 60 days before reset is shopping window.

Can I shop if I'm with a municipal utility?

No. Municipal utility customers (Belmont, Concord, Holyoke, Taunton, etc.) are exempt from deregulation. Your only option is the municipal rate.

How does the variable-rate cap work?

MA caps variable-rate spreads at 110% of Basic Service on a trailing-12-month basis. Doesn't prevent month-to-month spikes — just limits the long-run average.

Are there NJ-style USF programs in MA?

Eversource and National Grid offer income-qualified discount tiers (R-2 rates) — typically 20–30% off Basic Service for qualifying customers. Apply through your utility.
