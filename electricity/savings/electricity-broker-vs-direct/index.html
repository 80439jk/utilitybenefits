Electricity Broker vs Direct Enrollment vs PUC Site

Three ways to enroll in a retail electric plan. Each has tradeoffs. The right answer depends on your time, your state, and how complex your situation is.

UB
Reviewed by UtilityBenefits Editorial Team Energy & utilities research desk · cited primary sources
Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

Three channels: state PUC apples-to-apples sites (free, unbiased, run by Public Utility Commissions); direct enrollment on a provider's website (skip the middleman, but you have to pick the provider yourself); and brokers / marketplaces (concierge service, but they earn commissions and may push higher-margin plans). For most households, start at the state PUC site, find the cheapest plan at your usage tier, then enroll directly. Use a broker when you have a complex situation (commercial, multi-meter, demand-charge, or you've been burned before and want a human to walk you through).

The three channels

1. State PUC apples-to-apples sites

Operated by your state Public Utility Commission. Lists every plan available to your service address with the same disclosure format (Electricity Facts Label or equivalent). Free, no enrollment, no commissions. Examples: PowerToChoose.org (TX), PAPowerSwitch.com (PA), EnergyChoice.Ohio.gov (OH), PlugInIllinois.org (IL).

Pros: Unbiased. Comprehensive. State-regulated disclosure standard so plans are directly comparable. Free.

Cons: The interface is functional but not friendly. Filtering takes some practice. The default sort isn't always "cheapest at your usage" — you have to set that yourself.

Best for: Most households. Especially if you have a few minutes and a recent bill in front of you.

2. Direct enrollment with a provider

You pick a provider (Reliant, TXU, Constellation, Direct Energy, etc.) and enroll on their website. Same plans, same rates as the PUC site — but you're shopping one provider's catalog rather than the whole market.

Pros: Faster if you already know which provider you want. Provider-specific perks (free smart thermostat, refer-a-friend credits, EV charging discounts) sometimes only show up in direct enrollment flows.

Cons: You have to know which provider to start with. Easy to miss better plans from competitors. No comparison context.

Best for: Households who've already shopped on the PUC site, picked a winner, and want provider-specific perks.

3. Brokers and marketplaces

Third-party companies that aggregate plans across providers and walk you through enrollment — often by phone. Examples: Choose Energy, Save On Energy, ElectricChoice.com, and many local/regional brokers.

Brokers earn commissions from providers (typically $20–$80 per residential enrollment, more for commercial). The commission isn't itself bad — but it creates an incentive to push plans with higher commissions, which often aren't the cheapest plans for the customer.

Pros: Human walks you through. Useful if you're paralyzed by choice or the PUC site interface confuses you. Commercial brokers (which you're not, if you're reading this as a residential consumer) genuinely add value via demand-charge analysis and contract negotiation.

Cons: Conflict of interest. The plans pitched aren't always the cheapest at your tier. Some brokers also broker into ETF traps because the commission is paid up-front.

Best for: Complex situations (commercial, multi-meter, high-demand) or households who've had bad experiences with self-shopping and want a human safeguard.

How to read a broker's pitch

If you're talking to a broker, three questions to ask:

  1. "Are you showing me every plan available, or just plans you have a relationship with?" Honest brokers will say they have relationships with X providers; some plans aren't in their catalog. The unhonest will dodge.
  2. "What's the rate at my actual usage tier?" Not the headline rate. Force them to compute the bill at your actual kWh.
  3. "What's the post-contract default rate, and is it variable?" If they don't know off the top of their head, they're not paying attention to the back half of the contract.

Phone vs online enrollment

Most retail providers and brokers will enroll you over the phone. The pros: a human can explain the plan, walk through the EFL, and answer questions. The cons: you can't easily comparison-shop in real time, and call-recordings are sometimes used as enrollment authorization without explicit confirmation.

Three rules for phone enrollment:

  1. Ask the rep to email you the EFL before committing. Read it.
  2. Get the rep's name and a confirmation number. Save them.
  3. If you said "I'm thinking about it" and the rep moves to enroll you, say "no, do not enroll me, I'll call back." The recording matters.

We're a comparison and content site. Our specialist line is run by partner agencies that earn commissions when consumers enroll. Our content recommends what we think is honestly best for the consumer, including telling you to use free state PUC sites when that's the right answer. Full disclosure →

Why do brokers exist if state PUC sites are free and comprehensive?

Several reasons: many consumers find PUC sites confusing, brokers operate in markets the state site doesn't cover well (commercial, multi-meter), and providers value the customer acquisition channel enough to pay commissions. The model is similar to insurance brokers — sometimes useful, sometimes a pure markup.

Are there honest brokers?

Yes. Look for brokers who: disclose commissions transparently, present multiple plans (not just the highest-margin), use your actual usage in calculations, and don't pressure-close on the first call. Some commercial brokers add real value via contract negotiation; most residential brokers add modest value beyond the PUC site.

What's the difference between a broker and a marketplace?

Functionally similar. A broker typically interacts with you (phone, chat, email); a marketplace is more of a self-serve site that earns the same commissions. Both have the same basic conflict of interest.
