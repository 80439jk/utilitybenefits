Saving on Electric Bills in Regulated States

Most U.S. states (35 of 50, plus parts of MI and VA) don't allow you to shop for an electric supplier. You still have real savings paths — they're just different.

UB

Last reviewed May 4, 2026
Quick Answer · Speakable schema

In regulated states, your local utility (PG&E, FPL, Duke, Southern Company, Xcel, etc.) is your only electricity supplier — you can't shop. The savings paths are: time-of-use (TOU) rates if your utility offers them, federal IRA tax credits stacking with utility and state rebates ($10K+ available for heat pumps and weatherization), energy audits (often free or rebated by your utility), community solar or rooftop solar bill credits, and income-qualified assistance programs (LIHEAP, CARE, PIPP, etc.) that often pay $400–$1,500/year.

1. Time-of-use (TOU) rates

Most regulated utilities offer optional TOU rate plans where the price per kWh varies by hour of day. Typical structure:

  • Peak (typically 4pm–9pm weekdays in summer; varies winter): high rate, often 1.5–2× the standard tier
  • Off-peak (typically 10pm–6am, weekends): low rate, often 30–50% of peak
  • Shoulder: middle rate
  • Super off-peak (some utilities): overnight tier optimized for EV charging

When TOU saves money

  • You have an EV and can charge overnight
  • You have a smart thermostat and can pre-cool / pre-heat to avoid peak hours
  • You have flexible work hours and can run dishwasher, laundry, oven outside peak
  • You have rooftop solar (your usage profile likely already favors TOU)
  • You're empty-nest, snowbird, or otherwise have low summer afternoon usage

When TOU backfires

  • You're home during peak hours and use significant cooling/heating then (elderly, work-from-home with no flexibility)
  • Your usage is mostly small loads spread throughout the day
  • You're in a hot climate with afternoon peak rates and limited flexibility

Most utilities offer free 1-year TOU pilots — try it for a year and switch back if your bill goes up. CA, AZ, NV, and FL utilities are particularly aggressive on TOU expansion. Ask your utility specifically; TOU isn't always the default opt-in.

2. Federal IRA tax credits + utility rebates

The 2022 Inflation Reduction Act created the largest residential efficiency incentive program in U.S. history. The headline programs:

ProgramWhat it coversMax benefit
Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (25C)Insulation, windows, doors, audits, electrical panel upgrades$1,200/year (no lifetime cap)
Heat pump tax credit (25C)Air-source heat pumps, heat pump water heaters$2,000/year
Residential Clean Energy Credit (25D)Solar panels, solar water heaters, batteries30% of cost, no cap (through 2032)
HEEHRA / High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate ActHeat pumps, induction ranges, electrical upgrades — INCOME-LIMITED$14,000 lifetime per household
HOMES Rebate ProgramWhole-home efficiency retrofits$8,000 (low/moderate income)
State energy office rebatesVaries — check your state energy officeVaries
Utility-specific rebatesSmart thermostats, EV chargers, weatherization$50–$5,000 typical

HEEHRA and HOMES are administered by state energy offices and rolled out at different paces in different states. CA, NY, MA, CO, and a few others were first to launch. Most others are launching through 2026.

The stacking math is meaningful: a single-family home installing a heat pump can often combine the federal 25C credit ($2,000), HEEHRA rebate (up to $8,000 depending on income), state energy office rebate ($1,000–$3,000), and utility-specific rebate ($500–$2,000). Total stack: often $10,000–$15,000 on a system that costs $12,000–$20,000.

3. Home energy audits

Most regulated utilities offer free or rebated home energy audits. A trained auditor visits your home, runs blower-door tests, checks insulation, identifies air leaks, and produces a report. Cost: typically $0–$200 with utility rebates covering most or all.

The audit identifies the biggest savings opportunities. The average home has $200–$500/year of low-effort wins:

  • Air leaks around windows, doors, electrical outlets, attic hatches — typically 10–20% of heating/cooling costs lost to infiltration
  • Inadequate attic insulation — most pre-2000 homes have R-19 to R-30 when modern code calls for R-49 to R-60
  • Inefficient water heating — old tanks lose 5–10% of heat to standby losses
  • Phantom loads from electronics — modern home has 50–100 watts of always-on draw
  • Dryer vent leaks and lint buildup
  • HVAC duct leaks — typical home loses 20–30% of conditioned air through unsealed ducts

For income-qualified households, the federal Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) covers most of these fixes for free — often $5,000–$10,000 of work per home. Apply through your state energy office or LIHEAP intake.

4. Solar — rooftop and community

Two paths in regulated states:

Rooftop solar

Best for owner-occupied homes with good roof orientation and 5+ years of expected residency. The math depends heavily on:

  • Local sun-hours (CA/AZ/NV/CO/NM are best; PNW and New England are weakest)
  • Local utility's net metering rules (1:1 retail credit is best; some states have moved to "net billing" with smaller credits)
  • Federal 25D tax credit (30% of installation cost through 2032)
  • State and utility rebates (varies)

2026 typical economics: 6–8 kW residential system costs $18,000–$28,000 before incentives, $13,000–$20,000 after the federal 25D credit. Payback period is typically 6–12 years in good-sun states, 12–20 years in poor-sun states.

Community solar

For renters, condo dwellers, and homes without good roof orientation. You subscribe to a share of an off-site solar project; bill credits arrive monthly. No upfront cost. Save 5–10% on average.

Community solar is most developed in IL (Illinois Solar for All — income-qualified), NY (Community Distributed Generation), MA, CO, MN, MD, NJ, CA, OR. Other states have pilots launching through 2026–2030.

Income-qualified community solar programs (Solar for All in IL, NY-Sun in NY, etc.) often save $400–$800/year for participating households. Stacks with LIHEAP and other assistance.

5. Assistance programs (often the biggest single savings)

For income-qualified households (typically at or below 200% of the federal poverty level — about $62,400 for a family of four), state and federal assistance programs often deliver larger annual savings than any of the above paths combined:

  • LIHEAP — federal energy assistance, $400–$1,500/year typical. Available in every state. LIHEAP guide →
  • Utility-specific discount tiers — CARE/FERA (CA), PIPP Plus (OH), Reduced Rate (Eversource, National Grid), EAP (NH), USF (NJ), CAP (PA), EUSP (MD), UDP (DC), A-60 (RI). 20–76% bill discount depending on program.
  • Crisis funds — for shutoff threats and emergencies.
  • Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP) — free home weatherization, federally funded, administered by state energy offices.

Regulated states list

You're in a regulated state if you live in any of the following:

West & Mountain: CA, OR, WA, NV, AZ, UT, CO, ID, MT, WY, NM

Plains: ND, SD, NE, KS, OK

Midwest: MN, IA, WI, MO, IN (mostly), KY

South: FL, GA, SC, NC, TN, AL, MS, LA, AR

Other: WV, AK, HI, VT

VA and MI have limited deregulation but most residents can't actually shop. Texas has municipal islands (Austin, San Antonio) that are regulated within an otherwise deregulated state.

FAQ

Why can't I shop in my state?

State legislators decided not to deregulate. Each state PUC made the call independently. Some states (FL, GA, AL) have explicitly affirmed regulation; others (VA, MI) deregulated then re-regulated; the rest never moved to deregulate. The decision is political, not technical.

Will my state ever deregulate?

Probably not in the near term. Deregulation lost political momentum after the 2000–2001 California energy crisis and the 2021 Texas winter storm crisis. Most states that haven't deregulated by now aren't going to.

How do I know if my utility offers TOU?

Check your utility's website or call customer service. Almost every regulated utility offers some form of TOU plan, but not all are residential-friendly. Look for plans labeled "Time-of-Use," "Time-of-Day," "Smart Pricing," or similar.

What's the most-stacking-friendly heat pump rebate program?

HEEHRA (federal, income-qualified) plus state-level rebates plus utility rebates plus the 25C federal tax credit. Total stack often exceeds the cost of a basic heat pump for low-income households.

How long does WAP weatherization take?

Application to completion: typically 4–9 months. The work itself takes 1–3 days. WAP is income-qualified (typically 200% FPL).

Can renters do anything?

Yes. Renters can: enroll in TOU if their account is in their name, apply for LIHEAP and crisis assistance, enroll in community solar, get window AC efficiency rebates. The big-ticket items (heat pumps, weatherization, rooftop solar) require homeowner participation.
